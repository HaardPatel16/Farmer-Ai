1. SCHEME NAME & FULL OFFICIAL NAME
   Farmer Producer Organization (FPO) Scheme — Central Sector Scheme for Formation and Promotion of 10,000 FPOs, implemented by Small Farmers' Agri-Business Consortium (SFAC) and NABARD, with support from the Ministry of Agriculture & Farmers Welfare.

2. WHAT IS IT
   The FPO scheme aims to organize small and marginal farmers into collectives (FPOs or Farmer Producer Companies) to improve their bargaining power, reduce input costs, and increase market access. Small farmers in Gujarat typically have 1-2 hectares of land, making it difficult to negotiate bulk discounts on seeds, fertilizers, and machinery, or to get good prices for their produce. FPOs allow these farmers to pool their resources, buy inputs in bulk at lower prices, sell produce collectively for better prices, and access government schemes and bank loans that are difficult for individual small farmers to obtain. The central government provides Rs 18 lakh per FPO over 3 years as financial support for registration, management, capacity building, and infrastructure. Gujarat has seen rapid FPO growth, with over 5,000 FPOs registered in the state, making it one of the leading states in FPO formation.

3. WHO IS ELIGIBLE
   • Groups of farmers (minimum 300 members in plain areas, 100 in hilly/tribal areas)
   • Primarily small and marginal farmers (below 2 hectares) — at least 70% of members should be small/marginal
   • Women farmers are encouraged; there are special provisions for women-led FPOs
   • Farmers must be from the same district or nearby districts
   • Existing cooperatives can convert to FPOs
   • No income limit for individual members
   • Exclusions: Groups with fewer than the minimum members; groups dominated by large farmers (above 10 hectares)

4. BENEFITS PROVIDED
   • Central government grant: Rs 18 lakh per FPO over 3 years (Rs 6 lakh/year)
   • Grant covers: Registration fees, legal compliance, office setup, staff salaries, training, and capacity building
   • FPOs can access bank loans under the Agriculture Infrastructure Fund (AIF) at 3% interest subvention
   • FPOs are eligible for custom hiring centre (CHC) subsidies, warehouse subsidies, and processing unit subsidies
   • Collective input purchase: 10-20% discount on seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides through bulk buying
   • Collective output sale: 5-15% higher prices due to bulk marketing and better negotiation
   • Access to government procurement contracts (e.g., FCI, NAFED, state agencies)
   • Technical training and exposure visits organized by SFAC and NABARD
   • FPOs can receive CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility) funds from companies
   • Handholding support for 3-5 years from Cluster-Based Business Organizations (CBBOs)

5. HOW TO FORM AN FPO (STEP-BY-STEP)
   Step 1: Identify a group of 300+ farmers (or 100+ in hilly areas) willing to join. Hold a meeting to discuss the FPO concept and benefits.
   Step 2: Elect a promoter group (5-7 members) to lead the formation process.
   Step 3: Contact NABARD or SFAC for guidance. NABARD has designated "FPO Promoting Institutions" (FPIs) in Gujarat.
   Step 4: Prepare a business plan and membership list with Aadhaar numbers, land details, and contact information.
   Step 5: Register the FPO under the Companies Act, 2013, as a Farmer Producer Company (FPC) or as a cooperative society under the Cooperative Societies Act.
   Step 6: Open a bank account in the FPO's name.
   Step 7: Apply for the central government grant through the SFAC portal (sfacindia.com) or NABARD.
   Step 8: A designated CBBO (Cluster-Based Business Organization) is assigned to provide handholding support.
   Step 9: Start collective activities: input purchase, produce aggregation, and marketing.
   Step 10: Annual audits and compliance filings are mandatory to maintain grant eligibility.
   Documents: Membership list with Aadhaar, land records, business plan, registration application, and promoter details.

7. GUJARAT-SPECIFIC DETAILS
   Gujarat has over 5,000 registered FPOs, one of the highest in India. Major FPO clusters in Gujarat: cotton (Rajkot, Bhavnagar), groundnut (Junagadh, Amreli), cumin/fennel (Mehsana, Patan), and dairy (Anand, Banaskantha). Gujarat's Amul cooperative model has inspired many FPOs in the dairy sector. The Gujarat government provides additional state-level support for FPOs through the Gujarat Agro Industries Corporation (GAIC). Gujarat FPOs have successfully exported cumin, fennel, and groundnut to Middle East and European markets. The state has FPO-specific training programs at Anand Agricultural University and NAU (Navsari). Gujarat has integrated FPOs with the iKhedut portal, allowing FPOs to apply for collective subsidies. Many Gujarat FPOs have set up custom hiring centres, warehouses, and processing units using AIF loans.

8. COMMON PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS
   • Not enough members: Start with a core group and gradually expand through village meetings and sarpanch involvement.
   • Members not contributing: Create a small membership fee (Rs 100-500) to ensure commitment; hold regular meetings.
   • Business plan rejected: SFAC and NABARD provide templates; hire a consultant if needed (cost: Rs 5,000-15,000).
   • Grant delayed: Grants are released in installments based on milestones; ensure timely submission of utilization certificates and audit reports.
   • FPO members dropping out: Regular communication, transparent accounting, and visible benefits (better prices, cheaper inputs) retain members.
   • Difficulty getting bank loan: Approach NABARD first; they provide credit guarantees for FPOs under AIF.
   • Conflict among members: Elect a strong, transparent board; adopt clear bylaws; seek CBBO mediation.
