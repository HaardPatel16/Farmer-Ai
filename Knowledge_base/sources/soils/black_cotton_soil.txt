BLACK COTTON SOIL (VERTISOL) — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Black cotton soils, known internationally as Vertisols (WRB classification) or Vertisols
(USDA Soil Taxonomy), are the most widespread and agriculturally significant soils in
Gujarat. They derive their name from their deep black colour and historical importance
for cotton cultivation. These soils are formed from the weathering of basaltic rocks (Deccan
Traps) and are characterised by high clay content, particularly the smectite (montmorillonite)
clay mineral group. Vertisols are known for their remarkable "shrink-swell" behaviour —
expanding when wet and contracting dramatically when dry, forming deep, wide cracks. In
Gujarat, they are classified into deep, medium, and shallow black soils based on depth
and clay content.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Clay to clay loam. Clay content ranges from 40% to 60%, with some deep black
soils exceeding 60% clay. The fine texture makes these soils heavy and difficult to work
when wet or dry.

Structure: Angular blocky to prismatic structure when moist. The self-mulching property
(falling of surface soil into cracks) creates a natural granular surface over time. However,
when wet, the soil becomes sticky and plastic; when dry, it hardens into massive clods.

Colour: Deep black to dark greyish-brown. The colour is due to the presence of iron-manganese
oxides, titanium, and high organic matter relative to other Gujarat soils. Darker colour
indicates higher clay and organic matter content.

Depth: Variable — shallow black soils (30–50 cm), medium black soils (50–100 cm), and deep
black soils (100–200+ cm). Depth determines the agricultural potential and crop suitability.

Drainage: Impeded drainage is a major issue. The high clay content creates very slow
percolation rates (2–10 mm/hour). During monsoon, waterlogging is common in depressions
and flat areas. During dry periods, the soil becomes hard and impermeable.

Water-Holding Capacity: Very high (180–250 mm/metre). This is both an advantage and a
disadvantage — it sustains crops through short dry spells but causes waterlogging during
heavy rains.

Cracking: The defining feature. Deep black soils form cracks 2–10 cm wide and 50–100 cm
deep during the dry season. These cracks provide natural aeration, root penetration, and
self-mulching. However, they also damage farm equipment and infrastructure.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 7.5 to 8.8, typically slightly alkaline to moderately alkaline. High pH is due
to the presence of calcium carbonate (kankar) and exchangeable sodium in some areas.

Organic Matter: Low to moderate (0.4–0.8%). Despite the dark colour, the high temperature
and mineralisation rate in Gujarat prevent the accumulation of high organic matter. The
dark colour is primarily due to mineral constituents (clay-humus complexes, iron oxides)
rather than high organic carbon.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Low (120–200 kg/ha available N). The high clay content immobilises nitrogen,
    and rapid mineralisation depletes organic N reserves.
  • Phosphorus: Low to moderate (10–25 kg/ha available P). High calcium content fixes
    phosphorus into insoluble calcium phosphates, reducing availability.
  • Potassium: High to very high (250–400 kg/ha available K). The parent basaltic rocks
    are rich in potassium-bearing minerals (feldspars, micas). Most black cotton soils do
    NOT need potassium fertilisation unless intensively cropped.

Micronutrients: Zinc deficiency is the most widespread micronutrient problem in Gujarat's
black cotton soils. Over 60% of fields show Zn deficiency. Iron deficiency (lime-induced
chlorosis) occurs in high pH areas. Boron deficiency may occur in cotton and groundnut.
Manganese toxicity can occur in waterlogged conditions.

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): High (30–50 cmol/kg), reflecting the high clay content
and surface area of smectite minerals. This gives the soil excellent nutrient retention
capacity.

Calcium Carbonate (CaCO3): Present as kankar nodules, especially in deeper layers. Ranges
from 5% to 20% by weight. High CaCO3 exacerbates micronutrient deficiencies.

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Black cotton soils are found extensively across the state:
  • Saurashtra: Rajkot, Amreli, Bhavnagar, Junagadh, Gir Somnath, Jamnagar (medium and
    shallow black soils dominate).
  • Central Gujarat: Ahmedabad, Vadodara, Kheda, Anand, Panchmahal (medium black soils;
    some deep black soils in Vadodara).
  • South Gujarat: Bharuch, Narmada, Surat (deep black soils — the most productive in the
    state).
  • North Gujarat: Mehsana, Patan, Sabarkantha (patches of medium black soil).
  • Kutch: Bhuj, Anjar, Mundra (limited patches of medium black soil — the best
    agricultural lands in Kutch).

Extent: Approximately 40–45% of Gujarat's total geographical area. Black cotton soils are
the defining soil type of Gujarat agriculture.

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Best Crops (Highly Suitable):
  • Cotton — THE namesake crop. Black cotton soils are ideal for cotton due to high
    water-holding capacity, deep rooting, and good nutrient retention. Yields: 2.5–4.5
    quintals/ha (lint).
  • Groundnut — The most important oilseed crop on black cotton soils. The soil sustains
    groundnut through the critical pod-filling stage. Yields: 15–25 quintals/ha (pods).
  • Wheat — Excellent rabi crop on medium and deep black soils with irrigation. Yields:
    4.0–6.0 tonnes/ha.
  • Sorghum (Jowar) — Both kharif and rabi sorghum perform well. Yields: 25–35 quintals/ha.
  • Soybean — Expanding crop on deep black soils in Bharuch and Narmada.
  • Sugarcane — Excellent on deep black soils with irrigation and drainage management.
    Yields: 80–100 tonnes/ha.
  • Chickpea (Gram) — Good rabi pulse on medium black soils with residual moisture.

Moderately Suitable:
  • Bajra (Pearl Millet) — grows on lighter patches and raised bunds to avoid waterlogging.
  • Pigeon Pea (Tur) — long-duration varieties on medium black soils.
  • Castor — on deeper soils with good drainage.

Unsuitable Crops:
  • Root crops (potato, carrot, garlic) — poor drainage and clay texture cause misshapen
    roots and rotting.
  • Cumin and fennel — require well-drained sandy soils; heavy black soils cause waterlogging
    and fungal diseases.
  • Pineapple and cashew — require well-drained, acidic soils.

Yield Potential: High to very high when properly managed. Deep black soils in Bharuch and
Narmada are among the highest-yielding cotton and sugarcane areas in India. However, shallow
black soils in Saurashtra are drought-prone and yield potential is moderate.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 100–150 kg N/ha for cereals; 150–200 kg N/ha for cotton; 200–280 kg N/ha for
    sugarcane. Split applications are essential due to high leaching potential during cracking.
  • Phosphorus: 50–80 kg P2O5/ha. Use water-soluble forms (DAP, SSP) rather than rock phosphate.
    Apply at sowing, not broadcast on the surface.
  • Potassium: 0–30 kg K2O/ha for most crops. Test soil before applying K — most black cotton
    soils have high native K. Excess K can cause magnesium deficiency.
  • Zinc: 25–30 kg zinc sulphate/ha at sowing is critical. Zinc deficiency is the number one
    nutritional disorder in these soils. Foliar spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 + 0.25% lime during vegetative
    stage for quick correction.
  • Iron: Foliar spray of 0.5% ferrous sulphate for lime-induced chlorosis in high-pH areas.
  • Sulphur: 20–30 kg S/ha for oilseeds (groundnut, soybean) and cotton.

Irrigation: Careful water management is critical:
  • Drip irrigation is highly recommended for cotton, groundnut, and vegetables to prevent
    waterlogging and save water.
  • Sprinkler irrigation for wheat and fodder.
  • Avoid flood irrigation on black cotton soils — it causes waterlogging, root rot, and
    nutrient leaching through cracks.
  • Irrigation scheduling should account for the high water-holding capacity — less frequent,
    heavier irrigations are better than frequent light irrigations (which exacerbate surface
    waterlogging).

Tillage: Timing is everything:
  • NEVER till when the soil is very wet (sticky plastic state) — this destroys soil structure
    and creates massive clods that persist for the entire season.
  • Till at the optimum moisture content — when the soil is moist but not sticky, typically
    2–3 days after heavy rain or irrigation.
  • Deep tillage (chisel ploughing) to 40–50 cm depth every 3–4 years breaks subsoil hardpans
    and improves drainage.
  • Use wide-bottom ploughs rather than disc ploughs to avoid smearing and compaction.
  • Laser land levelling improves water distribution and prevents ponding in low spots.

Special Practices: Broad bed and furrow (BBF) system for cotton and groundnut — raised beds
(80–100 cm wide, 20–25 cm high) with furrows in between improve drainage and aeration. This
is the most important soil management innovation for black cotton soils in Gujarat.

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Waterlogging: The most common and damaging problem during monsoon. Remedy: BBF system;
    open drainage channels; subsurface drainage with mole drains or perforated pipes; avoid
    continuous flooding; grow rice in severely waterlogged areas.
  • Salinity/Alkalinity: In poorly drained areas, evaporation from the soil surface brings
    salts to the top, creating saline/alkaline patches. Remedy: Improve drainage; apply
    gypsum 2–5 tonnes/ha; leach with excess irrigation; grow salt-tolerant crops (rice,
    barley, cotton) during reclamation.
  • Soil Compaction: The heavy clay soil compacts under traffic, especially when wet.
    Remedy: Restrict field traffic to dry periods; use minimum/zero tillage; maintain soil
    organic matter; deep chiselling every 3–4 years.
  • Cracking and Structural Damage: Deep cracks damage foundations, pipelines, and tree roots.
    Remedy: Maintain soil moisture during dry periods; mulching reduces crack severity;
    design infrastructure to accommodate soil movement.
  • Nutrient Deficiencies: Zinc deficiency is universal. Iron chlorosis in high-pH areas.
    Remedy: Regular soil testing; prophylactic zinc application; foliar micronutrient sprays.
  • Erosion: Sheet erosion during intense monsoon rains on sloping black soil fields.
    Remedy: Contour bunding; graded bunding; vegetative barriers; mulching.
  • Hardpan Formation: Repeated tillage at the same depth creates a plough pan at 15–20 cm.
    Remedy: Deep chiselling or subsoiling to 40–50 cm; use of deep-rooted crops (alfalfa,
    sorghum) as biological drills.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for black cotton soils:

  • pH: Optimal 7.0–7.5. If pH > 8.5, check for sodicity (ESP) and apply gypsum if ESP > 15.
  • ECe: If > 4 dS/m, salinity is a concern. Install drainage and leach before sowing
    sensitive crops.
  • Organic Matter: Target > 0.8%. If below 0.5%, apply FYM 15–20 tonnes/ha and include
    legumes in rotation.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 150 kg/ha, apply higher N doses or grow green manure before
    the main crop.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 15 kg/ha, apply 60–80 kg P2O5/ha. Use water-soluble P
    fertilisers (DAP, SSP).
  • Potassium: If available K > 250 kg/ha, skip K fertiliser for 1–2 seasons and retest.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.6 ppm. Apply 25–30 kg zinc sulphate/ha prophylactically every
    year. Foliar Zn spray for standing crops showing deficiency.
  • Calcium Carbonate: If CaCO3 > 15%, expect iron and zinc deficiencies. Increase
    micronutrient applications and use chelated forms.

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 15–20 tonnes/ha annually. This is essential for improving
    structure, reducing cracking intensity, and enhancing water infiltration.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha, sunhemp, or cluster bean before cotton or groundnut.
    Green manure adds 80–120 kg N/ha and improves soil structure.
  • Crop Residue Management: Do NOT burn cotton or groundnut residues. Incorporate them
    into the soil to add organic matter and reduce erosion. Cotton stalks can be composted
    or used for mushroom cultivation before returning to the field.
  • Compost and Vermicompost: 3–5 tonnes/ha for intensive cropping areas. Vermicompost is
    particularly effective in improving the granular structure of black cotton soils.
  • Biofertilisers: Azotobacter, Azospirillum, PSB, and VAM (Vesicular Arbuscular Mycorrhiza)
    improve nutrient availability in the high clay matrix. VAM is especially beneficial for
    phosphorus uptake in high P-fixing black soils.
  • Organic Farming: Transition to organic farming is feasible for cotton and wheat in areas
    with good water availability. However, yield penalties during the 3-year transition
    period can be 15–25%. Support from organic markets is needed for economic viability.
  • Alley Cropping: Plant Leucaena, Gliricidia, or Subabul on field boundaries to provide
    green leaf manure, firewood, and erosion control.
