DEEP BLACK SOIL (DEEP VERTISOL) — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Deep black soils, known as deep Vertisols, are the most agriculturally productive and
physically distinctive soils in Gujarat. They are classified as Vertisols (USDA Soil Taxonomy)
due to their high clay content, deep cracks, and strong shrink-swell behaviour. These soils
are formed from the weathering of basaltic rocks (Deccan Traps) and are distinguished from
medium and shallow black soils by their exceptional depth (1–2 metres or more), very high clay
content (50–60%), and pronounced vertic properties. In Gujarat, they are the soils of the
"black gold" belt — the most intensively cultivated and highest-yielding areas in the state.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Clay to heavy clay. Clay content typically ranges from 50% to 60%, with the
smectite (montmorillonite) clay mineral group dominating. This gives the soil its extreme
shrink-swell capacity.

Structure: Prismatic to angular blocky structure in the subsoil; self-mulching granular
structure at the surface due to natural sloughing into cracks. When wet, the soil is
extremely sticky and plastic; when dry, it hardens into massive, concrete-like clods that
are difficult to work.

Colour: Deep black to very dark grey. The colour is due to the presence of iron-manganese
oxides, titanium, and finely dispersed organic matter-clay complexes. The darkness is more
intense than in medium or shallow black soils due to greater clay and organic matter content.

Depth: Very deep, 1 to 2+ metres. Unlike shallow black soils that restrict root growth,
deep black soils allow unrestricted root penetration. This depth is their defining advantage,
providing a large reservoir of water and nutrients.

Drainage: Poor to moderate. The high clay content creates very slow percolation (2–8 mm/hour).
During monsoon, waterlogging is common in flat areas and depressions. However, the deep cracks
provide some internal drainage during the initial stages of the wet season.

Water-Holding Capacity: Very high (200–280 mm/metre). This is among the highest of any soil
type in Gujarat. The soil can sustain crops through 3–4 week dry spells without irrigation,
making it valuable in regions with erratic rainfall.

Cracking: The most dramatic physical feature. During the dry season, deep black soils form
wide cracks (5–15 cm wide, 60–100+ cm deep). These cracks:
  • Provide natural aeration to depth
  • Allow roots to penetrate deeply
  • Cause self-mulching (surface soil falls into cracks, creating natural tillage)
  • Damage farm equipment, buildings, and roads
  • Expose subsoil, causing vertical mixing over time

Bulk Density: 1.1–1.4 g/cm³ when moist; increases when dry due to shrinkage and compaction.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 7.5 to 8.5, slightly to moderately alkaline. The high pH is due to calcium
 carbonate (kankar) and the presence of exchangeable sodium in some areas.

Organic Matter: Low to moderate (0.5–1.0%). Despite the dark colour, the high temperature and
mineralisation rate in Gujarat limit organic matter accumulation. The dark colour is primarily
due to mineral-humus complexes and iron oxides rather than high organic carbon content.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Low (150–220 kg/ha available N). The high clay content immobilises nitrogen,
    and rapid mineralisation in Gujarat's hot climate depletes organic N reserves.
  • Phosphorus: Low to moderate (12–25 kg/ha available P). High calcium content fixes
    phosphorus into insoluble calcium phosphates, reducing availability to plants.
  • Potassium: High to very high (280–450 kg/ha available K). The parent basaltic rocks are
    rich in potassium-bearing minerals. Deep black soils typically do NOT need potassium
    fertilisation unless intensively cropped for many decades.

Micronutrients: Zinc deficiency is the most widespread problem, affecting over 60% of fields.
Iron deficiency (lime-induced chlorosis) occurs in high-pH areas. Boron deficiency is common
in cotton and groundnut. Manganese toxicity can occur in waterlogged conditions. Copper and
molybdenum are generally adequate.

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Very high (35–55 cmol/kg), reflecting the enormous surface area
of smectite clay minerals. This gives the soil exceptional nutrient retention capacity, but
also means that nutrients can be tightly bound and less available.

Calcium Carbonate (CaCO3): Present as kankar nodules, especially in deeper layers. Ranges from
5% to 15% by weight. High CaCO3 exacerbates micronutrient deficiencies, particularly zinc and
iron.

Base Saturation: Very high (>90%), dominated by calcium and magnesium. This is natural for
vertisols and generally favourable for agriculture, but the high pH can limit micronutrient
availability.

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Deep black soils are concentrated in the most fertile regions of Gujarat:
  • South Gujarat: Bharuch (entire district), Narmada (entire district), parts of Surat
    (along Tapi river), and parts of Vadodara (eastern talukas). This is the "black gold"
belt of Gujarat.
  • Central Gujarat: Parts of Vadodara district (Karjan, Sinor, Dabhoi talukas), and limited
    areas in Anand and Kheda.
  • Saurashtra: Very limited — only in small pockets of Bhavnagar and Amreli where basaltic
    parent material is deep.

Extent: Approximately 8–10% of Gujarat's total geographical area. Despite their smaller
extent compared to medium black soils, deep black soils contribute disproportionately to
Gujarat's agricultural output because of their high productivity.

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Best Crops (Highly Suitable):
  • Cotton — THE premier crop for deep black soils. Gujarat produces some of India's finest
    long-staple cotton from these soils. The deep rooting zone, high water-holding capacity,
    and excellent nutrient retention support sustained cotton growth. Yields: 3.0–5.0 quintals/ha
    (lint) under good management.
  • Sugarcane — Outstanding performance due to deep rooting and high moisture availability.
    The Tapi belt (Bharuch, Surat) is Gujarat's largest sugarcane area. Yields: 100–130 tonnes/ha.
  • Soybean — Expanding rapidly on deep black soils in Bharuch and Narmada. The crop benefits
    from nitrogen fixation and the soil's moisture reserve. Yields: 20–30 quintals/ha.
  • Wheat — Excellent rabi crop with irrigation. Yields: 5.0–7.0 tonnes/ha, among the highest
    in Gujarat.
  • Sorghum (Jowar) — Both kharif and rabi sorghum perform very well. Yields: 30–40 quintals/ha.
  • Banana — Tissue-culture banana plantations thrive on deep black soils with drip irrigation
    in Bharuch and Narmada. Yields: 50–70 tonnes/ha.
  • Chickpea (Gram) — Good rabi pulse on residual moisture. Yields: 18–25 quintals/ha.
  • Pigeon Pea (Tur) — Long-duration varieties on deep soils. Yields: 15–20 quintals/ha.

Moderately Suitable:
  • Paddy (Rice) — Suitable but requires careful water management to prevent waterlogging.
    Direct-seeded rice is preferred over transplanting to avoid anaerobic conditions.
  • Groundnut — Good yields but soil crusting after rain can impede pod development. Yields:
    18–25 quintals/ha.
  • Maize — Kharif and rabi maize are suitable but require good drainage.

Unsuitable Crops:
  • Root crops (potato, carrot, garlic, onion) — Poor drainage and heavy clay texture cause
    misshapen roots, rotting, and poor quality. The soil sticks to tubers and is difficult to
    wash off.
  • Cumin and fennel — Require well-drained sandy soils; heavy black soils cause waterlogging
    and fungal diseases (wilt, root rot).
  • Pineapple, cashew, and rubber — Require well-drained, acidic soils; deep black soils are
    too alkaline and poorly drained for these crops.

Yield Potential: Very high to exceptional. Deep black soils in Bharuch and Narmada are among
the highest-yielding cotton and sugarcane areas in India. With proper irrigation and nutrient
management, these soils can support sustained high productivity for decades.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 120–180 kg N/ha for cereals; 180–250 kg N/ha for cotton; 250–350 kg N/ha for
    sugarcane (split into 5–6 applications). Split applications are essential because the
    high clay content can cause temporary immobilisation, and the cracks can cause deep
    leaching during early monsoon.
  • Phosphorus: 60–90 kg P2O5/ha. Use water-soluble forms (DAP, SSP) rather than rock phosphate.
    Band placement near the seed row is more effective than broadcasting due to high P fixation
    by calcium. Apply at sowing time.
  • Potassium: 0–40 kg K2O/ha for most crops. Test soil before applying K — most deep black
    soils have high native K (>300 kg/ha). Excess K can cause magnesium deficiency (interveinal
    chlorosis in older leaves).
  • Zinc: 30 kg zinc sulphate/ha at sowing is critical for all crops. Zinc deficiency is the
    number one nutritional disorder in deep black soils. Foliar spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 + 0.25%
    lime during vegetative stage (2–3 sprays at 15-day intervals) for quick correction.
  • Iron: Foliar spray of 0.5% ferrous sulphate for lime-induced chlorosis in high-pH areas.
    Use iron chelates (Fe-EDTA) for severe cases or for fruit crops.
  • Boron: 10–15 kg borax/ha for cotton, groundnut, and sugarcane. Boron deficiency causes
    hollow stem in sugarcane and poor boll development in cotton.
  • Sulphur: 30–40 kg S/ha for oilseeds (groundnut, soybean, castor) and cotton. Sulphur
    deficiency is increasingly common in intensively cropped deep black soils.
  • Magnesium: If interveinal chlorosis appears in older leaves despite adequate K, apply
    20–30 kg MgO/ha as magnesium sulphate.

Irrigation: Water management is critical — too much causes waterlogging; too little causes
moisture stress in the surface layers:
  • Drip irrigation is the recommended method for cotton, sugarcane, banana, and vegetables.
    It prevents waterlogging, saves 40–60% water, and allows precise fertigation (fertiliser
    + irrigation together).
  • Sprinkler irrigation for wheat and fodder crops.
  • Flood irrigation should be AVOIDED on deep black soils — it causes surface waterlogging,
    root rot, and nutrient leaching through cracks. If flood irrigation must be used (e.g.,
    for paddy), ensure levelled fields and controlled water depth.
  • Irrigation scheduling: Due to the very high water-holding capacity, irrigate less frequently
    but with adequate depth (60–80 mm per irrigation for cotton; 100–120 mm for sugarcane).
    Light, frequent irrigations waste water and cause surface saturation.

Tillage: Timing and method are crucial:
  • NEVER till when the soil is very wet (sticky plastic state) — this destroys the natural
    structure and creates massive clods that persist for the entire season. These clods
    reduce seed-soil contact and emergence.
  • Till at the optimum moisture content — when the soil is moist but not sticky, typically
    2–3 days after heavy rain or irrigation. The soil should crumble when squeezed, not
    form a ribbon.
  • Deep tillage (chisel ploughing) to 40–50 cm depth every 3–4 years breaks subsoil hardpans
    and improves internal drainage. This is especially important for sugarcane and cotton.
  • Use wide-bottom ploughs or blade harrows rather than disc ploughs to avoid smearing and
    compaction of the sticky clay.
  • Laser land levelling is highly recommended for precision irrigation and to prevent ponding
    in low spots. Even a 5–10 cm elevation difference can cause waterlogging in deep black soils.

Special Practices:
  • Broad Bed and Furrow (BBF) System: THE most important soil management innovation for deep
    black soils. Raised beds (80–100 cm wide, 20–25 cm high) with furrows in between improve
    drainage, aeration, and root development. BBF is essential for cotton, groundnut, and
    soybean on deep black soils.
  • Ridge and Furrow: For sugarcane, plant on ridges to prevent waterlogging and facilitate
    irrigation.
  • Mulching: Apply crop residue mulch (sugarcane trash, cotton stalks) to reduce evaporation,
    moderate soil temperature, and prevent surface cracking.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha or sunhemp before sugarcane or cotton to add organic
    matter and nitrogen.

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Waterlogging: The most common and economically damaging problem during monsoon. Deep black
    soils absorb water slowly, and flat areas become waterlogged. Remedy: BBF system; open
    drainage channels; subsurface drainage with mole drains or perforated pipes; laser land
    levelling; grow rice in severely waterlogged areas; avoid flood irrigation.
  • Salinity/Alkalinity: In poorly drained areas, capillary rise brings salts to the surface,
    creating saline patches. Remedy: Improve drainage; apply gypsum 2–5 tonnes/ha if pH > 8.5;
    leach with excess irrigation; grow salt-tolerant crops during reclamation.
  • Soil Compaction: The heavy clay soil compacts easily under traffic, especially when wet.
    Compaction reduces root penetration and water infiltration. Remedy: Restrict field traffic
    to dry periods; use minimum/zero tillage for wheat after paddy; maintain soil organic matter;
    deep chiselling every 3–4 years; use controlled traffic farming (CTF) where possible.
  • Cracking and Structural Damage: Deep cracks (10–15 cm wide, 60–100 cm deep) damage building
    foundations, roads, pipelines, and tree roots. Remedy: Maintain soil moisture during dry
    periods (irrigation if possible); mulching reduces crack severity; design infrastructure
    to accommodate soil movement (flexible joints in pipelines, reinforced foundations).
  • Nutrient Deficiencies: Zinc deficiency is universal. Iron chlorosis in high-pH areas.
    Boron deficiency in cotton and sugarcane. Remedy: Regular soil testing every 2–3 years;
    prophylactic zinc application; foliar micronutrient sprays; balanced fertilisation based
    on soil test.
  • Erosion: Sheet erosion during intense monsoon rains on sloping fields. Remedy: Contour
    bunding; graded bunding; vegetative barriers (vetiver grass); mulching; maintain ground
    cover.
  • Hardpan Formation: Repeated shallow tillage at the same depth (15–20 cm) creates a plough
    pan that restricts root penetration and water movement. Remedy: Deep chiselling or subsoiling
    to 40–50 cm; use of deep-rooted crops (alfalfa, sorghum, pigeon pea) as biological drills.
  • Low Organic Matter: Despite the dark colour, organic matter is low (0.5–1.0%). Remedy:
    Regular FYM application; green manuring; crop residue incorporation; composting; reduced
    tillage to preserve organic matter.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for deep black soils:

  • pH: Optimal range 7.0–7.5. If pH > 8.5, check for sodicity (ESP) and apply gypsum if
    ESP > 15. High pH indicates potential micronutrient problems.
  • ECe (Electrical Conductivity): If > 4 dS/m, salinity is a concern. Install drainage and
    leach before sowing sensitive crops.
  • Organic Matter: Target > 1.0%. If below 0.6%, apply FYM 20 tonnes/ha and include legumes
    in rotation. Low organic matter is the primary cause of poor structure and low microbial
    activity.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 180 kg/ha, apply higher N doses (180–220 kg N/ha) or grow green
    manure before the main crop. Include legumes (soybean, pigeon pea, green gram) in rotation.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 15 kg/ha, apply 80–100 kg P2O5/ha. Use water-soluble P
    fertilisers (DAP, SSP). Band placement is more effective than broadcasting.
  • Potassium: If available K > 300 kg/ha, skip K fertiliser for 2–3 seasons and retest. If
    K < 200 kg/ha, apply 40–60 kg K2O/ha. Note: After decades of intensive cropping, some
    deep black soils in Bharuch are showing K depletion.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.6 ppm (DTPA-extractable). Apply 25–30 kg ZnSO4/ha prophylactically
    every year. Foliar Zn spray for standing crops showing deficiency (stunted growth, rosette
    leaves, interveinal chlorosis).
  • Iron: DTPA-Fe < 4.5 ppm indicates deficiency. Apply FeSO4 or use foliar Fe-EDTA sprays.
    Interveinal chlorosis in young leaves is the diagnostic symptom.
  • Boron: Hot-water soluble B < 0.5 ppm indicates deficiency. Apply 10–15 kg borax/ha for
    cotton and sugarcane.
  • Sulphur: If available S < 10 ppm, apply 30–40 kg S/ha as gypsum or ammonium sulphate.
  • Calcium Carbonate: If CaCO3 > 15%, expect micronutrient problems. Increase foliar
    micronutrient applications and use chelated forms.

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 20–25 tonnes/ha annually. This is the single most important
    amendment for deep black soils. Organic matter improves structure, reduces cracking
    intensity, enhances water infiltration, and increases nutrient availability. FYM also
    provides a slow-release source of nutrients that complements chemical fertilisers.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha (Sesbania aculeata, 60–90 days), sunhemp (Crotalaria
    juncea, 45–60 days), or cluster bean (Guar) before cotton, sugarcane, or soybean. Green
    manure adds 100–150 kg N/ha equivalent, improves soil structure, and increases microbial
    activity. In the Tapi belt, dhaincha is the preferred green manure before sugarcane.
  • Crop Residue Management: Do NOT burn cotton, sugarcane, or wheat residues. Incorporate them
    into the soil or compost them before application. Cotton stalks can be used for mushroom
    cultivation (oyster mushrooms) before being returned to the field as spent compost. Sugarcane
    trash can be used as surface mulch or composted. Wheat straw incorporation improves soil
    tilth and organic matter.
  • Compost and Vermicompost: 5–8 tonnes/ha for intensive cropping. Vermicompost is particularly
    effective in improving the granular structure of black soils and providing available
    micronutrients in chelated form. Vermiwash (liquid extract from vermicompost) can be used
    as a foliar spray and soil drench.
  • Biofertilisers: Azotobacter, Azospirillum, PSB (Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria), and VAM
    (Vesicular Arbuscular Mycorrhiza) improve nutrient availability in the high clay matrix.
    VAM is especially beneficial for phosphorus and micronutrient uptake in high P-fixing
    deep black soils. Use rhizobium inoculants for soybean, pigeon pea, and chickpea.
  • Alley Cropping: Plant nitrogen-fixing trees (Leucaena leucocephala, Gliricidia sepium,
    Subabul — Leucaena leucocephala) on field boundaries to provide green leaf manure, firewood,
    and erosion control. Leaf litter from these trees adds 2–5 tonnes of organic matter per
    hectare per year (considering the boundary area). These trees are common in Bharuch and
    Narmada districts.
  • Cover Cropping: Use cowpea, moth bean, or horse gram during fallow periods to cover the
    soil, add biomass, fix nitrogen, and prevent moisture loss. In the Tapi belt, cowpea is
    grown as a cover crop during the summer months.
  • Reduced Tillage: Practice zero-till or minimum-till wheat after paddy or cotton to preserve
    soil organic matter, reduce fuel costs, and maintain soil structure. Zero-till wheat is
    expanding rapidly in Bharuch and Narmada on deep black soils.
  • Organic Farming: Transition to organic farming is feasible for cotton, sugarcane, and wheat
    in areas with good water availability. The challenge is managing micronutrient (especially
    zinc and boron) supply without synthetic chelates. Rock phosphate + PSB + VAM + organic
    matter can maintain P levels. Foliar sprays of compost tea and vermiwash can supply
    micronutrients. The 3-year transition period may see 15–25% yield penalties, but premium
    prices for organic cotton and sugar can offset this.
