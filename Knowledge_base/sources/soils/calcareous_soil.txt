CALCAREOUS SOIL — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Calcareous soils are soils with a high content of calcium carbonate (CaCO3), typically
exceeding 5% by weight in the surface layer and often much higher in the subsoil. In the
WRB classification, they are often classified as Calcisols or as Calcic subunits of other
soil types (Calcaric Cambisols, Calcaric Fluvisols). In Gujarat, calcareous soils are
not a distinct soil order but rather a chemical modification that occurs in arid and semi-arid
regions where evaporation exceeds precipitation, leading to the accumulation of calcium
carbonate (kankar) in the soil profile. These soils are often associated with desert and
semi-desert environments, parent materials rich in lime, and irrigation-induced secondary
salinisation.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Variable — sandy loam to clay loam. The texture depends on the parent material
and degree of weathering. Many calcareous soils in Gujarat are sandy loams due to aeolian
(wind-blown) deposition mixed with lime-rich alluvium.

Structure: Weak to moderate structure. High calcium carbonate can cement soil particles,
creating a weakly cemented, brittle structure that breaks down easily under tillage. Kankar
nodules (calcium carbonate concretions) of varying sizes are common, especially in the
subsoil.

Colour: Light grey to whitish-grey due to calcium carbonate coating on soil particles. In
areas with high organic matter, the colour may be greyish-brown. The presence of kankar
nodules gives a spotted or mottled appearance.

Depth: Variable — shallow to deep. Some areas have hard caliche layers (petrocalcic horizons)
at 30–60 cm depth, which restrict root penetration and water movement.

Drainage: Generally well-drained to excessively drained, especially in sandy calcareous
soils. However, the presence of kankar layers can create perched water tables and localised
waterlogging.

Water-Holding Capacity: Low to moderate (80–140 mm/metre). Sandy calcareous soils have low
water retention, while clay loam calcareous soils have moderate retention. The high calcium
carbonate content can reduce the water-holding capacity by cementing soil particles.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 8.2 to 8.8, typically moderately alkaline to strongly alkaline. The high pH is
directly caused by the excess calcium carbonate, which buffers the soil at high pH values.
Some areas may have pH > 9.0 if sodium carbonate is also present (saline-alkaline soils).

Organic Matter: Very low to low (0.2–0.5%). The arid climate, high pH, and rapid
mineralisation prevent organic matter accumulation. Low organic matter further reduces
nutrient availability and soil structure.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Very low (100–180 kg/ha available N). Low organic matter and high pH reduce
    N availability and increase ammonia volatilisation from applied urea.
  • Phosphorus: Low to moderate (10–20 kg/ha available P). At high pH, phosphorus reacts
    with calcium to form insoluble calcium phosphates (apatite), making it unavailable to
    plants. This is the primary phosphorus fixation mechanism in calcareous soils.
  • Potassium: Moderate to high (200–300 kg/ha available K). Potassium availability is less
    affected by calcium carbonate than N and P.

Micronutrients: The most critical constraint. High pH and calcium carbonate cause severe
deficiencies of:
  • Iron (Fe) — Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins) in young leaves, especially
    in citrus, groundnut, and sorghum. This is the most visible micronutrient deficiency.
  • Zinc (Zn) — Stunted growth, rosette leaves in citrus and mango, poor tillering in wheat.
  • Manganese (Mn) — Grey speck in oats and barley, interveinal chlorosis in beans.
  • Boron (B) — Heart rot in sugarbeet, cracked stems in celery, poor fruit set in citrus.
  • Copper (Cu) — Dieback in citrus, poor grain filling in wheat.

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Moderate (15–25 cmol/kg), depending on clay content. The
high calcium saturation (often > 70% of exchange sites) displaces other cations, reducing
micronutrient availability.

Calcium Carbonate (CaCO3): 5% to 30% by weight in the surface soil, and often higher in the
subsoil. Kankar nodules (2mm to 10cm diameter) are common.

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Calcareous soils are found in the arid and semi-arid regions of Gujarat:
  • North Gujarat: Banaskantha, Patan, and Mehsana districts — extensive areas of sandy
    calcareous soils derived from alluvium and wind-blown deposits.
  • Kutch: Northern and western Kutch — calcareous desert soils with high kankar content.
  • Saurashtra: Parts of Jamnagar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, and Porbandar — coastal calcareous
    soils formed from marine deposits and limestone weathering.
  • Middle Gujarat: Limited patches in Ahmedabad and Gandhinagar where groundwater is high
    in calcium bicarbonate.

Extent: Approximately 10–12% of Gujarat's total geographical area. The largest contiguous
area is in North Gujarat (Banaskantha-Patan-Mehsana belt).

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Best Crops (Adapted to Calcareous Conditions):
  • Bajra (Pearl Millet) — Highly tolerant of alkaline pH and low nutrient availability.
    GHB-538, GHB-732 perform well. Yields: 15–25 quintals/ha.
  • Castor — Excellent adaptation to calcareous soils. Deep roots access subsoil moisture.
    GCH-4, GCH-7. Yields: 15–25 quintals/ha.
  • Cumin — The most important spice crop on calcareous soils in Gujarat. GC-1, GC-2, GC-4.
    High-value crop with good market demand. Yields: 6–10 quintals/ha.
  • Fennel — Gujarat Fennel-1, GF-12. Tolerates high pH and responds well to micronutrient
    supplementation.
  • Fenugreek (Methi) — Leafy and seed varieties for local and export markets.
  • Mustard — Rai-5, GM-1. Tolerates alkaline conditions.
  • Wheat — With heavy micronutrient management. GW-273, GW-322. Yields: 3.0–4.5 tonnes/ha.

Moderately Suitable:
  • Cotton — With zinc and iron supplementation. Not ideal but grown in North Gujarat.
  • Groundnut — With foliar iron and zinc sprays. Better on sandy calcareous soils than
    on clayey calcareous soils.
  • Barley — Tolerates higher pH than wheat. Good for saline-calcareous areas.
  • Sorghum — With micronutrient management.

Unsuitable Crops:
  • Acid-loving crops: Tea, coffee, rubber, pineapple, blueberry (not grown in Gujarat
    anyway, but indicative).
  • Citrus (orange, lemon, kinnow) — severe iron and zinc chlorosis on calcareous soils
    unless heavily managed with chelates.
  • Potato — scab disease (Streptomyces scabies) is worse in high-pH soils; iron deficiency
    causes quality issues.
  • Rice — High pH reduces nutrient availability; poor drainage in heavy calcareous soils
    causes problems.

Yield Potential: Moderate to high with intensive management. The limiting factor is almost
always micronutrient availability, not soil depth or water. With proper micronutrient
management, cumin and fennel yields in North Gujarat match the best in the country.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 100–150 kg N/ha for cereals; 150–200 kg N/ha for cotton. Use ammonium
    sulphate or calcium ammonium nitrate (CAN) rather than straight urea to reduce
    ammonia volatilisation. Split applications are essential. Apply urea during cooler
    hours and incorporate into soil immediately.
  • Phosphorus: 50–80 kg P2O5/ha. Use water-soluble forms (DAP, SSP). Band placement
    near the seed is more effective than broadcasting due to high P fixation. Apply at
    sowing time.
  • Potassium: 30–50 kg K2O/ha. Most calcareous soils in Gujarat have adequate K.
  • Zinc: 25–30 kg zinc sulphate/ha at sowing. This is non-negotiable for most crops.
    Apply ZnSO4 in the seed furrow or broadcast and incorporate before sowing. Foliar
    spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 + 0.25% lime during vegetative growth for standing crops.
  • Iron: 25 kg ferrous sulphate/ha soil application OR foliar spray of 0.5% FeSO4
    (2–3 sprays at 15-day intervals) for crops showing chlorosis. For severe cases,
    use iron chelates (Fe-EDTA) at 1 kg/ha foliar.
  • Manganese: 15 kg manganese sulphate/ha soil application or 0.3% foliar spray.
  • Boron: 10 kg borax/ha for sensitive crops (cotton, groundnut, vegetables).
  • Sulphur: 20–30 kg S/ha for oilseeds and legumes. Gypsum (calcium sulphate) can
    supply both Ca and S but is only needed if soil Ca is low (rare in calcareous soils).

Irrigation: Drip irrigation is highly recommended for all crops to conserve water and
reduce nutrient leaching. Sandy calcareous soils require frequent, light irrigations.
Sprinkler irrigation is suitable for cereals and fodder. Flood irrigation should be
avoided on sandy calcareous soils as it wastes water and causes deep nutrient leaching.

Tillage: Light tillage is sufficient. Avoid deep ploughing which brings kankar nodules
to the surface and damages soil structure. Use rotavators or cultivators for seedbed
preparation.

Special Practices: Raised bed cultivation for vegetables and cumin to improve drainage
and aeration. Mulching with straw or plastic to reduce evaporation and soil temperature
in summer. Acidifying fertilisers (ammonium sulphate, ammonium phosphate) are preferred
over neutral/alkaline fertilisers.

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Iron and Zinc Deficiency: The most visible and economically damaging problem.
    Remedy: Prophylactic soil application of ZnSO4 and FeSO4; foliar sprays of chelated
    micronutrients; use of micronutrient-enriched fertilisers (Zn-enriched DAP). For
    orchards, trunk injection of iron chelates.
  • Phosphorus Fixation: High CaCO3 fixes P into unavailable forms. Remedy: Band placement
    of P fertiliser near the root zone; use of water-soluble P sources; application of
    organic matter (FYM) to reduce P fixation; use of PSB biofertiliser.
  • Ammonia Volatilisation: High pH causes rapid loss of N from surface-applied urea.
    Remedy: Deep placement of urea (7–10 cm depth); use of coated/slow-release urea;
    application during cooler hours; use of nitrification inhibitors.
  • Hardpan/Kankar Layers: Impede root growth and water movement. Remedy: Deep chiselling
    or subsoiling to break kankar layers; grow deep-rooted crops (castor, alfalfa) as
    biological drills; use explosives in extreme cases (rare, for orchards).
  • Soil Compaction: Low organic matter makes soil prone to compaction. Remedy: Add organic
    matter; avoid traffic on wet soil; use minimum tillage; maintain soil cover with
    residues or cover crops.
  • Salinity: In poorly drained calcareous areas, evaporation can cause salt accumulation.
    Remedy: Improve drainage; leach with excess irrigation; apply gypsum if sodium is high.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for calcareous soils:

  • pH: Optimal is 7.0–7.5. If pH > 8.5, expect severe micronutrient deficiencies.
    Apply sulphur or ammonium sulphate to lower pH marginally (difficult to change
    significantly in calcareous soils). Focus on micronutrient management.
  • ECe: If > 4 dS/m, salinity is a concern. Leach with excess irrigation and improve
    drainage.
  • Organic Matter: Target > 0.6%. If below 0.4%, apply FYM 15–20 tonnes/ha.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 150 kg/ha, increase N application and use slow-release
    forms. Include legumes in rotation.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 12 kg/ha, apply 80–100 kg P2O5/ha in bands. Use DAP
    or SSP, not rock phosphate.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.5 ppm. Apply 25–30 kg ZnSO4/ha prophylactically every year.
  • Iron: DTPA-extractable Fe < 4.5 ppm indicates deficiency. Apply FeSO4 or use foliar
    Fe-EDTA sprays.
  • Calcium Carbonate: If CaCO3 > 15%, expect micronutrient problems. Increase foliar
    micronutrient applications.

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 15–20 tonnes/ha annually. This is the single most
    important amendment for calcareous soils. Organic matter improves soil structure,
    reduces bulk density, increases water-holding capacity, and chelates micronutrients
    making them more available.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha, sunhemp, or cluster bean before cereals or
    cotton. Green manure adds biomass and nitrogen while improving soil physical properties.
  • Compost and Vermicompost: 3–5 tonnes/ha for intensive cropping. Vermicompost is rich
    in humic acids which chelate micronutrients and improve availability in high-pH soils.
  • Crop Residue Management: Do not burn crop residues. Incorporate wheat straw, bajra
    stover, and cotton stalks into the soil. Residue retention also reduces soil temperature
    and evaporation in summer.
  • Biofertilisers: PSB (Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria), Azotobacter, and VAM improve
    nutrient availability in the high-pH, low-organic-matter environment. VAM is particularly
    beneficial for phosphorus and micronutrient uptake.
  • Alley Cropping: Plant nitrogen-fixing trees (Leucaena, Gliricidia, Acacia) on field
    boundaries to provide green leaf manure, shade, and wind protection. Leaf litter from
    these trees adds organic matter and nutrients.
  • Cover Cropping: Use cowpea, moth bean, or horse gram during fallow periods to cover
    the soil, add biomass, and fix nitrogen.
  • Organic Farming: Transition to organic farming is feasible for cumin, fennel, and bajra
    in North Gujarat where water is available. The challenge is managing micronutrient
    supply without synthetic chelates. Rock phosphate + PSB + VAM + organic matter can
    maintain P levels. Foliar sprays of compost tea and vermiwash can supply micronutrients.
