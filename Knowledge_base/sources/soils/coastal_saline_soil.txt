COASTAL SALINE SOIL — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Coastal saline soils are salt-affected soils found in the maritime zones of Gujarat,
formed by the combined action of seawater intrusion, tidal flooding, and capillary rise
of saline groundwater. In the WRB classification, they are often Salic Fluvisols, Salic
Arenosols, or Solonchaks. In the USDA system, they are classified as Salids (Aridisols)
or Salic subgroups of Entisols and Inceptisols. These soils are characterised by high
electrical conductivity (ECe > 4 dS/m), sodium adsorption ratio (SAR > 13), and often
visible white salt crusts on the soil surface. They represent the most challenging
agricultural environment in Gujarat but can be reclaimed with sustained effort.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Sandy loam to sandy clay loam near the coast; clay loam in backwater and estuarine
areas. The texture varies with distance from the shoreline and the depositional environment.

Structure: Weak structure due to high sodium content. Sodium disperses clay particles,
destroying aggregates and creating a massive, hard-setting surface. The soil becomes
puddled and impermeable when wet.

Colour: Grey to greyish-brown with white salt efflorescence (salt crust) on the surface
during dry periods. In severely affected areas, the surface may appear almost white due
to salt crystallisation. Darker colours indicate organic matter in marshy areas.

Depth: Variable — shallow to deep. In some areas, a hard saline pan may occur at 20–50
cm depth, restricting root penetration.

Drainage: Poor to very poor. High sodium content causes clay dispersion, which blocks soil
pores and reduces infiltration rates to near zero. Water stands on the surface for days
after rainfall or irrigation.

Water-Holding Capacity: Low to moderate, but the high salinity makes much of the water
unavailable to plants (osmotic stress). Even when soil moisture is adequate, plants cannot
absorb water due to the high salt concentration in the soil solution.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 8.0 to 9.0, typically alkaline. The high pH is due to the presence of sodium
carbonate and sodium bicarbonate. In some areas, pH may exceed 9.5 during the dry season
when carbonate precipitates.

Electrical Conductivity (ECe): 4 to 30+ dS/m. Moderately saline (4–8 dS/m) soils can support
salt-tolerant crops; strongly saline (8–16 dS/m) soils require reclamation; very strongly
saline (>16 dS/m) soils are difficult to reclaim for agriculture.

Sodium Adsorption Ratio (SAR): 13 to 60+. High SAR indicates sodium dominance on the
exchange complex, which causes soil dispersion and poor physical conditions.

Exchangeable Sodium Percentage (ESP): 15 to 50+. ESP > 15 indicates sodic conditions; ESP
> 40 indicates severe sodicity.

Organic Matter: Very low (0.1–0.4%). The hostile environment (salinity, poor aeration)
suppresses microbial activity and organic matter decomposition.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Very low (80–150 kg/ha available N). Salinity inhibits nitrogen mineralisation
    and nitrification.
  • Phosphorus: Low (8–15 kg/ha available P). High calcium and sodium fix phosphorus into
    unavailable forms.
  • Potassium: Variable. Sodium competes with potassium for uptake, causing potassium
    deficiency even when soil K is adequate.

Micronutrients: Zinc, iron, and manganese deficiencies are common due to high pH and poor
aeration. Boron toxicity can occur in some coastal areas due to seawater boron.

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Coastal saline soils are found along the entire coastline of Gujarat:
  • Saurashtra Coast: Jamnagar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, Porbandar, Gir Somnath, Junagadh (coastal
talukas), Amreli (coastal talukas), Bhavnagar (coastal areas).
  • Kutch Coast: Mundra, Mandvi, Lakhpat, Abdasa — extensive areas of coastal salinity
    due to the proximity of the Rann of Kutch and the Arabian Sea.
  • South Gujarat Coast: Valsad, Navsari, Surat (coastal talukas), Bharuch (Gulf of Khambhat
    side). Salinity is less severe here due to higher rainfall but occurs in estuarine areas.
  • Gulf of Kutch: The entire Gulf of Kutch coastline has saline soils extending 5–20 km inland.

Extent: Approximately 5–7% of Gujarat's total geographical area. The most severely affected
districts are Jamnagar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, Porbandar, and Kutch.

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Salt-Tolerant Crops (Best Options):
  • Cotton — Moderately salt-tolerant. Grows on soils with ECe up to 8–10 dS/m with proper
    management. Bt cotton hybrids are grown in coastal Saurashtra. Yields: 1.5–2.5 quintals/ha
    (lint) under saline conditions.
  • Barley — Highly salt-tolerant cereal. Can grow on soils with ECe up to 12–16 dS/m.
    Not commonly grown in Gujarat but useful for reclamation and fodder.
  • Safflower — Moderately salt-tolerant oilseed. Good for partially reclaimed soils.
  • Sugarbeet — Highly salt-tolerant root crop. Can be grown on moderately saline soils with
    high irrigation.
  • Sorghum — Moderately salt-tolerant. Forage sorghum for green fodder.
  • Sesame (Til) — Moderately salt-tolerant oilseed. GT-1, GT-2 can tolerate ECe up to 6–8 dS/m.
  • Cluster Bean (Guar) — Moderately salt-tolerant and drought-tolerant. Useful for reclaiming
    saline soils while providing income.

Less Suitable (require significant reclamation):
  • Groundnut — Sensitive to salinity; pod rot increases in saline conditions. Only for
    mildly saline soils (ECe < 4 dS/m).
  • Wheat — Moderately sensitive; can grow on mildly saline soils with extra irrigation.
  • Bajra — Moderately tolerant but prefers non-saline conditions.

Unsuitable:
  • Rice — requires very low salinity; only for fully reclaimed or naturally non-saline pockets.
  • Citrus and most fruit crops — highly sensitive to salinity.
  • Vegetables — most are sensitive to salinity and should not be grown until reclamation
    is complete.

Yield Potential: Low to moderate under saline conditions. Reclaimed soils can achieve
yields comparable to non-saline soils. The key is that reclamation is a gradual process
(3–5 years) and farmers must use salt-tolerant crops during the reclamation period.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 80–120 kg N/ha for tolerant cereals; 120–150 kg N/ha for cotton. Use ammonium
    sulphate or calcium ammonium nitrate rather than urea (reduces ammonia volatilisation at
    high pH). Split applications are essential.
  • Phosphorus: 40–60 kg P2O5/ha. Band placement is more effective than broadcasting. Use DAP
    or SSP. Phosphorus availability is very low in saline-sodic soils.
  • Potassium: 30–50 kg K2O/ha. Extra K may be needed because sodium competes with K for uptake.
  • Zinc: 25 kg zinc sulphate/ha — essential in saline soils. Foliar spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 for
    standing crops.
  • Iron: Foliar spray of 0.5% FeSO4 (2–3 sprays) for chlorosis. Iron availability is severely
    reduced in saline-sodic conditions.
  • Sulphur: 20–30 kg S/ha. Sulphur is needed for protein synthesis and helps in sodium
    displacement (as calcium sulphate/gypsum).

Irrigation: Leaching is the key reclamation strategy. Apply irrigation in excess of crop
water requirement (leaching fraction of 15–20%) to dissolve salts and move them below the
root zone. Drip irrigation is preferred for established crops as it maintains uniform soil
moisture and prevents salt accumulation at the root zone. Never allow the soil to dry
completely — salt concentration increases as water evaporates.

Tillage: Minimal tillage to avoid bringing salts to the surface. Tillage also destroys the
thin surface mulch that reduces evaporation. If tillage is necessary, do it immediately
after irrigation when salts are leached downward.

Special Practices: Raised bed cultivation for salt-tolerant crops to improve drainage and
prevent waterlogging. Mulching with straw or plastic to reduce evaporation and salt
accumulation at the surface. Planting on the sides of ridges rather than the top (where
salts accumulate).

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • High Salinity (ECe > 8 dS/m): Remedy: Leach with excess irrigation water (canal or fresh
    groundwater); install subsurface drainage (mole drains or perforated pipes) to remove
    saline water from the root zone; grow salt-tolerant crops during reclamation.
  • Sodicity (ESP > 15): Remedy: Apply gypsum (calcium sulphate) at 5–10 tonnes/ha. Gypsum
    replaces sodium on the exchange complex with calcium, which improves soil structure
    and permeability. The replaced sodium is then leached out with irrigation. For severely
    sodic soils (ESP > 40), apply 10–15 tonnes/ha. This is the most important reclamation
    amendment for coastal saline soils in Gujarat.
  • Poor Drainage: Remedy: Open drainage channels; subsurface drainage systems; raised bed
    cultivation; avoid over-irrigation; break up hardpans with deep tillage.
  • Salt Crust Formation: Remedy: Mechanical removal of salt crusts (not recommended as it
    returns salts to the soil); light irrigation or rainfall to dissolve crusts; mulching
    to prevent evaporation; avoid fallowing (bare soil accumulates more salt).
  • Nutrient Imbalance: Remedy: Regular soil testing; balanced fertilisation; extra K to
    counteract Na competition; micronutrient foliar sprays.
  • Waterlogging: Remedy: Install drainage systems; grow rice in the first year of reclamation
    (rice tolerates waterlogging and helps leach salts); use raised beds for other crops.
  • Seawater Ingress: Remedy: In coastal aquifers, reduce groundwater pumping to prevent
    seawater intrusion; construct subsurface barriers where feasible; use only fresh water
    for irrigation.

Reclamation Sequence:
  Year 1: Apply gypsum (5–10 t/ha); leach heavily; grow dhaincha (Sesbania) or rice (puddling
    helps leaching). If no irrigation, grow salt-tolerant grasses or leave fallow with mulching.
  Year 2: Reapply gypsum (2–5 t/ha); grow barley or cotton. Continue leaching.
  Year 3: Apply gypsum as needed based on soil test; grow cotton or sorghum. Begin regular
    cropping.
  Year 4+: Normal cropping with salt-tolerant varieties; maintain leaching fraction; monitor
    soil salinity annually.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for coastal saline soils:

  • ECe (Electrical Conductivity): < 4 dS/m = non-saline; 4–8 = moderately saline (salt-
    tolerant crops possible); 8–16 = strongly saline (reclamation needed); > 16 = very
    strongly saline (difficult to reclaim).
  • pH: 8.0–8.5 = mildly alkaline; 8.5–9.0 = moderately alkaline (sodic); > 9.0 = strongly
    alkaline (severe sodicity). Apply gypsum if pH > 8.5 and ESP > 15.
  • SAR (Sodium Adsorption Ratio): < 13 = non-sodic; 13–20 = marginally sodic; 20–40 = sodic;
    > 40 = strongly sodic. Apply gypsum if SAR > 13.
  • ESP (Exchangeable Sodium Percentage): < 15 = non-sodic; 15–30 = sodic; 30–50 = strongly
    sodic; > 50 = very strongly sodic. Gypsum rate: 5 t/ha for ESP 15–30; 10 t/ha for ESP
    30–50; 15 t/ha for ESP > 50.
  • Organic Matter: Target > 0.5%. Apply FYM 20–25 tonnes/ha to improve structure and microbial
    activity.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 120 kg/ha, increase N application and use ammonium forms.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 10 kg/ha, apply 60–80 kg P2O5/ha in bands.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.5 ppm. Apply 25 kg ZnSO4/ha prophylactically.
  • Iron: DTPA-Fe < 4.5 ppm indicates deficiency. Foliar Fe spray is essential.

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 20–25 tonnes/ha annually. Organic matter is critical for
    improving soil structure in saline-sodic soils. Humic substances from organic matter
    help flocculate dispersed clay particles and improve permeability.
  • Green Manuring: Grow dhaincha (Sesbania aculeata) or sunnhemp (Crotalaria juncea) during
    the reclamation phase. These salt-tolerant legumes add biomass, fix nitrogen, and improve
    soil structure. Incorporate before flowering.
  • Compost: Use well-decomposed compost or vermicompost (5–8 tonnes/ha) to avoid salt
    addition from raw manure. Vermicompost is rich in humic acids which improve soil structure.
  • Crop Residue Management: Incorporate salt-tolerant crop residues (cotton, barley, sorghum)
    rather than burning. Residue mulch on the soil surface reduces evaporation and salt accumulation.
  • Biofertilisers: Use VAM (Vesicular Arbuscular Mycorrhiza) fungi which improve plant
    tolerance to salinity and enhance nutrient uptake. Azotobacter and phosphate-solubilising
    bacteria (PSB) help in nutrient-poor saline soils.
  • Afforestation: Plant salt-tolerant trees (Casuarina, Eucalyptus, Acacia, Prosopis) on
    field boundaries and degraded patches. Trees reduce wind speed, lower evaporation, and
    add leaf litter. Casuarina is particularly successful in coastal Gujarat.
  • Cover Cropping: Use cluster bean, cowpea, or horse gram during the reclamation period
    to cover the soil, add biomass, and prevent salt accumulation at the surface.
  • Organic Farming: Full organic farming is difficult on saline soils during the reclamation
    phase because organic matter decomposition is slow and nutrient release is insufficient
    for high yields. However, after 4–5 years of reclamation, organic practices can maintain
    soil health. Use compost tea and vermiwash as foliar supplements.
