================================================================================
ALLUVIAL SOIL — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Alluvial soils (Fluvisols and parts of Cambisols in WRB classification) are the most
fertile and agriculturally important soils in Gujarat. They are formed by the deposition
of sediments carried by rivers over thousands of years. These soils are young, immature
soils that have not undergone extensive weathering or profile development. They are
characterised by distinct horizontal stratification (layering) and excellent fertility
due to regular replenishment by fresh silt during river floods. In Gujarat, alluvial
soils are classified as Inceptisols and Entisols in the USDA Soil Taxonomy system.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Sandy loam to clay loam, depending on the distance from the river source and
the velocity of deposition. Near river banks, soils are coarser (sandy loam); further
away, they are finer (silty clay loam). The textural variability within a single field
is common.

Structure: Weak to moderate granular or sub-angular blocky structure. The stratified
nature creates distinct horizons that can affect root penetration and water movement.

Colour: Light grey to brownish-grey, often with visible mottling due to alternating
oxidation and reduction conditions. Darker colours indicate higher organic matter
content in older alluvial deposits.

Depth: Very deep to extremely deep (1.5 to 3+ metres), with no significant hardpan
or restrictive layer. This makes alluvial soils ideal for deep-rooted crops like
sugarcane and banana.

Drainage: Generally well-drained, but low-lying areas can suffer from poor drainage
and waterlogging during monsoon. Percolation rates are moderate to high (15–30 mm/hour).

Water-Holding Capacity: Moderate to high (120–180 mm/metre), depending on texture.
Clay loam alluvials have higher water retention than sandy loam alluvials.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 7.0 to 8.0, typically neutral to slightly alkaline. This is ideal for most
crops. Some areas near the coast may have pH up to 8.2 due to saline influence.

Organic Matter: Moderate (0.6–1.2%), higher than most Gujarat soils due to good vegetation
cover and regular deposition of fresh organic material. However, intensive cultivation
can deplete organic matter rapidly.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Low to moderate (150–250 kg/ha). Regular additions of organic matter
    and fertilisers are needed.
  • Phosphorus: Moderate to high in young alluvials; low to moderate in older, leached
    deposits. Available phosphorus ranges from 15–35 kg/ha.
  • Potassium: High due to the presence of K-bearing minerals (feldspars, micas) in
    river sediments. Available K often exceeds 250 kg/ha.

Micronutrients: Generally adequate, but zinc deficiency (Zn < 0.6 ppm) can occur in
high-pH areas. Iron, manganese, and copper are usually sufficient. Boron deficiency
may occur in intensively cropped vegetable areas.

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Moderate (15–25 cmol/kg), reflecting the mixed mineralogy
of the alluvium. This provides good nutrient retention.

Salinity: Generally low (ECe < 2 dS/m) except in coastal and poorly drained areas where
it can rise to 4–8 dS/m.

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Alluvial soils are found extensively along the major river valleys of Gujarat:
  • South Gujarat: Tapi river belt (Surat, Tapi districts), Narmada river valley
    (Bharuch, Narmada), Mahi river delta (Kheda, Anand).
  • Middle Gujarat: Sabarmati river valley (Ahmedabad, Gandhinagar), Mahi river
    (Kheda, Anand, Panchmahal).
  • North Gujarat: Banas and Saraswati river valleys (Sabarkantha, Banaskantha —
    limited alluvial patches).
  • Saurashtra: Limited alluvial deposits along seasonal rivers (Bhadar, Shetrunji)
    in Bhavnagar and Amreli.

Extent: Approximately 15–18% of Gujarat's total geographical area. The most productive
alluvial belts are the Tapi-Narmada deltaic region and the Mahi-Sabarmati river valleys.

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Best Crops (Highly Suitable):
  • Sugarcane — THE premier crop for alluvial soils. The deep, fertile, well-drained
    soil with high potassium and good water retention supports sustained sugarcane
    growth for multiple ratoons. Yields: 80–120 tonnes/ha.
  • Banana — Tissue-culture banana thrives in alluvial soils due to depth and fertility.
    Yields: 40–60 tonnes/ha.
  • Paddy (Rice) — Excellent for both kharif and rabi paddy under irrigation. Alluvial
    clay loams are ideal for paddy due to high water-holding capacity.
  • Vegetables — Tomato, brinjal, chilli, okra, cucurbits, and leafy vegetables. The
    high fertility and good drainage allow intensive year-round vegetable cultivation.
  • Wheat — Rabi wheat performs very well on alluvial soils with irrigation. Yields:
    4.5–6.5 tonnes/ha.
  • Cotton — Bt cotton on alluvial clay loams in Bharuch and Surat.

Moderately Suitable:
  • Groundnut — Good yields on sandy loam alluvials, but not as high as on black soils.
  • Maize — Suitable for kharif and rabi maize.
  • Turmeric and Ginger — on well-drained raised beds.

Unsuitable Crops:
  • Deep-rooted plantation crops (cashew, rubber) may face waterlogging in poorly
    drained alluvial pockets.
  • Cumin and fennel (which require well-drained sandy soils) are less suitable on
    heavy clay alluvials.

Yield Potential: Very high. Alluvial soils are among the highest-yielding soils in
Gujarat when properly irrigated and managed. Response to fertiliser and irrigation
is excellent.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 120–180 kg N/ha for cereals; 200–300 kg N/ha for sugarcane (split into
    4–5 applications).
  • Phosphorus: 40–60 kg P2O5/ha for most crops. Use DAP or SSP based on soil test.
  • Potassium: 30–50 kg K2O/ha for most crops. Higher K (60–80 kg/ha) for sugarcane
    and banana. Note: Young alluvials may need less K due to high native K.
  • Zinc: 25 kg zinc sulphate/ha if soil Zn < 0.6 ppm. Foliar spray (0.5% ZnSO4) during
    critical growth stages.
  • Boron: 10 kg borax/ha for vegetable and sugarcane areas showing deficiency.

Irrigation: Alluvial soils respond exceptionally well to irrigation. Recommended methods:
  • Flood irrigation for paddy and sugarcane (traditional but water-intensive).
  • Drip irrigation for banana, vegetables, and cotton (water-efficient, 40–60% savings).
  • Sprinkler irrigation for wheat and groundnut.
  • Subsurface drip irrigation is being tested for sugarcane in Surat and Bharuch.

Tillage: Light to medium tillage is sufficient. Avoid excessive ploughing which can
destroy the natural granular structure. Laser land levelling is highly recommended for
precision irrigation and water distribution.

Special Practices: Green manuring with dhaincha or sunhemp before paddy transplanting;
mulching with crop residues for vegetables to conserve moisture and suppress weeds;
raised bed cultivation for vegetables to prevent waterlogging in monsoon.

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Waterlogging: Low-lying alluvial areas can suffer from waterlogging during heavy
    monsoon. Remedy: Construct open drainage channels; install subsurface drainage (mole
    drains or perforated pipes); practice raised bed cultivation for vegetables; avoid
    continuous paddy flooding in the same field year after year.
  • Salinity Build-up: Poor drainage in canal command areas can lead to secondary
    salinisation. Remedy: Leach salts with extra irrigation; apply gypsum 2–5 tonnes/ha
    if soil pH > 8.5; improve drainage; avoid over-irrigation.
  • Soil Erosion: Riverbank erosion during floods can damage fields. Remedy: Plant
    vetiver grass and bamboo along riverbanks; construct earthen bunds; avoid cultivating
    right up to the river edge.
  • Nutrient Imbalance: Intensive cropping can deplete nitrogen and zinc. Remedy:
    Regular soil testing; balanced fertilisation; organic matter addition; crop rotation
    with legumes.
  • Flooding: Flash floods can deposit coarse sand and gravel on fields. Remedy: Remove
    coarse deposits; level the field; add organic matter to restore fertility; plant
    cover crops to stabilise the soil.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for alluvial soils, consider the following:

  • pH: Optimal range 7.0–7.5. If pH > 8.0, check for salinity (ECe) and apply gypsum
    if needed. If pH < 6.5, apply lime (rare in Gujarat alluvials).
  • Organic Matter: Target > 1.0%. If below 0.8%, apply FYM 10–15 tonnes/ha or green
    manure.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 200 kg/ha, apply higher N doses or include legumes in
    rotation.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 15 kg/ha, apply 60–80 kg P2O5/ha initially, then
    maintain with 40–50 kg/ha.
  • Potassium: If available K > 250 kg/ha, reduce K fertiliser or skip for one season.
    If K < 150 kg/ha, apply 60–80 kg K2O/ha.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.6 ppm. Apply 25 kg zinc sulphate/ha if deficient. Foliar
    spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 + 0.25% lime during peak vegetative stage for quick correction.
  • Iron: Deficiency is rare but can occur in high-pH areas. Foliar spray of 0.5% ferrous
    sulphate if symptoms appear (interveinal chlorosis in young leaves).

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 10–15 tonnes/ha annually. This is the single most
    effective amendment for alluvial soils, improving structure, water retention, and
    nutrient availability.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha (Sesbania aculeata) or sunhemp (Crotalaria
    juncea) before paddy or sugarcane. Green manure adds 80–120 kg N/ha equivalent.
  • Compost: Use vermicompost (3–5 tonnes/ha) or urban compost for vegetable fields.
  • Crop Residue Management: Incorporate wheat straw, sugarcane trash, and paddy straw
    into the soil rather than burning. This adds organic matter and prevents air pollution.
  • Biofertilisers: Use Azotobacter, Rhizobium, and PSB (Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria)
    to enhance nutrient cycling and reduce chemical fertiliser dependence.
  • Cover Cropping: Plant cowpea, cluster bean, or horse gram during fallow periods to
    protect soil and add biomass.
  • Organic Farming: The Tapi-Narmada belt has scope for organic vegetable and sugarcane
    farming due to good initial fertility and water availability. Transition period is
    3 years for organic certification.
