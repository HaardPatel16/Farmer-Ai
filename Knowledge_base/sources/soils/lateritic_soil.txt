LATERITIC SOIL — COMPLETE SOIL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. SOIL TYPE NAME & CLASSIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Lateritic soils, classified as Ferralsols (WRB) or Ultisols/Oxisols (USDA), are formed by
intense tropical weathering and leaching of parent rocks under high rainfall and temperature
conditions. The name "laterite" comes from the Latin word "later," meaning brick, because
these soils harden like iron when exposed to air. In Gujarat, lateritic soils are found in
the hilly and undulating terrain of the eastern tribal belt. They are characterised by high
iron and aluminium oxide content, acidic to neutral pH, low fertility, and distinctive red,
yellow, or brown colouration. These soils represent the oldest and most weathered soils in
the state, having lost most of their base minerals through millions of years of leaching.

2. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Texture: Clay to clay loam in the subsoil; sandy loam to loam in the surface layer due to
eluviation (leaching of clay from the topsoil). The clay fraction is dominated by kaolinite
(rather than smectite in black soils), which has low shrink-swell capacity.

Structure: Weak to moderate granular structure in the topsoil; massive or platy in the subsoil.
The presence of iron oxides can create indurated (hardened) layers at depth.

Colour: Red, reddish-brown, yellowish-red, or brown. The colour is due to the accumulation of
iron oxides (hematite, goethite) and aluminium oxides that remain after silica and bases have
been leached away. Bright red colour indicates well-drained conditions; yellowish colour
indicates periodic waterlogging.

Depth: Shallow to moderate (30–80 cm). In many areas, a hard lateritic pan or ironstone
concretions (murram) occur at 30–50 cm depth, restricting root penetration. On steep slopes,
soil depth may be less than 20 cm over bedrock.

Drainage: Well-drained to excessively drained on slopes; poor drainage in valley bottoms and
where hardpans occur. The high rainfall in the tribal belt means that drainage is generally
adequate, but saturation can occur during intense monsoon events.

Water-Holding Capacity: Low to moderate (80–140 mm/metre). The kaolinitic clay has lower
water-holding capacity than smectite clay. Sandy surface layers drain quickly. However, clay
subsoils can hold water but it is often tightly bound.

Erosion Risk: Very high. Lateritic soils on slopes are highly susceptible to erosion due to:
  • High rainfall intensity in the tribal belt
  • Weak soil structure
  • Shallow depth
  • Removal of vegetation cover
Sheet erosion, rill erosion, and gully formation are common problems.

3. CHEMICAL PROPERTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
pH Range: 5.0 to 6.5, typically acidic to slightly acidic. The acidity is due to the leaching
of bases (calcium, magnesium, potassium) and the presence of aluminium ions in the soil
solution. In some areas, pH may drop to 4.5 in the subsoil.

Organic Matter: Low to moderate (0.5–1.0%). Despite forest cover, the high temperature and
rainfall accelerate decomposition. Organic matter content is higher under forest than under
agriculture.

NPK Status:
  • Nitrogen: Low (120–200 kg/ha available N). Low organic matter and rapid mineralisation
    limit nitrogen availability.
  • Phosphorus: Very low (5–15 kg/ha available P). This is the most limiting nutrient in
    lateritic soils. Phosphorus is strongly fixed by iron and aluminium oxides into
    insoluble forms (Fe-P and Al-P compounds). Even when high P fertiliser is applied,
    much of it becomes unavailable within weeks.
  • Potassium: Low to moderate (100–200 kg/ha available K). Bases have been leached, but
    some K remains in the weathered minerals.

Micronutrients: Variable. Iron and aluminium are abundant (they form the soil itself), but
availability depends on pH. At low pH, aluminium toxicity can occur (root damage, stunted
growth). Manganese toxicity can occur in waterlogged conditions. Zinc and boron deficiencies
are common. Copper is generally adequate.

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Low to moderate (10–20 cmol/kg). The kaolinite clay has low
CEC compared to smectite. This means the soil has limited ability to retain nutrients, making
frequent fertiliser applications necessary.

Base Saturation: Low (<50%). The soil is dominated by hydrogen and aluminium ions on the
exchange complex. Calcium and magnesium are deficient.

Aluminium Toxicity: Exchangeable aluminium > 2 ppm can cause root tip damage and poor nutrient
uptake in sensitive crops. This is a concern in strongly acidic lateritic soils (pH < 5.2).

4. WHERE FOUND IN GUJARAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Lateritic soils are found in the eastern hilly and tribal districts of Gujarat:
  • Dang District: The entire district, especially in the Western Ghats region. The highest
    rainfall area in Gujarat with dense forests and steep slopes.
  • Tapi District: Hilly talukas (Vyara, Nizar, Uchhal) and the Satpura range foothills.
  • Narmada District: Hilly and undulating areas, particularly in the eastern talukas bordering
    Madhya Pradesh.
  • Chhota Udaipur District: The entire district, characterised by undulating terrain and rocky
    outcrops. Some of the most degraded agricultural lands in Gujarat are found here.
  • Panchmahal District: Eastern hilly talukas (Godhra, Lunavada, Santrampur, Kadana).
  • Dahod District: Eastern hilly areas bordering Madhya Pradesh.
  • Mahisagar District: Hilly and undulating terrain, especially in the eastern part.
  • Vadodara District: Limited patches in the eastern hilly talukas (Chhota Udaipur border).

Extent: Approximately 12–15% of Gujarat's total geographical area. The lateritic zone is
predominantly tribal, with lower agricultural productivity than the alluvial and black soil
regions.

5. SUITABILITY FOR CROPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Best Crops (Adapted to Lateritic Conditions):
  • Maize — The most important staple crop of the tribal belt. Tolerates acidic conditions
    and grows well with moderate management. Varieties: Gujarat Makka-1, Ganga-5. Yields:
    20–30 quintals/ha with good management (10–15 q/ha under poor management).
  • Paddy (Rice) — Both upland and lowland rice. Tolerates acidity and poor drainage. Local
    landraces are adapted to these conditions. Varieties: GR-11, Jaya. Yields: 25–35 quintals/ha
    (transplanted) in valley areas.
  • Tur (Pigeon Pea) — Long-duration varieties tolerate poor soil and fix nitrogen. BDN-2,
    ICPL-87. Yields: 12–18 quintals/ha.
  • Sorghum (Jowar) — Drought-tolerant grain and fodder sorghum on marginal lands.
  • Millets — Finger millet (ragi) and pearl millet (bajra) in drier areas.
  • Tuber Crops — Tapioca (cassava), sweet potato, and yam are traditional crops that tolerate
    poor soils and provide food security.
  • Cashew — The most suitable plantation crop for lateritic soils. Expanding in Chhota
    Udaipur and Narmada. Tolerates acidity and poor fertility.
  • Pineapple — Tolerates acidity and can grow on slopes with terracing.

Moderately Suitable:
  • Wheat — With heavy liming and fertilisation. Yields are lower than on alluvial or black soils.
  • Gram (Chickpea) — On limed soils with phosphorus fertilisation.
  • Vegetables — Tomato, brinjal, chilli with heavy amendment and irrigation.
  • Mango — Local varieties perform reasonably well with management.
  • Rubber — Experimental plantations in Dang (requires very high rainfall and terracing).

Unsuitable Crops:
  • Cotton — Requires neutral pH and high fertility; poor performance on acidic lateritic soils
    without heavy amendment.
  • Sugarcane — Requires deep, fertile, well-watered soils; not suitable for most lateritic areas.
  • Groundnut — Pod development is poor; iron chlorosis is not a problem (iron is abundant) but
    calcium deficiency can cause empty pods.
  • Cumin and fennel — Require well-drained, calcareous soils; completely unsuitable.
  • Citrus — Lime-induced chlorosis is not the issue (iron is abundant), but manganese toxicity
    and poor drainage can cause problems.

Yield Potential: Low to moderate under traditional management. With intensive amendment
(liming, heavy organic matter, phosphorus fertilisation), yields can approach those of alluvial
soils. However, the cost of amendment is often prohibitive for small tribal farmers. The key
is to match crops to soil capabilities rather than attempting to convert lateritic soils
into high-input fields.

6. MANAGEMENT PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fertiliser Recommendations:
  • Nitrogen: 80–150 kg N/ha for cereals; 120–180 kg N/ha for cotton (if grown). Use ammonium
    sulphate or urea. Split applications are essential due to low CEC and leaching risk.
  • Phosphorus: 60–100 kg P2O5/ha. This is higher than for other soils because of extreme P
    fixation. Use water-soluble P (DAP, SSP) and apply in bands near the root zone. Apply
    at sowing time. Rock phosphate is NOT suitable for these soils due to low acidity
    in some areas (pH > 5.5). Use of PSB (Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria) biofertiliser
    is strongly recommended.
  • Potassium: 40–60 kg K2O/ha. Most lateritic soils are deficient in K due to leaching.
  • Lime: 2–4 tonnes/ha of agricultural lime (dolomite or calcite) every 3–4 years to raise
    pH to 6.0–6.5. Lime application is essential for maize, wheat, and legumes. Apply lime
    2–3 months before sowing and incorporate thoroughly. Lime also improves phosphorus
    availability by reducing Fe/Al fixation.
  • Zinc: 25 kg zinc sulphate/ha. Deficiency is common due to low organic matter and high pH
    after liming. Foliar spray of 0.5% ZnSO4 for standing crops.
  • Boron: 10 kg borax/ha for legumes and vegetables.
  • Iron: Generally not needed as iron oxides are abundant. However, if pH is raised too high
    by over-liming, iron deficiency can occur. Maintain pH below 6.8 to prevent this.
  • Manganese: Monitor for toxicity if pH < 5.2. Apply lime if Mn toxicity symptoms appear
    (brown spots on leaves, stunted roots).

Irrigation: Limited irrigation is practiced due to hilly terrain and poor water access.
Where available:
  • Drip irrigation is preferred for vegetables and horticulture on terraced fields.
  • Sprinkler irrigation for cereals on flat valley areas.
  • Tank irrigation from traditional village ponds for paddy transplantation.
  • Micro-catchments and farm ponds for supplemental irrigation.

Tillage: Careful tillage is needed to prevent erosion:
  • Contour tillage (ploughing along the contour, not up and down the slope) is mandatory.
  • Minimum tillage to preserve soil structure and reduce erosion risk.
  • Deep tillage should be avoided on shallow soils over bedrock.
  • Use of hand tools (hoe, pickaxe) is common on steep slopes where machinery cannot operate.

Special Practices:
  • Terracing: Essential for cropping on slopes >10%. Bench terraces, contour bunds, and
    half-moon terraces are used in Dang and Chhota Udaipur. Terracing reduces erosion by
    80–90% and creates level planting surfaces.
  • Contour Farming: Planting along contours, not up and down slopes. This is the minimum
    requirement for any cultivation on lateritic slopes.
  • Agroforestry: Planting nitrogen-fixing trees (Albizia, Leucaena, Gliricidia) along
    contours and field boundaries. Trees stabilise soil, provide shade, and add leaf litter.
  • Mulching: Apply crop residue mulch or grass mulch to reduce erosion, conserve moisture,
    and moderate soil temperature. This is especially important in the dry season.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate green manure crops (dhaincha, sunnhemp, cowpea) before
    maize or paddy to add nitrogen and organic matter.

7. PROBLEMS & REMEDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Soil Erosion: THE most severe and chronic problem. Heavy monsoon rains on slopes with weak
    soil structure cause massive soil loss. Remedy: Terracing; contour bunding; contour farming;
    vegetative barriers (vetiver grass, lemon grass); agroforestry; maintain ground cover;
    avoid bare fallows; construct check dams in gullies.
  • Acidity and Aluminium Toxicity: Low pH and high exchangeable aluminium damage roots and
    reduce nutrient uptake. Remedy: Apply agricultural lime (2–4 t/ha) every 3–4 years; grow
    acid-tolerant crops (maize, paddy, cashew); use acid-tolerant varieties; avoid over-liming
    (pH > 6.8 can cause micronutrient deficiencies).
  • Phosphorus Fixation: The most limiting nutrient constraint. Iron and aluminium oxides
    fix applied phosphorus into unavailable forms. Remedy: Apply P in bands near roots; use
    water-soluble P fertilisers; apply PSB biofertiliser; maintain pH 6.0–6.5 (optimal for P
    availability); use organic matter to chelate Fe/Al and reduce fixation; apply small,
    frequent P doses rather than one large application.
  • Low Fertility: Low N, P, K, and organic matter. Remedy: Heavy organic matter addition (FYM
    15–20 t/ha, green manuring); balanced fertilisation based on soil test; inclusion of
    legumes in rotation; use of biofertilisers; shifting to agroforestry and horticulture which
    better match soil capabilities.
  • Shallow Depth: Hard rock or lateritic pan close to surface restricts rooting. Remedy:
    Grow shallow-rooted crops; use raised beds to increase effective rooting depth; break
    hardpans with blasting or deep ripping (expensive, rarely done); focus on perennial crops
    (cashew, mango) that can penetrate cracks in rock.
  • Water Scarcity: Despite high rainfall, water runs off quickly due to slopes and poor
    infiltration. Remedy: Construct farm ponds, check dams, and percolation tanks; practice
    contour bunding to capture runoff; use mulching to increase infiltration; plant cover
    crops during monsoon to reduce runoff.
  • Poor Drainage in Valleys: Valley bottoms can become waterlogged during monsoon. Remedy:
    Open drainage channels; raised bed cultivation for vegetables; grow rice in waterlogged
    patches.
  • Nutrient Leaching: High rainfall leaches nutrients from the sandy surface layer. Remedy:
    Split fertiliser applications; use slow-release fertilisers; maintain organic matter to
    improve nutrient retention; apply fertilisers just before planting rather than far in advance.

8. SOIL TESTING INTERPRETATION for this soil type
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When interpreting soil test results for lateritic soils:

  • pH: Optimal range 6.0–6.5. If pH < 5.5, apply lime (2–4 t/ha) and expect aluminium toxicity.
    If pH > 6.8, expect micronutrient deficiencies (iron, zinc, manganese) despite high
    total iron content.
  • Organic Matter: Target > 1.0%. If below 0.6%, apply FYM 20 t/ha and green manure.
  • Nitrogen: If available N < 150 kg/ha, increase N application and include legumes.
  • Phosphorus: If available P < 10 kg/ha, apply 80–100 kg P2O5/ha in bands. Use DAP or SSP.
    Monitor P fixation — retest after 6 months to assess recovery.
  • Potassium: If available K < 150 kg/ha, apply 60–80 kg K2O/ha. Most lateritic soils need
    regular K application.
  • Zinc: Critical level 0.6 ppm. Apply 25 kg ZnSO4/ha prophylactically.
  • Lime Requirement: If pH < 5.5, apply 3–4 t/ha agricultural lime. If pH 5.5–6.0, apply
    2–3 t/ha. Apply 2–3 months before sowing and incorporate thoroughly.
  • Aluminium: If exchangeable Al > 2 ppm, apply lime immediately. Toxicity symptoms include
    stunted roots, poor tillering, and purple leaf colouration.
  • Iron: Total Fe is high but available Fe may be low if pH > 6.5. DTPA-Fe < 4.5 ppm indicates
    deficiency. Foliar Fe spray if symptoms appear.

9. ORGANIC MATTER IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Farm Yard Manure (FYM): Apply 15–20 tonnes/ha annually. This is critical for improving
    soil structure, water retention, and nutrient availability in lateritic soils. Organic
    matter also chelates iron and aluminium, reducing phosphorus fixation.
  • Green Manuring: Incorporate dhaincha, sunnhemp, or cluster bean before maize or paddy.
    Green manure adds 80–120 kg N/ha, improves structure, and increases microbial activity.
    In the tribal belt, dhaincha is grown in jhum fallows before shifting cultivation.
  • Compost and Vermicompost: 5–8 tonnes/ha for intensive cropping. Vermicompost is rich in
    humic acids which improve soil structure and chelate micronutrients. Vermicompost is
    particularly effective in reducing phosphorus fixation in lateritic soils.
  • Crop Residue Management: Do not burn residues. Incorporate maize stover, paddy straw,
    and legume residues into the soil. Mulching with crop residues reduces erosion and
    adds organic matter gradually.
  • Biofertilisers: PSB (essential for phosphorus management), Azotobacter, Azospirillum,
    and VAM. VAM fungi improve phosphorus uptake in high P-fixing lateritic soils and
    enhance drought tolerance.
  • Agroforestry: Integrate trees (cashew, mango, jackfruit, bamboo) into cropping systems.
    Leaf litter from trees adds organic matter, improves soil structure, and provides shade.
    Cashew is particularly suitable as it tolerates poor soils and provides income.
  • Cover Cropping: Use cowpea, horse gram, or moth bean during fallow periods to cover soil,
    add biomass, and fix nitrogen. Cover crops are especially important during monsoon to
    prevent erosion.
  • Forest Litter Collection: In forested areas (Dang), collect and compost leaf litter from
    forest floors (with permission) to make rich organic compost. This traditional practice
    provides high-quality organic matter.
  • Organic Farming: Transition to organic farming is well-suited for lateritic soils in the
tribal belt because many farmers already use low chemical inputs. However, maintaining
    phosphorus supply without synthetic fertilisers is challenging. Use rock phosphate + PSB
    + VAM + organic matter. Foliar sprays of compost tea and vermiwash can supply
    micronutrients. The 3-year transition period is manageable for subsistence crops.
