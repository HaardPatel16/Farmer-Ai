KUTCH RAINFALL & AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kutch (Kachchh) is the largest district of Gujarat, located in the north-western
extremity of the state, bordering Pakistan and the Arabian Sea. It includes the
Rann of Kutch (Great and Little Rann), Banni grasslands, and the mainland Kutch.
The district is divided into talukas such as Bhuj, Anjar, Mundra, Mandvi, Rapar,
Lakhpat, Nakhatrana, and Abdasa. Agriculture in Kutch is among the most challenging
in Gujarat due to aridity, salinity, and climatic extremes. The district is famous
for its handicrafts, salt production, and livestock (Kutchi camel, Kankrej cattle),
but crop agriculture is limited to specific zones.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: Kutch receives the lowest rainfall in Gujarat, averaging 300mm
to 450mm annually, with the western and northern fringes (Rann area) receiving
less than 250mm. The monsoon is highly erratic and concentrated in July–August.
Frequently, the district receives below-normal rainfall during the kharif season,
forcing farmers to rely on drought-mitigation strategies.

Temperature: Extreme temperatures are characteristic. Summer (April–June) sees
temperatures of 40°C to 46°C, with the Rann surface exceeding 50°C. Winter
(December–January) nights can drop to 4°C to 8°C, with frost risk in low-lying
areas. The diurnal temperature range is very high (15–20°C difference).

Humidity: Extremely low humidity (15–25%) during summer, rising to 60–70% during
monsoon. The arid climate causes very high evapotranspiration rates (2000mm+ annually).

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought is a near-perennial risk. The district faces drought
conditions in 3 out of 5 years. Flooding is rare but can occur in the Rann when
cyclonic rainfall from the Arabian Sea drains into the flat salt desert. The 2001
earthquake also altered local drainage patterns in some areas.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kutch has diverse but generally poor soils:
  • Sandy desert soils (Aeolian sands) in northern and western Kutch — very low
    water-holding, low organic matter, shifting dunes.
  • Saline-alkaline soils (usar/reh) around the Rann fringe — high EC and pH,
    hard surface pans, poor crop establishment.
  • Medium black soils in parts of Bhuj, Anjar, and Mundra — the best agricultural
    soils but limited in extent.
  • Coastal saline soils in Mundra, Mandvi, and Lakhpat — tidal influence,
    high sodium content.

General soil fertility: Very low organic matter (0.1–0.4%), low nitrogen, phosphorus,
and zinc. High calcium carbonate in many areas. Salinity is the dominant soil
constraint.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops (strictly rainfed or minimal irrigation):
  • Cotton — the most important cash crop; drought-resistant and short-duration
    Bt cotton varieties (e.g., RCH-2, various BG-II hybrids) are essential.
    Sowing: June 25 to July 20, only after assured rainfall.
  • Bajra (Pearl Millet) — GHB-538, GHB-732; drought-escape crop on sandy soils.
  • Guar (Cluster Bean) — drought-tolerant legume for fodder and gum; crucial
    for soil nitrogen fixation.
  • Castor — GCH-4, GCH-7 on deeper soils.
  • Moth Bean (Matki) — traditional pulse of Kutch on marginal lands.

Rabi Crops (very limited, on irrigated pockets or residual moisture):
  • Cumin — high-value spice crop in Anjar, Bhuj, and Rapar with irrigation.
  • Mustard — Rai-5, GM-1 on sandy soils.
  • Wheat — very limited, only in command areas or with borewell irrigation.

Crop Rotation:
  • Bajra – Guar – Fallow (3-year rotation on sandy soils)
  • Cotton – Cumin (with supplemental irrigation)
  • Castor – Fallow (on marginal lands)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Water is the most critical limiting factor. The district has no perennial rivers.
The Narmada canal system does not reach Kutch (except through the proposed
Kutch Branch Canal, partially under construction). Groundwater is saline in most
areas; only deep aquifers in Bhuj, Anjar, and Nakhatrana provide fresh water.

Traditional water sources: Tanks (villages), farm ponds, and check dams are vital.
Rainwater harvesting from rooftops and roads is promoted. The Kutch branch of the
Sardar Sarovar canal (when completed) will transform the agricultural potential.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Very late and risky. Kharif sowing only after cumulative rainfall
exceeds 60mm. Farmers often practice "dry sowing" (pre-sowing before rains) with
risk-mitigation. If monsoon fails by July 20, farmers switch to contingency crops
like guar or fodder sorghum.

Irrigation Methods: Drip irrigation is non-negotiable for any irrigated crop due
to extreme water scarcity. Subsurface drip is experimented with for cotton. Flood
irrigation is practically absent except in a few canal command areas.

Intercropping: Cotton + Guar (2:1) is common. Mixed cropping of bajra + moth bean
provides food and fodder security.

Special Practices: Deep ploughing to break hardpans in black soils; sand dune
stabilisation with Prosopis and Acacia; use of windbreaks to reduce evaporation;
mulching with crop residues to conserve soil moisture.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Extreme Water Scarcity: The defining challenge. Most agriculture is rainfed.
  • Salinity: Soil and groundwater salinity limits crop choice in over 40% of
the district.
  • Pests: Whitefly in cotton, termites in sandy soils, spodoptera in irrigated
    vegetable pockets.
  • Diseases: Wilt in cotton and guar; root rot in bajra during waterlogging
    (rare heavy rain events).
  • Weather: Heatwaves, dust storms, delayed monsoon, and intermittent droughts.
  • Livestock-Crop Conflict: Free grazing by large livestock populations damages
    standing crops.
  • Soil: Wind erosion, sand dune encroachment, and poor soil fertility.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • PMFBY — crop insurance is critical for Kutch farmers due to high rainfall risk.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies (drip/sprinkler) at 55–90% subsidy.
  • MGNREGA — water harvesting structures, check dams, and afforestation.
  • National Mission on Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA) — drought-proofing and
    land reclamation.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — for saline soil management and amendment advice.
  • Gujarat State Agricultural Marketing Board — APMC infrastructure for cotton
    and cumin marketing.
  • Kutch Branch Canal (Sardar Sarovar Project) — ongoing infrastructure project
    that will bring Narmada water to the district.
  • Disaster Relief and Drought Assistance — from Gujarat State Disaster Management.
