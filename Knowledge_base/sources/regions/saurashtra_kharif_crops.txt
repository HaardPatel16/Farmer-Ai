SAURASHTRA KHARIF CROPS & CROPPING PATTERN — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The Saurashtra peninsula is the largest and most agriculturally distinctive region
of Gujarat, comprising the districts of Rajkot, Amreli, Junagadh, Gir Somnath,
Bhavnagar, Morbi, Jamnagar, Porbandar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, and Botad. It is a triangular
peninsula jutting into the Arabian Sea, covering approximately 35,000 square
kilometres. Saurashtra is the backbone of Gujarat's groundnut and cotton economy
and is often called the "groundnut bowl" of India. The region is predominantly
semi-arid, with agriculture shaped by monsoon dependence, water scarcity, and
the unique agro-ecology of the peninsula.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: Annual rainfall varies from 550mm in the western parts (Jamnagar,
Devbhoomi Dwarka) to 750mm in the eastern parts (Bhavnagar, Junagadh). The monsoon
arrives around June 10–20 in southern Saurashtra and June 20–30 in northern
Saurashtra. The coefficient of variability is high (30–40%), making rainfall
unreliable and drought a recurring feature.

Temperature: Summer temperatures (April–June) range from 38°C to 44°C, with coastal
areas moderated by sea breezes. Winter temperatures are 10°C to 28°C. The region
experiences strong hot winds ("loo") during May and June.

Humidity: Coastal areas have high humidity (75–85%) during monsoon; inland areas
are drier (60–70%). Post-monsoon humidity drops rapidly, affecting rabi crop
establishment on residual moisture.

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought is the dominant risk — moderate to severe drought occurs
once every 3–4 years. The region also faces "pauna" (mid-monsoon dry spells) in
July–August, which are critical for groundnut pod development. Flooding is rare
except in localized depression areas after cyclonic rainfall.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Saurashtra has a mosaic of soil types:
  • Shallow to Medium Black Cotton Soils (Vertisols) — Dominant in central and
    eastern Saurashtra (Rajkot, Amreli, Bhavnagar, Junagadh). Clay content 40–50%,
    high water-holding capacity but prone to cracking and waterlogging. Depth 30–80cm.
  • Sandy Loams — Coastal fringe talukas (Porbandar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, coastal
    Jamnagar). Well-drained but poor water and nutrient retention. Salinity issues
    within 10–15km of the coast.
  • Rocky Terrain — Gir plateau (Gir Somnath, Junagadh) and parts of Bhavnagar.
    Shallow soils with patches of forest. Suitable for mango orchards and fodder.

General fertility: Low to moderate organic matter (0.3–0.6%), moderate potassium,
low to moderate phosphorus, widespread zinc deficiency. Soil crusting after heavy
rains is a major problem for groundnut emergence.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops (the defining agricultural season):
  • Groundnut — THE dominant crop of Saurashtra. Gujarat is the largest groundnut-
    producing state in India, and Saurashtra accounts for over 70% of Gujarat's
    production. Varieties: GJG-9, GJG-22, GJG-32, GG-7, GG-20, ICGV-91114.
    Sowing: June 15 to July 20. Most groundnut is rainfed. Pod-filling stage
    (August–September) is the most water-sensitive period.
  • Cotton — Bt cotton is the second most important kharif crop, especially on
    medium to heavy black soils in Rajkot, Amreli, and Bhavnagar. Hybrids: RCH-2,
    Bunny, various BG-II varieties. Increasingly irrigated via check dams and lift
    irrigation.
  • Bajra (Pearl Millet) — GHB-538, GHB-732; grown on lighter sandy soils and
    as a drought-escape crop. Important for food and fodder security.
  • Sesame (Til) — GT-1, GT-2; often intercropped with groundnut or grown as a
    mixed crop.
  • Castor — GCH-4, GCH-7; on deeper soils and in mixed cropping systems.
  • Pigeon Pea (Tur) — limited but expanding in Junagadh and Gir Somnath.

Post-Monsoon (Rabi) Crops:
  • Very limited rabi cultivation due to water scarcity. Only on residual soil
    moisture or where check dams provide supplemental irrigation.
  • Wheat — GW-273, GW-322 in limited irrigated pockets.
  • Chickpea (Gram) — GG-1, GG-2 on medium black soils that retain moisture.
  • Cumin — high-value spice crop in irrigated coastal areas (Jamnagar, Porbandar).

Recommended Rotations:
  • Groundnut – Wheat (irrigated, limited areas)
  • Cotton – Chickpea (on medium black soils with moisture retention)
  • Bajra – Gram (on lighter soils)
  • Groundnut + Castor (6:2 intercropping — very common)
  • Groundnut + Cotton (1:1 in some areas)
  • Cotton + Green Gram (1:1 relay)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Water scarcity is THE defining constraint of Saurashtra agriculture:
  • No Major Rivers — The peninsula has no perennial rivers. The Bhadar, Shetrunji,
    and Aji are seasonal rivers that dry up by winter.
  • Groundwater — Over-exploited in most districts. Water tables have declined by
    2–5 metres in the past two decades. Salinity ingress in coastal aquifers.
  • Check Dams — The backbone of Saurashtra's water harvesting strategy. Over
    100,000 check dams and farm ponds have been constructed, transforming the
    water availability in many villages.
  • Narmada Canal — The Sauni Yojana (Saurashtra Narmada Avartan Irrigation) is
    a mega-project piping Narmada water to Saurashtra dams, which is revolutionizing
    irrigation in the region. Phase 1 and 2 are operational; Phase 3 under construction.
  • Lift Irrigation — From seasonal rivers and check dams for cotton and groundnut.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing is strictly monsoon-dependent. Groundnut: June 20
– July 20. Cotton: June 15 – July 10. Bajra: July 1–25. Delayed sowing beyond
July 25 drastically reduces yields. Farmers often practice "pre-monsoon sowing" on
light soils if early showers arrive.

Irrigation Methods: Drip irrigation is heavily promoted and subsidised under
PM-KISAN and GGRC schemes. It is now the dominant method for cotton and is
expanding for groundnut. Sprinkler irrigation is used for wheat and fodder.

Intercropping: Groundnut + Castor (6:2 rows) is the most common intercropping system,
providing income stability if one crop fails. Mixed cropping of bajra + pigeon pea
is practiced in marginal areas.

Special Practices: Aflatoxin management in groundnut (proper drying, storage);
integrated pest management (IPM) for bollworm in cotton; seed treatment with
thiram + carbendazim for groundnut to prevent soil-borne diseases; contour bunding
and graded bunding for soil conservation; laser land levelling for water-efficient
irrigation.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Water Scarcity: The single biggest challenge. Most agriculture is rainfed.
    Groundnut yields are highly variable due to rainfall uncertainty.
  • Pests: White grub (Holotrichia consanguinea) — the most devastating pest of
    groundnut in Saurashtra, causing 20–40% yield loss. Spodoptera and pink bollworm
    in cotton. Jassids and thrips in cotton and groundnut.
  • Diseases: Tikka leaf spot (Cercospora) and collar rot (Aspergillus) in
    groundnut; wilt and bollworm in cotton; downy mildew in bajra.
  • Weather: Delayed monsoon onset, mid-season dry spells (July–August), terminal
    drought during groundnut pod-filling, and cyclonic disturbances in September.
  • Soil: Zinc deficiency in over 60% of soils; iron chlorosis in calcareous
    patches; soil crusting after heavy rains impeding groundnut emergence; poor
    drainage in black cotton soils during wet years.
  • Post-Harvest: Aflatoxin contamination in groundnut if not dried properly;
    price volatility for groundnut and cotton.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Sauni Yojana — flagship project bringing Narmada water to Saurashtra dams.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies (drip/sprinkler) at 55–90% subsidy.
  • Mukhya Mantri Sujalam Sufalam Jal Sanchay Yojana — water harvesting structures.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — free soil testing and fertiliser recommendations.
  • National Food Security Mission (NFSM) — oilseeds (groundnut) and pulses.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for groundnut, cotton, and bajra.
  • Gujarat State Agricultural Marketing Board (GSAMB) — APMC infrastructure,
    price support for groundnut under MSP.
  • Kisan Call Centre (KCC) — 1800-180-1551 for advisory.
