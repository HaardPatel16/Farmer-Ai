SOUTH GUJARAT DETAILED AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
South Gujarat is the southernmost and most humid agro-climatic zone of Gujarat,
extending from the industrial hub of Surat to the forested hills of Dang district
on the Maharashtra border. It encompasses the districts of Surat, Bharuch, Navsari,
Valsad, Tapi, Dang, and Narmada. The zone is defined by the Tapi and Narmada river
basins, the Western Ghats in Dang, and the coastal plains of Valsad and Navsari.
This is the most agriculturally diverse region of Gujarat, supporting tropical
horticulture (mango, banana), intensive cash cropping (sugarcane, cotton), rice
cultivation, and tribal shifting agriculture in the hills. The region benefits
from abundant water, fertile soils, and proximity to major urban markets (Surat,
Mumbai).

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: South Gujarat receives the highest rainfall in Gujarat, with a
stark gradient from west to east:
  • Dang district: 2000–3000mm (orographic rainfall from the Western Ghats)
  • Valsad and Navsari: 1500–2000mm
  • Surat and Tapi: 1200–1600mm
  • Bharuch and Narmada: 1000–1400mm

The monsoon arrives early (June 10–15) and withdraws late (October 5–15). The zone
benefits from both the Arabian Sea branch and retreating monsoon depressions. Dang
district can receive rainfall until mid-October.

Temperature: Moderate throughout the year. Summer: 32–38°C. Winter: 12–28°C. The
Western Ghats and coastal influence prevent temperature extremes. Dang district is
cooler (max 30°C in summer, min 8°C in winter).

Humidity: Very high year-round. Monsoon: 80–95%. Winter: 65–75%. The high humidity
supports lush vegetation but also favours fungal and bacterial diseases.

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought is extremely rare. The primary risk is EXCESS water and
poor drainage. Flash floods are common during monsoon depressions (200–400mm in 24
hours). The Tapi and Narmada rivers flood during heavy upstream rainfall. Surat city
has experienced major flooding (1968, 2006). Waterlogging in black cotton soils is a
persistent problem in Bharuch and Narmada.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Deep Black Cotton Soils (Deep Vertisols) — Found in Bharuch, Narmada, and parts
    of Surat. Very deep (1–2 metres), high clay content (50–60% montmorillonite),
    high water-holding capacity, slightly alkaline (pH 7.5–8.5). These are the most
    productive soils in the zone, supporting cotton, sugarcane, and soybean.
  • Alluvial Soils — Found along the Tapi, Narmada, Purna, and Kim rivers in Surat,
    Bharuch, Navsari, and Valsad. Deep, fertile, sandy loam to clay loam, well-
    drained, pH 7.0–7.8. Ideal for sugarcane, banana, paddy, and vegetables. High
    natural fertility due to annual river silt deposition.
  • Lateritic Soils — Found in the hilly talukas of Dang, Tapi, and parts of Valsad.
    Formed by intense weathering in high rainfall. Acidic (pH 4.5–5.5), low in
    phosphorus, prone to erosion. Suitable for forest species, cashew, rubber, and
    pineapple.

General fertility: Alluvial and black soils are moderate to high fertility. Lateritic
soils require heavy amendment. Organic matter is moderate to high (0.8–1.5%) in
alluvial and black soils, but very low (<0.5%) in lateritic soils.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops:
  • Sugarcane — THE dominant cash crop of the Tapi belt. Surat, Bharuch, and Tapi
    districts have the highest sugarcane productivity in Gujarat. Varieties: Co-86032,
    CoM-0265, Co-0238. Multiple sugar mills operate in this belt. Ratoon crops
    contribute significantly to farmer income.
  • Paddy (Rice) — Major in Valsad, Navsari, Tapi, and Dang. Both direct-seeded and
    transplanted systems are used. Varieties: GR-11, Jaya, Pusa-44, hybrid rice.
    Upland rice in Dang and Tapi hills.
  • Cotton — Bt cotton on black soils in Bharuch, Narmada, and Surat. Hybrids: RCH-2,
    Bunny, various BG-II varieties.
  • Groundnut — GJG-9, GJG-22 in Valsad and Navsari (moderate rainfall, well-drained
    alluvial soils).
  • Vegetables — Tomato, brinjal, chilli, okra, cucurbits, and leafy vegetables for
    Surat city and Mumbai markets. Intensive year-round cultivation.

Rabi and Year-Round Crops:
  • Mango — Alphonso (Hapus) orchards in Valsad and Navsari produce export-quality
    fruit. Kesar mango is also grown. Flowering: November–December; harvest: April–May.
    The humid climate and alluvial soils give South Gujarat mangoes their distinctive
    sweetness and fibre-free texture.
  • Banana — Tissue-culture banana plantations in Bharuch, Narmada, and Surat.
    Varieties: Grand Naine, Robusta. Year-round production with drip irrigation.
  • Coconut — Coastal talukas of Valsad and Navsari have traditional coconut
    plantations.

Tribal and Forest Areas (Dang, Narmada, Tapi, Chhota Udaipur border):
  • Rice (upland and lowland), Maize, Tur (pigeon pea), Millets (ragi, sorghum, pearl
    millet), Tuber crops (tapioca, sweet potato, yam), and Forest produce (bamboo,
    mahua, tendu leaves).
  • Shifting cultivation (jhum) is practiced in remote Dang hills on a limited scale.

Recommended Rotations:
  • Sugarcane – Ratoon – Soybean / Green Gram (3-year system)
  • Paddy – Wheat / Paddy – Gram (double cropping with irrigation)
  • Cotton – Wheat (on black soils)
  • Mango – Vegetables (intercropping in young orchards, 0–5 years)
  • Banana – Papaya – Vegetables (multi-tier system)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
South Gujarat has the most abundant water resources in the state:
  • Sardar Sarovar (Narmada) Canal — The largest canal command in this zone. The
    main canal and distributaries (Bharuch, Narmada, Surat, Tapi branches) provide
    reliable irrigation for sugarcane, cotton, and paddy. The Narmada canal is the
    backbone of modern agriculture in South Gujarat.
  • Tapi River System — Direct river irrigation and lift irrigation from the Tapi
    and its tributaries (Purna, Girna, Bori) in Surat and Tapi districts. The Ukai
    dam is the major storage project.
  • Narmada River — Direct lift irrigation and canal command in Narmada and Bharuch
    districts. The Sardar Sarovar dam is the source.
  • Groundwater — Shallow and generally good quality in alluvial areas (5–15m depth).
    However, water tables are declining in intensively cultivated areas of Surat and
    Bharuch due to year-round sugarcane and banana cultivation.
  • Minor Irrigation Projects — Karjan dam, Kakrapar weir, and numerous minor tanks
    provide supplemental irrigation.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing begins very early (June 10–25) due to early and reliable
monsoon. Sugarcane is planted year-round with peak planting in February–March (spring
plant) and September–October (autumn plant). Paddy is transplanted in July–August.
Rabi wheat is sown in November–December. Mango is natural flowering — no sowing window.

Irrigation Methods: Flood irrigation is still widely used for sugarcane and paddy due
to abundant water availability. Drip irrigation is rapidly expanding for sugarcane,
banana, mango, and vegetables under PMKSY and GGRC schemes. Subsurface drip is being
trialed for sugarcane. Sprinkler irrigation is used for vegetables and groundnut.

Drainage Management: This is the most critical practice in South Gujarat. Farmers must:
  • Maintain open drainage channels between fields.
  • Install subsurface drainage (mole drains, perforated pipes) in sugarcane fields.
  • Practice raised bed cultivation for vegetables to prevent waterlogging.
  • Construct field bunds with proper overflow outlets.
  • Avoid continuous flooding in paddy to prevent methane emissions and soil sickness.

Intercropping: Multi-tier systems are common:
  • Sugarcane + Soybean (relay-sown after earthing-up)
  • Banana + Papaya (short-term intercrop)
  • Mango + Vegetables (intercropping in young orchards)
  • Coconut + Pepper + Cocoa (traditional homestead model in Valsad/Navsari)

Special Practices: Integrated Nutrient Management (INM) for sugarcane (organic manure
+ green manure + chemical fertilisers); prophylactic fungicide sprays for mango
anthracnose and powdery mildew; hormone (ethephon) treatment for uniform mango
flowering; ratoon management in sugarcane (stubble shaving, gap filling, trash blanketing);
plastic mulching for vegetables to prevent weed growth and conserve moisture.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Drainage / Waterlogging: Excess rainfall and poor drainage cause waterlogging in
    low-lying alluvial areas and black cotton soils. Root rot in sugarcane, yellowing
    and wilting in paddy, and fruit drop in mango are direct consequences.
  • Post-Harvest Losses: High humidity causes rapid spoilage of perishables (mango,
    banana, vegetables). Inadequate cold storage and poor road connectivity in remote
    areas (Dang, Tapi) lead to 20–30% post-harvest losses.
  • Pests: Sugarcane stem borer (Chilo auricilius), early shoot borer (Chilo infuscatellus);
    brown plant hopper (BPH) and stem borer (Scirpophaga) in paddy; mango hopper and
    fruit fly (Bactrocera dorsalis); mealybug and banana bunchy top virus (BBTV) in
    banana; diamondback moth in cabbage and cauliflower.
  • Diseases: Red rot (Colletotrichum) and wilt in sugarcane; blast (Pyricularia) and
    sheath rot in paddy; Panama wilt (Fusarium) and Sigatoka leaf spot in banana;
    anthracnose and powdery mildew in mango; downy mildew and bacterial wilt in
    vegetables; yellow vein mosaic in okra.
  • Weather: Flash floods during monsoon depressions (July–September); strong winds
    during cyclones causing lodging in sugarcane and banana; heavy cloud cover in
    November–December affecting mango flowering; unseasonal rains during mango harvest
    (April–May) causing fruit cracking and quality loss.
  • Soil: Waterlogging-induced salinity in poorly drained black cotton soils; iron
    and zinc deficiency in alkaline alluvial soils; acidity and erosion in lateritic
    hilly areas; declining organic matter due to intensive monoculture of sugarcane.
  • Market Access: While Surat provides excellent market access for nearby farmers,
    remote tribal areas (Dang, eastern Tapi) suffer from poor road connectivity and
    limited market infrastructure.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Sardar Sarovar Canal Command Area Development — canal lining, water distribution,
    and field channels in Bharuch, Narmada, Surat, and Tapi.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies for sugarcane, banana, mango, and vegetables.
  • National Horticulture Mission (NHM) — mango, banana, and vegetable development;
    nursery infrastructure; organic farming promotion.
  • Agricultural Export Zone (AEZ) — mango and banana export promotion, quality
    certification, and packhouse infrastructure in Valsad and Navsari.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — intensive soil testing for sugarcane and paddy fields.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for sugarcane, paddy, cotton, groundnut, and mango.
  • Rashtriya Krishi Vikas Yojana (RKVY) — integrated farming, value chain development,
    and farmer producer organisation (FPO) support.
  • Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) — special programmes for Dang, Narmada, and Tapi tribal
    farmers including rice intensification, horticulture, and livestock development.
  • Mission for Integrated Development of Horticulture (MIDH) — orchard development,
    protected cultivation, and post-harvest infrastructure.
  • National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA) — organic farming, water
    conservation, and climate-resilient practices in hilly areas.
