================================================================================
COASTAL SAURASHTRA AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Coastal Saurashtra forms the maritime agro-climatic belt along the Arabian Sea
coast of Gujarat. It includes the districts of Porbandar, Devbhoomi Dwarka,
Jamnagar, Gir Somnath, and the coastal talukas of Junagadh and Amreli. The zone
is characterised by flat to gently rolling coastal plains, sandy loam soils near
the shore, and increasing clay content inland. This region is a major contributor
to Gujarat's groundnut economy and serves as an important fisheries-agriculture
interface. Coastal wind patterns, sea breeze, and monsoon depressions from the
Arabian Sea create a unique microclimate distinct from inland Saurashtra.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: Annual rainfall ranges from 600mm to 800mm, with the monsoon
arriving slightly earlier (mid-June) due to proximity to the sea. The zone is
prone to erratic rainfall distribution, with some years receiving heavy downpours
from cyclonic depressions.

Temperature: Moderated by sea breezes. Summer temperatures range from 34°C to 40°C,
significantly lower than inland Saurashtra. Winter temperatures are pleasant, from
12°C to 28°C. Coastal areas rarely experience frost.

Humidity: High humidity prevails during monsoon (75–90%) and post-monsoon months,
which increases disease pressure in groundnut and cotton. Sea spray contributes to
salt deposition on crops in areas within 2km of the shoreline.

Drought/Flood Risk: Moderate drought risk, but the greater threat is salinity
ingression in groundwater due to tidal influence and over-extraction. Cyclonic
disturbances can cause storm surges and flooding in low-lying coastal areas,
especially in Porbandar and Devbhoomi Dwarka.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Soil types transition from coastal sandy loams to medium black cotton soils inland.
Coastal soils (within 10–15km of shoreline) are often saline-sodic due to seawater
ingress. The pH ranges from 7.8 to 8.8 near the coast, improving to 7.5–8.0 inland.
Organic matter is generally low (0.3–0.5%). Coastal soils are well-drained but have
poor water and nutrient retention. Inland soils retain more moisture and support
heavier crops. Gypsum application is commonly needed within 15km of the coast.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops:
  • Groundnut — the dominant crop; GJG-9, GJG-22, and GJG-32 varieties are
    popular. Sowing: June 15 to July 10. Coastal sandy loams are ideal for
    pod development.
  • Cotton — BG-II hybrids on medium black soils inland; salinity-tolerant
    varieties recommended near coast.
  • Onion — kharif onion is a major cash crop in Jamnagar and Porbandar;
    varieties like Agrifound Dark Red and Baswant-780 are grown.
  • Bajra — marginal areas and sandy soils.
  • Sesame and Castor — mixed cropping on marginal lands.

Rabi Crops:
  • Cumin — high-value spice crop in Jamnagar and Porbandar districts.
  • Wheat — limited areas with irrigation.
  • Gram (Chickpea) — on residual moisture.

Recommended Rotations:
  • Groundnut – Cumin (highly profitable in irrigated areas)
  • Cotton – Gram
  • Onion – Fallow (if no irrigation)
  • Groundnut + Onion (intercropping on wider rows)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Groundwater is the primary source, but coastal aquifers suffer from salinity
ingression. In Jamnagar, Porbandar, and Devbhoomi Dwarka, groundwater within 10km
of the coast is often brackish (EC > 3 dS/m). The Narmada canal system does not
reach this zone; instead, local water harvesting (check dams, percolation tanks)
and the Sauni Yojana (filling local dams with Narmada water via pipelines) are
critical. Farm ponds and roof rainwater harvesting are increasingly adopted.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing begins with pre-monsoon showers (June 10–25).
Groundnut is sown early to utilise full monsoon. Cotton sowing extends to July 10.
Rabi cumin is sown in October–November after groundnut harvest.

Irrigation Methods: Drip irrigation is essential for cotton, cumin, and onion.
Sprinkler systems are used for groundnut protection irrigation. Wind-resistant
sprinkler designs are preferred due to strong coastal winds.

Intercropping: Groundnut + Castor (6:2) and Cotton + Onion are practiced. Multi-
tier cropping with coconut (in homesteads) is traditional in coastal villages.

Special Practices: Gypsum application (2–5 tonnes/ha) for salinity reclamation;
salinity-tolerant seed varieties; raised bed cultivation for onion to prevent
waterlogging.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Salinity: Groundwater salinity ingress within 10–15km of coast; soil
    salinity in low-lying areas.
  • Pests: White grub in groundnut, spodoptera and jassids in cotton, thrips
    in onion.
  • Diseases: Tikka leaf spot and rust in groundnut; fusarium wilt in cotton
    and onion; downy mildew in pearl millet.
  • Weather: Cyclonic storms and storm surges (June–September); strong sea winds
    causing lodging in tall crops; erratic monsoon breaks.
  • Soil: Coastal sand dunes shifting; poor nutrient retention in sandy soils.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Sauni Yojana (Saurashtra Narmada Avartan Irrigation) — Narmada water to
    Saurashtra dams via pipelines and canals.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies for drip and sprinkler.
  • MGNREGA — check dams, farm ponds, and coastal shelterbelt plantations.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — especially for saline soil management.
  • National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA) — drought-proofing and
    land reclamation.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for groundnut and cotton.
  • Gujarat State Horticulture Mission — support for coconut and mango orchards.

9. DISTRICTS INCLUDED IN THIS ZONE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Porbandar (entire district)
  • Devbhoomi Dwarka (entire district)
  • Jamnagar (entire district)
  • Gir Somnath (entire district)
  • Junagadh (coastal talukas: Visavadar, Keshod, Mangrol, Malia, etc.)
  • Amreli (coastal talukas: Rajula, Jafrabad, etc.)
