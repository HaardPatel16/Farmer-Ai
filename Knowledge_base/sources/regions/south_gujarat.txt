SOUTH GUJARAT AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
South Gujarat is the most humid and agriculturally diversified zone of Gujarat,
receiving the highest rainfall in the state. It encompasses the districts of Surat,
Navsari, Valsad, Bharuch, Tapi, Dang, and Narmada. The zone is bordered by Maharashtra
to the south and south-east, and by the Arabian Sea to the west. The terrain varies
from flat alluvial plains along the Tapi and Narmada rivers to the densely forested
Western Ghats (Dang district). South Gujarat is the sugarcane, mango, and rice belt
of the state, with strong horticultural and cash crop orientation. The region is also
industrialised, with Surat being a major urban centre demanding large quantities of
vegetables and dairy products.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: South Gujarat receives the highest rainfall in Gujarat, ranging
from 1200mm to 3000mm annually. The distribution is highly uneven:
  • Dang district: 2000–3000mm ( receives monsoon clouds from the Western Ghats)
  • Valsad and Navsari: 1500–2000mm
  • Surat and Tapi: 1200–1600mm
  • Bharuch and Narmada: 1000–1400mm

The monsoon arrives early (June 10–15) and withdraws late (October 5–15). The zone
also receives significant post-monsoon rainfall from retreating monsoon depressions
and cyclonic systems from the Bay of Bengal.

Temperature: Moderate temperatures due to proximity to the sea and high cloud cover.
Summer (April–June): 32°C to 38°C. Winter (December–February): 12°C to 28°C. The
zone rarely experiences extreme heat or frost.

Humidity: Very high humidity throughout the year (70–90% during monsoon; 55–70% in
winter). The high humidity favours fungal diseases in crops and requires prophylactic
management.

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought is rare in this zone. The dominant risk is EXCESS water —
waterlogging, flooding, and poor drainage. Heavy rainfall events (200–400mm in 24
hours) are common during monsoon depressions, causing flash floods in Surat, Tapi,
and Dang. The Tapi and Narmada rivers are prone to flooding during heavy upstream
rainfall.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Deep Black Cotton Soils (Vertisols) — Found in Bharuch, Narmada, and parts of
    Surat. Very deep (1–2 metres), high clay content (50–60%), high water-holding
capacity, and slightly alkaline (pH 7.5–8.5). Excellent for cotton, sugarcane, and
    soybean.
  • Alluvial Soils — Found along the Tapi, Narmada, and Purna river valleys in
    Surat, Bharuch, and Navsari. Highly fertile, well-drained, sandy loam to clay
    loam texture. Ideal for sugarcane, banana, and vegetables.
  • Lateritic Soils — Found in the hilly talukas of Dang, Tapi, and parts of
    Valsad. Acidic, low in fertility, prone to erosion. Suitable for forest crops,
    cashew, and rubber.

General fertility: Moderate to high in alluvial areas; high potassium in black soils;
low phosphorus and zinc in lateritic soils. Organic matter is moderate to high
(0.8–1.5%) in alluvial and black soils due to good vegetation cover.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops:
  • Sugarcane — THE dominant cash crop of South Gujarat. The Tapi river belt
    (Surat, Bharuch, Tapi) is the largest sugarcane-growing area in Gujarat with
    multiple sugar mills. Varieties: Co-86032, CoM-0265, Co-0238. Ratoon management
    is critical for profitability.
  • Paddy (Rice) — Major crop in Valsad, Navsari, Tapi, and Dang. Varieties: GR-11,
    Jaya, Pusa-44, and hybrid rice. Transplanted kharif paddy dominates.
  • Cotton — Bt cotton on black soils in Bharuch, Narmada, and Surat.
  • Groundnut — GJG-9, GJG-22 in Valsad and Navsari (moderate rainfall areas).
  • Vegetables — Tomato, brinjal, chilli, okra, and cucurbits for Surat city market.

Rabi and Year-Round Crops:
  • Mango — Alphonso (Hapus) orchards in Valsad and Navsari are famous for export-
    quality fruit. Flowering: November–December; harvest: April–May.
  • Banana — Bharuch and Narmada districts have large banana plantations. Tissue
    culture varieties are popular.
  • Vegetables — Year-round cultivation in Surat, Navsari, and Valsad for urban
    markets.

Tribal and Forest Areas (Dang, Narmada, Tapi, Chhota Udaipur border):
  • Rice (upland and lowland), Maize, Tur (pigeon pea), tuber crops (tapioca, sweet
    potato), and millets (ragi, sorghum).

Recommended Rotations:
  • Sugarcane – Ratoon – Legume (soybean or green gram)
  • Paddy – Wheat / Paddy – Gram (where irrigation is available)
  • Cotton – Wheat (on black soils)
  • Mango – Vegetables (intercropping in young orchards)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
South Gujarat has abundant water resources compared to other zones:
  • Sardar Sarovar (Narmada) Canal — Commands large parts of Bharuch, Narmada,
    Surat, and Tapi. The main canal and distributary network provide reliable
    irrigation for sugarcane and cotton.
  • Tapi River System — Direct irrigation from the Tapi river and its tributaries
    (Purna, Girna) for sugarcane in Surat and Tapi districts.
  • Narmada River — Direct lift irrigation in Narmada and Bharuch districts.
  • Groundwater — Generally good quality and shallow (5–15m) in alluvial areas.
    However, over-extraction in Surat and Bharuch is causing water table decline.
  • Minor Dams — Ukai (Tapi), Karjan, and other medium projects provide supplemental
    irrigation.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing begins early (June 10–25) due to early monsoon arrival.
Sugarcane is planted year-round but peak planting is February–March and September–
October. Paddy is transplanted July–August. Mango flowering is natural (November–
December) with no sowing window.

Irrigation Methods: Flood irrigation is still dominant for sugarcane and paddy due
to abundant water. Drip irrigation is expanding for sugarcane, banana, and vegetables
under PMKSY. Sprinkler irrigation is used for vegetables and groundnut.

Drainage Management: This is THE critical practice in South Gujarat. Farmers must
maintain open drainage channels, subsurface drainage in sugarcane fields, and
raised bed cultivation for vegetables to prevent waterlogging.

Intercropping: Sugarcane + Soybean (relay), Banana + Papaya, Mango + Vegetables
(young orchards), and Coconut + Pepper (in homesteads) are common.

Special Practices: Integrated pest management for sugarcane borers; prophylactic
sprays for downy mildew and blast in paddy; hormone treatment for mango flowering;
ratoon management in sugarcane (stubble shaving, gap filling, and heavy fertilisation).

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Drainage / Waterlogging: Excess rainfall causes waterlogging in low-lying areas,
    damaging standing crops. Root rot in sugarcane and yellowing in paddy are common.
  • Post-Harvest Losses: Perishables (mango, banana, vegetables) suffer high losses
    due to inadequate cold chain infrastructure and rapid ripening in humid weather.
  • Pests: Sugarcane stem borer (Chilo), early shoot borer; brown plant hopper and
    stem borer in paddy; mango hopper and fruit fly; mealybug in banana.
  • Diseases: Red rot and wilt in sugarcane; blast and sheath rot in paddy;
    Panama wilt in banana; anthracnose in mango; powdery mildew in vegetables.
  • Weather: Flash floods during monsoon depressions; strong winds during cyclones
    causing lodging in sugarcane and banana; heavy cloud cover affecting mango
    flowering.
  • Soil: Waterlogging-induced salinity in poorly drained areas; iron and zinc
    deficiency in alkaline alluvial soils; acidity and erosion in lateritic hilly
    areas.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Sardar Sarovar Canal Command Area Development — extensive coverage in this zone.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation for sugarcane, banana, and vegetables.
  • National Horticulture Mission — mango, banana, and vegetable development.
  • Agricultural Export Zone (AEZ) — mango and banana export infrastructure.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — for precision nutrient management in sugarcane.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for sugarcane, paddy, cotton, and groundnut.
  • Rashtriya Krishi Vikas Yojana (RKVY) — integrated farming and value chain
    development.
  • Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) — for Dang, Narmada, and Tapi tribal farmers.
