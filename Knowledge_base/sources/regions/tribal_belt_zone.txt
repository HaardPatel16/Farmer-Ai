TRIBAL BELT AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The Eastern Tribal Belt of Gujarat is a distinct agro-climatic zone characterised
by hilly and undulating terrain, forest cover, tribal populations, and predominantly
rainfed agriculture. It encompasses the districts of Dahod, Chhota Udaipur, Panchmahal,
Mahisagar, Narmada, Tapi, and Dang. The zone borders Madhya Pradesh and Maharashtra
and is part of the larger tribal region of eastern Gujarat. The terrain ranges from
rolling hills in Panchmahal and Dahod to steep Western Ghats in Dang. Agriculture
here is largely subsistence-oriented, with maize, paddy, and tur (pigeon pea) as
staple crops. The zone has significant potential for horticulture (mango, cashew) and
forest-based enterprises, but infrastructure and market access remain challenges.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: The zone receives good rainfall ranging from 900mm to 2200mm
annually, with a steep gradient from west to east:
  • Dang district: 2000–2200mm (highest in Gujarat, orographic effect from Western Ghats)
  • Tapi and Narmada: 1200–1600mm
  • Panchmahal and Dahod: 900–1100mm
  • Chhota Udaipur and Mahisagar: 900–1200mm

The monsoon arrives by June 15–20 and withdraws by October 10–15. The zone benefits
from both south-west and retreating monsoon systems.

Temperature: Summer temperatures (April–June): 36–42°C in the western parts (Dahod,
Panchmahal); cooler (30–35°C) in Dang due to elevation and forest cover. Winter
(December–February): 8–26°C. Frost is rare except in elevated areas of Dang and
Panchmahal.

Humidity: Moderate to high. Monsoon: 75–90%. Winter: 50–65%. Dang district maintains
higher humidity year-round due to dense forest cover.

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought risk is moderate in the western parts (Dahod, Panchmahal)
where rainfall is lower and more variable. Flood and landslide risk is significant
in Dang and Tapi during heavy monsoon events due to steep terrain and poor drainage
in narrow valleys. Soil erosion is a chronic problem across the zone.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Lateritic Soils — Dominant in hilly areas. Formed by intense weathering in high
    rainfall. Acidic (pH 5.0–6.5), low in organic matter and phosphorus, prone to
    erosion. Red and yellow colouration due to iron and aluminium oxides.
  • Medium Black Soils — Found in valleys and flat areas of Dahod, Panchmahal, and
    Chhota Udaipur. Moderate water-holding capacity, better fertility than lateritic
    soils, but prone to waterlogging in depressions.
  • Alluvial Soils — Limited patches along river valleys (Mahi, Narmada, Tapi
    tributaries). More fertile and deeper, supporting the best crop yields in the zone.
  • Shallow Rocky Soils — Extensive on hill slopes and plateaus. Very thin soil cover
    over bedrock, suitable only for forest species and fodder grasses.

General fertility: Low to moderate. Organic matter is low (0.3–0.8%) due to rapid
mineralisation in warm climate. Nitrogen and phosphorus are generally deficient.
Potassium is moderate. Zinc, boron, and iron deficiencies are common in lateritic
areas.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops (dominant, mostly rainfed):
  • Maize — THE staple crop of the tribal belt. Grown extensively in Dahod, Panchmahal,
    Chhota Udaipur, and Narmada. Varieties: Gujarat Makka-1, Ganga-5, African Tall.
    Used for food, fodder, and local liquor production. Sowing: June 15 – July 15.
  • Paddy (Rice) — Upland and lowland rice in Dang, Tapi, Narmada, and Chhota Udaipur.
    Varieties: GR-11, Jaya, and local landraces. Transplanted in lowland areas;
    direct-seeded on uplands and terraced fields.
  • Tur (Pigeon Pea) — The most important pulse crop. Long-duration varieties (6–9
    months) are common. Intercropped with maize or sorghum. Varieties: BDN-2, ICPL-87.
  • Sorghum (Jowar) — Fodder and grain sorghum on marginal lands.
  • Millets — Pearl millet (bajra) and finger millet (ragi) in drier areas.
  • Tuber Crops — Tapioca (cassava), sweet potato, and yam are traditional subsistence
    crops, especially in Dang and Narmada.
  • Vegetables — Tomato, brinjal, chilli, and beans for local consumption and nearby
    urban markets (Godhra, Dahod).

Rabi Crops (limited, where water is available):
  • Wheat — GW-273, GW-322 in irrigated pockets of Panchmahal and Dahod.
  • Gram (Chickpea) — GG-1, GG-2 on medium black soils.
  • Mustard — Rai-5, GM-1 in non-irrigated areas.
  • Lentils and Peas — minor crops for local consumption.

Forest Produce and Horticulture:
  • Mango (Kesar and local varieties) — orchards in Panchmahal and Dahod.
  • Cashew — expanding in Chhota Udaipur and Narmada on lateritic soils.
  • Bamboo, Mahua, Tendu leaves — important non-timber forest products (NTFPs) for
tribal income.
  • Teak and mixed forest species — Dang district has dense teak forests.

Recommended Rotations:
  • Maize – Wheat (irrigated pockets)
  • Maize + Tur (intercropping — most common system)
  • Paddy – Gram (lowland areas)
  • Sorghum – Mustard (marginal lands)
  • Fallow – Maize – Tur (3-year rotation on poor soils)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Water availability is a major constraint despite good rainfall:
  • Rainfed Dependence: Over 80% of cropped area is rainfed. Single-cropping is the
    norm on most farms.
  • Minor Rivers — Mahi, Narmada, and Tapi tributaries provide seasonal surface water.
    The Panam and Karjan rivers are important in Panchmahal.
  • Tanks and Ponds — Traditional village tanks are the primary source of supplemental
    irrigation. Many are silted and need desilting.
  • Check Dams — Increasingly constructed under MGNREGA and tribal welfare schemes to
    capture monsoon runoff and enable a second crop.
  • Groundwater — Limited and often deep (30–60m) in hilly areas. Water quality is
    generally good but extraction is difficult and expensive for small farmers.
  • Narmada Canal — Limited reach in this zone; only parts of Narmada and Panchmahal
    have canal access.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing is closely tied to monsoon onset (June 15 – July 15).
Late sowing after July 20 severely reduces yields. Rabi sowing is October–November,
only in areas with residual moisture or irrigation.

Irrigation Methods: Very limited irrigation. Where available, flood irrigation from
tanks is traditional. Drip irrigation is being promoted under PMKSY for horticulture
(mango, cashew, vegetables). Micro-sprinklers are used for nursery raising.

Soil and Water Conservation: This is the highest priority for sustainable agriculture in
    the tribal belt:
  • Contour bunding and contour farming on slopes to prevent erosion.
  • Bench terracing in steep areas (Dang, Tapi).
  • Check dams and gully plugs to capture runoff and recharge groundwater.
  • Farm ponds for water harvesting.
  • Agroforestry (Albizia, Leucaena, Casuarina) on field boundaries to stabilise soils
    and provide fodder/firewood.

Intercropping: Maize + Tur (1:1 or 2:1) is the traditional and most sustainable system.
Mixed cropping of sorghum + pigeon pea + cowpea is also practiced. Multi-tier systems
with mango + vegetables + pulses are promoted in horticultural pockets.

Special Practices: Shifting cultivation (jhum) is practiced in remote Dang areas on
a limited scale; settled agriculture is promoted by the government. Forest-based farming
(alley cropping, taungya) is traditional. Organic farming is common due to low chemical
input use. Seed treatment with biofertilisers (Rhizobium, PSB) is promoted for pulses.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Soil Erosion: The most severe and chronic problem. Heavy monsoon rains on sloping
    terrain cause sheet erosion, gully formation, and topsoil loss. Degraded lands are
    common in Dahod and Panchmahal.
  • Water Scarcity: Despite good rainfall, most agriculture is rainfed and single-crop.
    Lack of water harvesting infrastructure limits cropping intensity.
  • Pests: Stem borer in maize and sorghum; pod borer in pigeon pea; root knot nematode
    in vegetables; termites in timber species.
  • Diseases: Turmeric leaf blight in tuber crops; wilt in pigeon pea; rust in sorghum;
    blast in rice; mosaic disease in vegetables.
  • Weather: Delayed monsoon, mid-season dry spells, heavy rainfall causing landslides
    in Dang, and unseasonal hail.
  • Soil: Low fertility, acidity in lateritic soils, zinc and phosphorus deficiency,
    and shallow depth on rocky slopes.
  • Socio-Economic: Small and marginal landholdings (average <1.5 hectares); poor road
    connectivity; limited market access; low adoption of improved varieties and
    technology; migration of youth to urban areas reducing farm labour.
  • Livestock: Free grazing by large cattle and goat populations degrades vegetation
    and causes soil compaction.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) — dedicated budget for tribal area development in agriculture,
    horticulture, and animal husbandry.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies for horticulture and vegetables.
  • MGNREGA — soil and water conservation structures (check dams, contour bunding,
    farm ponds, plantations) — very active in this zone.
  • National Food Security Mission (NFSM) — maize, rice, and pulses support.
  • National Horticulture Mission (NHM) — mango, cashew, and vegetable development.
  • Rashtriya Krishi Vikas Yojana (RKVY) — integrated farming and value chain support.
  • Vanbandhu Kalyan Yojana (Gujarat) — comprehensive tribal welfare scheme covering
    agriculture, education, health, and infrastructure.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for maize, paddy, tur, and cotton.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — free soil testing and amendment recommendations.
  • Forest Rights Act (FRA) — recognition of tribal rights over forest land for
    cultivation and NTFP collection.

9. DISTRICTS INCLUDED IN THIS ZONE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Dahod (entire district)
  • Chhota Udaipur (entire district)
  • Panchmahal (entire district — though western parts overlap with Middle Gujarat)
  • Mahisagar (entire district)
  • Narmada (entire district — overlaps with South Gujarat for canal-irrigated areas)
  • Tapi (entire district — overlaps with South Gujarat for alluvial plain areas)
  • Dang (entire district — entirely tribal, highest rainfall, most forested)
