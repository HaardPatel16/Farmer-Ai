================================================================================
MIDDLE GUJARAT AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Middle Gujarat is the agricultural heartland and industrial corridor of Gujarat.
It encompasses the districts of Ahmedabad, Vadodara, Anand, Kheda, Gandhinagar,
and Panchmahal. The zone lies between the Sabarmati, Mahi, and Narmada river basins,
benefiting from fertile alluvial and medium black soils. This is one of the most
prosperous and diversified agricultural zones in the state, with strong linkages
to dairy cooperatives (Amul, Dudh Sagar), tobacco processing, and cash crop
production. The terrain is predominantly flat to gently undulating, with some
hilly areas in Panchmahal.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: The zone receives moderate to good rainfall ranging from 750mm to
900mm annually. The monsoon arrives by June 15–20 and withdraws by late September.
Rainfall distribution is relatively more reliable than in Saurashtra, though
occasional dry spells in July–August can stress crops.

Temperature: Summer temperatures (April–June) range from 38°C to 44°C. Winter is mild
(10°C to 28°C). The zone experiences occasional cold waves in January, with minimum
temperatures dropping to 6–8°C in Ahmedabad and Gandhinagar.

Humidity: Moderate humidity (45–65%) during summer, rising to 70–85% during monsoon.
Post-monsoon humidity supports rabi crop establishment.

Drought/Flood Risk: Moderate drought risk in rainfed areas; however, the extensive
canal network mitigates drought impact significantly. Localized flooding occurs in
low-lying areas of Anand, Kheda, and Vadodara along riverbanks during heavy monsoon
years. Waterlogging in black cotton soil areas is a concern in wet years.

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Two dominant soil types characterize Middle Gujarat:
  • Goradu Soils (Alluvial loams) — Found in Ahmedabad, Gandhinagar, Kheda, and
    Anand. These are deep, well-drained, sandy loam to clay loam soils with good
    fertility. pH 7.5–8.0. High response to irrigation and fertiliser.
  • Medium Black Soils (Vertisols) — Found in Vadodara, Panchmahal, and parts of
    Anand. Clay content 35–45%, high water-holding capacity, but prone to
    waterlogging and cracking. pH 7.8–8.5. Low in organic matter (0.4–0.7%).

General fertility: Moderate to high in available potassium, moderate in phosphorus,
low in organic matter and zinc. Soil testing is widely practiced in this zone.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif Crops:
  • Tobacco — Anand and Kheda are the tobacco belt of Gujarat; Virginia and
    Natu varieties. Sowing: June–July.
  • Cotton — Bt cotton hybrids on medium black soils in Vadodara and Panchmahal.
  • Paddy (Rice) — HYV varieties like GR-11, Jaya, and Pusa-44 in irrigated areas
    of Anand, Kheda, and Vadodara.
  • Bajra — GHB-538 on lighter soils in Ahmedabad and Gandhinagar.
  • Castor and Sesame — mixed crops in Vadodara.

Rabi Crops:
  • Wheat — GW-273, GW-322, Lok-1 are major varieties; extensive cultivation in
    Ahmedabad, Gandhinagar, and Anand under irrigation.
  • Gram (Chickpea) — GG-1, GG-2 on medium black soils.
  • Cumin — high-value crop in Anand and Kheda.
  • Mustard — GM-1, Rai-5 in non-irrigated areas.

Year-Round / Cash Crops:
  • Dairy Fodder — bajra-neem, sorghum, and hybrid napier for the cooperative dairy
    sector. This is the Amul hinterland.
  • Vegetables — tomato, brinjal, chilli, and okra for Ahmedabad and Vadodara urban markets.
  • Banana — Bharuch border areas (part of this zone influence).

Recommended Rotations:
  • Tobacco – Wheat – Green Gram (3-crop system with irrigation)
  • Cotton – Wheat (on medium black soils)
  • Paddy – Wheat (double cropping with canal irrigation)
  • Bajra – Mustard (rainfed rotation)

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Middle Gujarat has the best irrigation infrastructure in the state:
  • Narmada Canal System — Sardar Sarovar canal commands large parts of Ahmedabad,
    Gandhinagar, Kheda, Anand, and Vadodara. Branch canals like Dora, Mahi, and
    Fatehpur canals distribute water.
  • Mahi River System — Direct irrigation and canal network in Kheda and Anand.
  • Sabarmati River — Ahmedabad and Gandhinagar depend on Sabarmati and groundwater.
  • Groundwater — Generally good quality and moderate depth (10–25m) in most of
the zone, though declining in Ahmedabad and Gandhinagar urban fringes.
  • Tanks and Reservoirs — Wanakbori, Meshwo, and other medium dams provide
    supplemental irrigation.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Kharif sowing begins June 15–July 10. Rabi sowing (wheat, gram)
October–November. Tobacco is transplanted in July–August. Paddy is transplanted
July–August.

Irrigation Methods: Canal irrigation is dominant. Drip and sprinkler irrigation
are rapidly expanding for tobacco, cotton, and vegetables. Flood irrigation is still
common for paddy and wheat but being discouraged.

Intercropping: Cotton + Green gram (1:1), Tobacco + Onion, and Castor + Groundnut
are practiced. Relay cropping of wheat into standing cotton is experimented with.

Special Practices: Tobacco requires precise fertiliser scheduling (heavy NPK with
micronutrients), topping and desuckering. Green manure (dhaincha) is commonly
incorporated before paddy transplanting. Zero-till wheat after paddy is expanding
in Kheda and Anand to save water and fuel.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Pests: Bollworm complex in cotton (despite Bt), tobacco caterpillar, aphids
    in mustard, brown plant hopper in paddy.
  • Diseases: Wilt in cotton and pigeon pea, blast in paddy, leaf curl in chilli,
    black shank in tobacco.
  • Weather: Untimely rains during tobacco curing (October–November) cause quality
    loss. Hailstorms in March damage wheat and mustard.
  • Soil: Waterlogging in black cotton soils during wet years; zinc deficiency in
    alluvial soils; declining organic matter due to intensive cropping.
  • Water: Canal water distribution inequalities; groundwater depletion in Ahmedabad
    urban fringe; industrial water competition.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Sardar Sarovar Canal Command Area Development — canal lining, water distribution.
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation for tobacco, cotton, and vegetables.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — extensive coverage in this zone.
  • National Food Security Mission (NFSM) — wheat and pulses.
  • National Horticulture Mission — banana, mango, and vegetable cultivation.
  • Gujarat Cooperative Milk Marketing Federation (GCMMF / Amul) — dairy support
    and fodder development.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for tobacco, cotton, paddy, and wheat.
  • Agricultural Export Zone (AEZ) — tobacco and cumin export support.

9. DISTRICTS INCLUDED IN THIS ZONE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Ahmedabad (entire district)
  • Vadodara (entire district)
  • Anand (entire district)
  • Kheda (entire district)
  • Gandhinagar (entire district)
  • Panchmahal (entire district, though hilly eastern parts overlap with Tribal Belt)
