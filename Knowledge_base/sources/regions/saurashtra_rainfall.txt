SAURASHTRA RAINFALL & CLIMATE PROFILE — COMPLETE REGIONAL PROFILE
================================================================================

1. REGION OVERVIEW & LOCATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Saurashtra is the extensive peninsula region of Gujarat, bounded by the Gulf of
Kutch to the north, the Arabian Sea to the west and south, and the Gulf of Khambhat
to the south-east. It encompasses the districts of Rajkot, Amreli, Junagadh, Gir
Somnath, Bhavnagar, Morbi, Jamnagar, Porbandar, Devbhoomi Dwarka, and Botad. The
region is a physiographic and cultural entity with a distinct agricultural identity
centred on groundnut and cotton. Rainfall is the single most important determinant
of agricultural output in Saurashtra, as the majority of cultivated area is rainfed.

2. CLIMATE & RAINFALL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall Pattern: Saurashtra receives annual rainfall ranging from 550mm to 750mm,
with significant spatial and temporal variation. The eastern districts (Bhavnagar,
Junagadh) receive higher rainfall (650–750mm), while the western districts (Jamnagar,
Devbhoomi Dwarka) receive lower rainfall (550–650mm). The monsoon arrives via the
Arabian Sea branch of the south-west monsoon, typically between June 10 and June 25.

Monsoon Distribution:
  • June: 15–20% of annual rainfall (pre-monsoon showers and onset)
  • July: 35–40% of annual rainfall (peak monsoon)
  • August: 25–30% of annual rainfall (critical for groundnut pod development)
  • September: 10–15% of annual rainfall (withdrawal phase, often erratic)

The coefficient of variability is 30–40%, which is among the highest in Gujarat.
This means that rainfall is highly unreliable, and farmers face significant
production risk every year.

Temperature: Summer (April–June) temperatures range from 38°C to 44°C, with coastal
areas (Porbandar, Devbhoomi Dwarka) moderated by sea breezes. Winter (December–
February) temperatures are 10°C to 28°C. The diurnal temperature range is large
(12–15°C).

Humidity: During monsoon, humidity ranges from 70% to 85%. Post-monsoon (October–
November), humidity drops rapidly to 40–55%. The pre-monsoon period (May–June) is
extremely dry, with humidity below 30%.

Drought/Flood Risk: Drought is the dominant climatic risk. The India Meteorological
Department (IMD) classifies Saurashtra as a "drought-prone" region. Farmers are
advised to store rainwater and use micro-irrigation for water conservation. Cyclonic
disturbances from the Arabian Sea can bring intense rainfall and storm surges in
coastal districts (September–November).

3. SOIL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rainfall variability interacts strongly with soil properties in Saurashtra:
  • Medium Black Cotton Soils — High water-holding capacity (150–180mm/m) helps
    buffer short dry spells but causes waterlogging in very wet years.
  • Sandy Loams — Low water-holding capacity (60–80mm/m) makes crops highly vulnerable
to even short dry spells. Quick drainage is an advantage in heavy rains.
  • Shallow Black Soils — Limited rooting depth (30–50cm) means crops exhaust
    available soil moisture rapidly.

Soil moisture recharge during monsoon is critical for both kharif crop survival
and any possibility of rabi cropping on residual moisture.

4. MAJOR CROPS & CROP ROTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kharif crops are entirely dependent on monsoon rainfall:
  • Groundnut — requires 500–600mm well-distributed rainfall. Most sensitive during
    flowering and pegging (July–August) and pod-filling (August–September).
  • Cotton — requires 600–700mm; more tolerant of dry spells than groundnut if
    established well. Bt cotton is increasingly irrigated via check dams.
  • Bajra — drought-escape crop; can survive on 350–450mm rainfall.
  • Sesame and Castor — mixed crops on marginal lands.

Rabi crops are extremely limited and depend on residual soil moisture plus one
or two supplemental irrigations from check dams.

5. WATER RESOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Saurashtra has no perennial rivers. Water resources are entirely dependent on:
  • Rainwater harvesting — check dams, farm ponds, percolation tanks, and village
    tanks. Over 100,000 water harvesting structures have been built in the past
two decades.
  • Groundwater — over-exploited in most districts. Water tables have declined by
    2–5 metres since the 1990s.
  • Sauni Yojana — the flagship Narmada water pipeline project that fills Saurashtra
    dams with Narmada water, providing a new and transformative irrigation source.
  • Lift irrigation — from seasonal rivers and check dams.

IMD advisories for Saurashtra farmers strongly emphasise rainwater storage and
micro-irrigation adoption as the primary drought-mitigation strategies.

6. AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sowing Windows: Strictly rainfall-dependent. Groundnut sowing should be completed
by July 20 for optimal yields. Delayed sowing beyond July 25 results in yield
penalties of 15–25% due to shortened pod-filling duration. Cotton sowing window is
wider (June 15 – July 10).

Rainwater Management: Farmers are advised to:
  • Construct farm ponds (minimum 20m x 20m x 3m) for water storage.
  • Adopt drip irrigation for all irrigated crops — it reduces water use by 40–60%.
  • Use mulching (organic or plastic) to reduce evaporation.
  • Practice contour farming and graded bunding to capture runoff.
  • Install check dams in seasonal streams.

Micro-Irrigation: The Gujarat Green Revolution Company (GGRC) and PM-KISAN have
driven massive adoption of drip and sprinkler systems. Over 60% of cotton area in
Saurashtra is now under drip irrigation.

7. COMMON CHALLENGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • Rainfall Variability: The single biggest challenge. A 20% deviation from normal
    rainfall can cause 30–40% yield loss in groundnut.
  • Mid-Season Dry Spells: "Pauna" (July–August breaks) are common and devastating
    for groundnut during pegging and pod-filling.
  • Terminal Drought: Early withdrawal of monsoon (before September 15) damages
    late-sown crops.
  • Cyclonic Disturbances: September–November cyclones can cause lodging in cotton,
    pod rot in groundnut, and storm surges in coastal areas.
  • Groundwater Depletion: Over-extraction for irrigation has lowered water tables,
    increasing pumping costs and causing quality deterioration in coastal areas.
  • Soil Moisture Stress: Even in normal rainfall years, poor distribution can cause
    moisture stress during critical crop growth stages.

8. GOVERNMENT SCHEMES APPLICABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  • PMKSY — micro-irrigation subsidies (55–90% for small and marginal farmers).
  • Sauni Yojana — Narmada water to Saurashtra dams via pipeline network.
  • Mukhya Mantri Sujalam Sufalam Jal Sanchay Yojana — water harvesting support.
  • PMFBY — crop insurance for groundnut, cotton, and bajra.
  • Soil Health Card Scheme — free soil testing and fertiliser recommendations.
  • National Food Security Mission (NFSM) — oilseeds and pulses support.
  • Gujarat State Disaster Management Authority — drought relief and contingency
    planning.
  • IMD Agromet Advisory Services — block-level weather forecasts and advisories.
