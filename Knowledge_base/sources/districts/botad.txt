================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - BOTAD DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Botad is a relatively new district in Gujarat, carved out of Bhavnagar district
in 2013. It is located in the central part of the Saurashtra peninsula. The
district covers approximately 2,500 sq km with a population of about 700,000. It
comprises 4 talukas: Botad, Gadhada, Barwala, and Ranpur. Botad is known for
its agricultural economy and the famous Panjrapol (animal shelter) tradition.
The district is rapidly developing its infrastructure and agriculture is the
mainstay of the economy.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Botad falls in the Central Saurashtra Agro-Climatic Zone. The climate is
semi-arid tropical with hot summers and mild winters. The district receives
low to moderate annual rainfall of 550-650 mm, with the monsoon concentrated
in July and August. The average maximum temperature ranges from 30°C in January
to 43°C in May, while minimum temperatures range from 10°C in winter to 27°C in
summer. Relative humidity peaks at 70-75% during the monsoon and drops to 20-25%
in summer. The district is drought-prone and experiences frequent heatwaves.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The dominant soil types are:
• Medium black soil (Vertisols) covering 70% of the district
• Sandy loam in some western areas
• Saline-alkali patches in low-lying areas near the Little Rann of Kutch

Soil texture is predominantly clayey to clay-loam. Soil pH ranges from 7.5 to
8.5, generally neutral to mildly alkaline. Fertility status is moderate but
declining in intensively cultivated areas. Organic carbon content is low to
medium (0.3-0.5%). Some areas show zinc and sulphur deficiency.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 550-650 mm (low, erratic, drought-prone)
Irrigation sources:
• Borewells and tubewells: Primary source; depths 80-180 metres
• Check dams and farm ponds: Extensive construction under watershed programmes
• Village ponds for supplementary irrigation
• Narmada water: Saurashtra Branch Canal reaches parts of the district

Water issues: Botad is one of the more water-scarce districts in Gujarat.
Groundwater depletion is significant, with water tables falling 10-20 metres in
the last two decades. The Narmada canal has improved irrigation security in
command areas but coverage is limited. Drought is a recurring problem.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Cotton - 50,000 ha, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21, Bt hybrids
• Groundnut - 40,000 ha, varieties: GG-20, GG-22, GG-7
• Bajra (Pearl millet) - 30,000 ha, varieties: GHB-558, GHB-732
• Castor - 15,000 ha, varieties: GCH-7, GCH-9
• Sesame (Til) - 10,000 ha, varieties: G Til-3, G Til-10

RABI (November-March):
• Wheat - 30,000 ha, varieties: GW-366, GW-496, Lok-1
• Gram (Chickpea) - 15,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7
• Cumin - 10,000 ha, varieties: GC-1, GC-4
• Mustard - 8,000 ha, varieties: GJM-2, GJM-3

SUMMER (March-May):
• Vegetables: Limited irrigated pockets - Tomato, Brinjal, Okra
• Fodder: Hybrid Napier, Jowar for dairy

Productivity: Cotton 2.5-3.5 quintals/ha lint; Groundnut 12-18 quintals/ha pods;
Wheat 4.0-5.0 tonnes/ha (irrigated); Cumin 4-6 quintals/ha.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli in irrigated areas
• Fruits: Mango (Kesar), Guava in small orchards
• Fodder crops: Hybrid Napier, Jowar, Lucerne for dairy cattle

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds (Gir, local) and crossbred cattle
• Buffalo: Surti and Mehsani breeds; dairy is important in irrigated villages
• Poultry: Growing backyard and small commercial farms
• Sheep and Goats: Significant population in semi-arid areas
• Fodder availability: Moderate in irrigated areas; rainfed areas depend on
  crop residues. Fodder shortage is severe in summer and drought years.

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Spotted bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Groundnut: Late leaf spot (Tikka), Early leaf spot, Tobacco caterpillar,
  Jassids, Thrips
• Bajra: Downy mildew (Green ear), Ergot, Smut, Shoot fly
• Castor: Semi-looper (Achaea janata), Capsule borer, Leaf miner
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids
• Gram: Pod borer (Helicoverpa), Wilt (Fusarium), Ascochyta blight

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Drought and water scarcity - the most critical challenge
• Groundwater depletion in intensively cultivated areas
• Erratic monsoon affecting crop establishment and yields
• Declining soil fertility in continuous cotton-groundnut areas
• Labour shortage during peak sowing and harvesting seasons
• Limited market infrastructure and cold storage facilities
• Climate change increasing temperature extremes and monsoon variability

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Botad or Bhavnagar KVK serving the area
• ATMA: Active extension programmes in all talukas
• Soil Testing Labs: Mobile soil testing vans; district lab at Bhavnagar
• APMCs: Botad, Gadhada, Barwala, Ranpur
• Check Dam Schemes: Government has built check dams under Sujalam Sufalam
• Narmada Canal: Saurashtra Branch Canal expanding coverage
• Drought Relief: Special drought relief packages during severe drought years

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Prioritize water conservation: adopt drip irrigation for cotton and groundnut
  to save 40-50% water and improve yields
• Practice crop rotation (cotton-groundnut-bajra) to reduce soil-borne diseases
  and improve soil health
• Use seed treatment with thiram + carbendazim for groundnut and cotton to
  prevent seedling diseases
• Install pheromone traps for pink bollworm monitoring from day 30 after cotton
  sowing; spray only at economic threshold
• Grow low-water crops like cumin, castor, and sesame in water-scarce areas
  instead of water-intensive cotton
• Construct farm ponds and check dams for rainwater harvesting and groundwater
  recharge
• Apply gypsum @ 500 kg/ha in groundnut at flowering for pod development
• Grow fodder crops (hybrid Napier, Jowar) on 10-15% of land for dairy support
• Practice deep summer ploughing (May) to expose soil pests and pathogens to
  solar heat
• Use soil test-based fertilization; address zinc deficiency with ZnSO4 @ 25 kg/ha
