================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - DEVBHOOMI DWARKA DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Devbhoomi Dwarka is a coastal district in the western part of Gujarat, carved
out of Jamnagar district in 2013. It is located on the Arabian Sea coast and
is famous for the Dwarka temple, one of the Char Dham pilgrimage sites. The
district covers approximately 5,400 sq km with a population of about 750,000.
It comprises 4 talukas: Dwarka, Khambhalia, Kalyanpur, and Bhanvad. The district
is predominantly rural with agriculture and fishing as the main livelihoods.
The coastal location influences the climate and agriculture significantly.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Devbhoomi Dwarka falls in the North Saurashtra Coastal Agro-Climatic Zone. The
climate is tropical semi-arid with maritime influence. The district receives low
annual rainfall of 500-600 mm, with the monsoon concentrated in July and August.
The average maximum temperature ranges from 28°C in January to 40°C in May, while
minimum temperatures range from 10°C in winter to 26°C in summer. Relative humidity
is high (75-85%) during the monsoon and moderate (50-60%) in winter due to
maritime influence. The coastal climate moderates extreme temperatures compared
to inland Saurashtra. The district is prone to cyclonic storms from the Arabian Sea.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The dominant soil types are:
• Coastal sandy soils covering 50% of the district near the coast
• Medium black soil (Vertisols) in inland talukas (Khambhalia, Bhanvad)
• Saline-alkali soils in low-lying coastal areas and near the Rann of Kutch
• Alluvial soils along seasonal rivers and streams

Soil texture is predominantly sandy near the coast and clayey to clay-loam inland.
Soil pH ranges from 7.5 to 9.0, with coastal areas being more alkaline. Fertility
status is low in sandy coastal soils but moderate in inland black soils. Organic
carbon content is low (0.2-0.4%) in sandy areas. Coastal soils require gypsum
and organic amendments for sustainable cultivation.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 500-600 mm (low, erratic, drought-prone)
Irrigation sources:
• Borewells and tubewells: Primary source; depths 100-250 metres
• Check dams and farm ponds: Limited construction on seasonal streams
• Village ponds for supplementary irrigation
• Narmada water: Saurashtra Branch Canal reaches some parts of the district
• Bhadar and Ozat rivers: Seasonal rivers providing limited water

Water issues: Groundwater is the main source but faces depletion and salinity.
Coastal areas have saline groundwater due to sea water ingress. Inland areas have
better groundwater but face over-extraction. The Narmada canal has improved
irrigation in some areas but coverage is limited. Water scarcity is a major
constraint for agriculture.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Groundnut - 70,000 ha, varieties: GG-20, GG-22, GG-7, TMV-2
• Cotton - 40,000 ha, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21, Bt hybrids
• Bajra (Pearl millet) - 30,000 ha, varieties: GHB-558, GHB-732
• Onion - 15,000 ha, varieties: Bhima Shakti, N-53
• Castor - 10,000 ha, varieties: GCH-7, GCH-9

RABI (November-March):
• Wheat - 30,000 ha, varieties: GW-366, GW-496, Lok-1
• Gram (Chickpea) - 15,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7
• Cumin - 10,000 ha, varieties: GC-1, GC-4
• Mustard - 8,000 ha, varieties: GJM-2, GJM-3
• Onion - 10,000 ha (Rabi onion)

SUMMER (March-May):
• Vegetables: Tomato, Brinjal, Okra, Chilli in irrigated pockets
• Fodder: Hybrid Napier, Jowar for dairy

Productivity: Groundnut 12-18 quintals/ha pods; Cotton 2.5-3.5 quintals/ha lint;
Wheat 4.0-5.0 tonnes/ha (irrigated); Onion 200-300 quintals/ha; Cumin 4-6 quintals/ha.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Onion: Important cash crop for local and distant markets
• Coconut: Coastal areas have coconut plantations
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli in irrigated areas
• Fruits: Mango (Kesar), Guava in small orchards
• Fodder crops: Hybrid Napier, Jowar, Lucerne for dairy cattle

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds (Gir, local) and crossbred cattle
• Buffalo: Surti and Mehsani breeds; dairy is important
• Poultry: Backyard poultry and small commercial farms
• Sheep and Goats: Significant population; rearing is common in drought-prone areas
• Fishing: Important coastal livelihood alongside agriculture
• Fodder availability: Moderate in irrigated areas; rainfed areas depend on crop
  residues. Fodder shortage is acute in summer and drought years.

8. CROP CALENDAR
----------------

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Groundnut: Late leaf spot (Tikka), Early leaf spot, Tobacco caterpillar,
  Jassids, Thrips (PBNV vector)
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Spotted bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Onion: Thrips, Purple blotch (Alternaria porri), Stemphylium leaf blight
• Bajra: Downy mildew (Green ear), Ergot, Smut, Shoot fly
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids
• Castor: Semi-looper (Achaea janata), Capsule borer

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Coastal salinity ingress affecting groundwater and soils
• Drought and erratic rainfall affecting crop establishment
• Groundwater depletion in inland areas
• Limited irrigation infrastructure
• Cyclone risk from Arabian Sea affecting coastal agriculture
• Declining soil fertility in intensively cultivated areas
• Labour shortage during peak sowing and harvesting seasons
• Limited market infrastructure and cold storage facilities
• Soil salinity in low-lying areas near the coast and Rann

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Devbhoomi Dwarka or Jamnagar KVK serving the area
• ATMA: Active extension programmes in all talukas
• Soil Testing Labs: Mobile soil testing vans; district lab at Jamnagar
• APMCs: Dwarka, Khambhalia, Kalyanpur, Bhanvad
• Check Dam Schemes: Government has built check dams under Sujalam Sufalam
• Narmada Canal: Saurashtra Branch Canal expanding coverage
• Coastal Salinity Management: Schemes for gypsum application and salt-tolerant
  crop promotion
• Fisheries Department: Support for fishing communities alongside agriculture

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• In coastal saline areas, apply gypsum @ 2-3 tonnes/ha and grow salt-tolerant
  crops like barley, cumin, date palm, or coconut initially
• Adopt drip irrigation for cotton and onion to save water and increase yields
• Practice crop rotation (groundnut-cotton-bajra) to reduce soil-borne diseases
  and improve soil health
• Use seed treatment with thiram + carbendazim for groundnut and cotton to
  prevent seedling diseases
• Install pheromone traps for pink bollworm monitoring from day 30 after cotton
  sowing; spray only at economic threshold
• Grow fodder crops (hybrid Napier, Jowar) on 10-15% of land for dairy support
• Apply gypsum @ 500 kg/ha in groundnut at flowering for pod development
• Practice summer ploughing (May) to expose soil-borne pests and pathogens to
  solar heat
• Construct farm ponds and check dams for rainwater harvesting and groundwater
  recharge
• Use soil test-based fertilizers; address zinc deficiency with ZnSO4 @ 25 kg/ha
