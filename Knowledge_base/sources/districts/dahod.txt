================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - DAHOD DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Dahod is a predominantly tribal district in eastern Gujarat, located on the
Gujarat-Madhya Pradesh border. It is part of the Panchmahal hills and is one
of the most backward districts in Gujarat. The district covers approximately
3,600 sq km with a population of about 1.0 million, of which over 60% belongs
to tribal communities (mainly Bhil, Bhilala, and Patelia). It comprises 7
talukas: Dahod, Devgadhbaria, Dhanpur, Fatepura, Garbada, Limkheda, and
Sanjeli. The district is characterized by undulating terrain, scattered forests,
and a predominantly subsistence agricultural economy.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Dahod falls in the Middle Gujarat Agro-Climatic Zone (tribal belt). The climate
is sub-humid tropical with hot summers and mild winters. The district receives
good annual rainfall of 900-1000 mm, well-distributed from June to September.
The average maximum temperature ranges from 28°C in January to 43°C in May,
while minimum temperatures range from 10°C in winter to 26°C in summer. Relative
humidity is high (75-85%) during the monsoon and moderate (40-50%) in winter.
The undulating terrain creates local variations in rainfall and temperature.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The district has diverse soil types:
• Lateritic and medium black soil in undulating terrain (50% of district)
• Red and mixed red-black soils in foothill areas
• Medium black soil in river valleys and lowlands (30% of district)
• Shallow rocky soils on hill slopes
• Alluvial soils along the Panam and Mahi rivers

Soil texture varies from sandy loam to clay-loam. Soil pH ranges from 6.5 to 8.0,
with lateritic soils being slightly acidic and valley soils neutral to mildly
alkaline. Fertility status is low to moderate in undulating areas due to soil
erosion, while valleys have moderate fertility. Organic carbon is low (0.3-0.5%).
Zinc and boron deficiency is common in lateritic soils.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 900-1000 mm (good, well-distributed)
Irrigation sources:
• Kadana dam and Panam river: Provide limited canal irrigation to some areas
• Medium and minor irrigation projects (check dams, percolation tanks)
• Tubewells in valleys and plains; depths 40-100 metres
• Traditional water harvesting: Farm ponds, katas, and bori bands
• Mahi river: Provides seasonal surface water in some talukas

Water issues: The district is predominantly rainfed with only 20-25% area under
irrigation. Water scarcity is acute in summer. Soil erosion on undulating terrain
reduces water retention. The undulating terrain makes canal irrigation difficult.
Kadana dam provides some relief but coverage is limited. Watershed development
is a critical need.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Maize - 60,000 ha, varieties: Ganga-5, Ganga-11, GM-6, African Tall
• Paddy (Rice) - 30,000 ha in valleys, varieties: GR-11, GR-12, Gurjari
• Cotton - 25,000 ha in plains, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21
• Tur (Pigeon pea) - 20,000 ha, varieties: GTP-82, GT-101
• Soybean - 15,000 ha, varieties: Gujarat Soybean-1, JS-335
• Bajra (Pearl millet) - 15,000 ha, varieties: GHB-558, GHB-732

RABI (November-March):
• Wheat - 25,000 ha (irrigated areas), varieties: GW-366, GW-496, Lok-1
• Gram (Chickpea) - 20,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7
• Mustard - 10,000 ha, varieties: GJM-2, GJM-3
• Vegetables: Tomato, Brinjal, Okra in irrigated pockets

SUMMER (March-May):
• Vegetables: Limited irrigation in valleys
• Fodder: Jowar, Maize for green fodder

Productivity: Maize 15-20 quintals/ha (rainfed); Paddy 4.5-6.0 tonnes/ha;
Wheat 4.0-5.5 tonnes/ha (irrigated); Cotton 2.0-3.0 quintals/ha lint;
Soybean 12-15 quintals/ha.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Mango: Local and Kesar varieties in home gardens and small orchards
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli in valley areas
• Fruits: Guava, papaya, custard apple (seetaphal), banana
• Spices: Turmeric and ginger in limited areas with good rainfall
• Forest produce: Mahua flowers, tendu leaves, medicinal plants from forests

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds (Gir, local) and some crossbred cattle
• Buffalo: Local buffalo breeds for milk and draught
• Goats and Sheep: Large population in tribal areas; important for livelihoods
• Poultry: Backyard poultry is common in tribal villages
• Pigs: Reared by some tribal communities
• Fodder availability: Limited in rainfed areas; farmers depend on forest grazing
  and crop residues. Fodder shortage is severe in summer months.

8. CROP CALENDAR
----------------
Month          | Major Activities
---------------|-----------------------------------------------
June           | Maize sowing, Paddy nursery, Cotton sowing (plains)
July           | Maize/cotton sowing continues, Tur sowing, Soybean sowing

April-May      | Summer vegetables, Fodder, Land preparation

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Maize: Stem borer, Fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda), Leaf blight
• Paddy: Stem borer, Brown plant hopper (BPH), Leaf folder, Blast
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Tur: Pod borer (Helicoverpa), Wilt (Fusarium), Phytophthora blight
• Soybean: Stem fly, Green semilooper, Girdle beetle, Yellow mosaic virus
• Bajra: Downy mildew (Green ear), Ergot, Smut, Shoot fly
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Predominantly rainfed agriculture (70-75% area)
• Soil erosion on undulating terrain
• Low productivity due to traditional farming practices and limited input use
• Limited market access and infrastructure in remote villages
• Poor road connectivity affecting agricultural marketing
• Water scarcity in summer and drought-prone years
• Shallow and rocky soils limiting crop choices in some areas
• Limited access to improved seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides in remote areas
• Tribal farmers' limited access to credit and formal markets

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Dahod (affiliated with Gujarat Agricultural
  University) - provides tribal-specific training and demonstrations
• ATMA: Tribal area programmes with special focus on sustainable agriculture
• Watershed Development: IWMP (Integrated Watershed Management Programme)
  active in all talukas
• Soil Testing: Mobile soil testing vans visit periodically
• APMCs: Dahod, Devgadhbaria, Limkheda, Garbada
• Tribal Development: ITDP (Integrated Tribal Development Programme) schemes
  for input support, market linkage, and infrastructure
• Vanbandhu Kalyan Yojana: Special scheme for tribal welfare and livelihood
  improvement
• MGNREGA: Active for watershed works, farm ponds, and soil conservation

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Adopt contour farming, bunding, and terracing on slopes to reduce soil erosion
  and conserve water
• Grow maize and tur as intercropping to improve land use efficiency and reduce
  risk from mono-cropping
• Plant agroforestry trees (Neem, Mahua, Amla) on field boundaries for shade,
  fodder, and additional income
• Use improved seed varieties of maize and paddy for higher yields; treat seeds
  with thiram + carbendazim before sowing
• In irrigated areas, practice crop rotation of cotton-wheat or paddy-wheat to
  maintain soil health
• Collect and compost crop residues to improve soil organic matter rather than
  burning
• Harvest rainwater through farm ponds, check dams, and percolation tanks;
  the government provides subsidies for water harvesting structures
• Use FYM (5-10 tonnes/ha) and vermicompost to improve soil fertility in
  low-fertility areas
• Practice mixed farming (crops + livestock + forest produce) for better risk
  management and household income stability
• Form Self-Help Groups (SHGs) and Farmer Producer Organizations (FPOs) for
  collective marketing and input procurement
• Apply soil test-based fertilizers; address zinc deficiency with ZnSO4 @ 25 kg/ha
  in deficient soils
