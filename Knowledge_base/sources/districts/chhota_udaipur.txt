================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - CHHOTA UDAIPUR DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Chhota Udaipur is a predominantly tribal district in eastern Gujarat, carved
out of Vadodara district in 2013. It is located on the Gujarat-Madhya Pradesh
border, nestled in the Vindhya and Satpura hill ranges. The district covers
approximately 3,500 sq km with a population of about 1.1 million, of which over
70% belongs to tribal communities (mainly Rathwa, Nayak, Bhil). It comprises
6 talukas: Chhota Udaipur, Kavant, Nasvadi, Jetpur Pavi, Sankheda, and Bodeli.
The district is characterized by hilly terrain, dense forests, and a rich
tribal cultural heritage.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Chhota Udaipur falls in the Middle Gujarat Agro-Climatic Zone (tribal hill belt).
The climate ranges from sub-humid in the valleys to humid in the hills. The
district receives good annual rainfall of 900-1100 mm, among the higher rainfall
areas in Gujarat. The monsoon is well-distributed from June to September. The
average maximum temperature ranges from 28°C in January to 40°C in May, while
minimum temperatures range from 10°C in winter to 25°C in summer. Relative
humidity is high (75-85%) during the monsoon and moderate (40-50%) in winter.
The hills create local microclimates with cooler temperatures and higher rainfall.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The dominant soil types are:
• Lateritic soils in hilly and undulating terrain (60% of district)
• Medium black soil in river valleys and lowlands
• Red and mixed red-black soils in foothill areas
• Shallow rocky soils on hill slopes and forest margins
• Alluvial soils along the Orsang and Kabini rivers

Soil texture varies from sandy loam in valleys to clay-loam in lowlands. Soil pH
ranges from 6.5 to 7.8, with lateritic soils being slightly acidic and valley
soils neutral. Fertility status is low to moderate in hilly areas due to soil
erosion and leaching, while valleys have moderate fertility. Organic carbon is
low (0.2-0.4%) in hill soils due to rapid decomposition. Zinc and boron
deficiency is common in lateritic soils.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 900-1100 mm (good, well-distributed)
Irrigation sources:
• Orsang and Kabini rivers: Provide seasonal surface water
• Medium and minor irrigation projects (check dams, percolation tanks, weirs)
• Tubewells in plains and valleys (Bodeli, Sankheda); depths 30-80 metres
• Traditional water harvesting: Katas, bori bands, and farm ponds
• Narmada canal: Limited reach into the district

Water issues: The hilly talukas (Kavant, Nasvadi, Jetpur Pavi) are predominantly
rainfed with limited irrigation. Water scarcity is acute in summer. Soil erosion
on hill slopes reduces water retention. The undulating terrain makes canal
irrigation difficult. Watershed development is critical for sustainable agriculture.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Maize - 50,000 ha, varieties: Ganga-5, Ganga-11, GM-6, African Tall
• Paddy (Rice) - 40,000 ha in valleys and lowlands, varieties: GR-11, GR-12
• Cotton - 30,000 ha in plains, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21
• Tur (Pigeon pea) - 20,000 ha, varieties: GTP-82, GT-101
• Finger millet (Ragi) - 10,000 ha, traditional tribal crop

RABI (November-March):
• Wheat - 20,000 ha (irrigated valleys), varieties: GW-366, GW-496
• Gram (Chickpea) - 15,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7
• Mustard - 8,000 ha, varieties: GJM-2, GJM-3
• Vegetables: Tomato, Brinjal, Okra in irrigated pockets

SUMMER (March-May):
• Vegetables: Limited irrigation in valleys
• Fodder: Jowar, Maize for green fodder

Productivity: Maize 15-20 quintals/ha (rainfed); Paddy 4.5-6.0 tonnes/ha;
Wheat 4.0-5.5 tonnes/ha (irrigated); Cotton 2.0-3.0 quintals/ha lint.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Mango: Local and Kesar varieties in home gardens and small orchards
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli in valley areas
• Fruits: Guava, papaya, custard apple (seetaphal), jackfruit
• Spices: Turmeric and ginger in limited hilly areas with good rainfall
• Forest produce: Mahua flowers, tendu leaves, medicinal plants, bamboo
• The district is famous for its lac cultivation on ber trees (Kusumi lac)

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds (Gir, local hill breeds) and some crossbred cattle
• Buffalo: Local buffalo breeds for milk and draught
• Goats and Sheep: Large population in tribal hilly areas; integral to tribal
  livelihoods and culture
• Poultry: Backyard poultry is common in tribal villages
• Pigs: Reared by some tribal communities
• Fodder availability: Limited in rainfed areas; farmers depend on forest
  grazing and crop residues. Fodder shortage is severe in summer months.

8. CROP CALENDAR
----------------

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Maize: Stem borer, Fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda), Leaf blight,
  Turcicum blight
• Paddy: Stem borer, Brown plant hopper (BPH), Leaf folder, Blast
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Tur: Pod borer (Helicoverpa), Wilt (Fusarium), Phytophthora blight
• Finger millet: Blast, Neck blast, Stem borer
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Predominantly rainfed agriculture in hilly talukas (60-70% area)
• Soil erosion on hill slopes and deforestation
• Low productivity due to traditional farming practices and limited input use
• Limited market access and infrastructure in remote tribal villages
• Poor road connectivity affecting agricultural marketing and input delivery
• Water scarcity in summer and drought-prone years
• Shallow and rocky soils limiting crop choices in hilly areas
• Limited access to improved seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides in remote areas
• Human-wildlife conflict due to proximity to forests

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Chhota Udaipur (affiliated with Gujarat
  Agricultural University) - provides tribal-specific training and demonstrations
• ATMA: Tribal area programmes with special focus on sustainable agriculture
• Watershed Development: IWMP (Integrated Watershed Management Programme)
  active in all hilly talukas
• Soil Testing: Mobile soil testing vans visit tribal areas periodically
• APMCs: Chhota Udaipur, Bodeli, Sankheda
• Tribal Development: ITDP (Integrated Tribal Development Programme) schemes
  for input support, market linkage, and infrastructure
• Forest Department: Joint Forest Management (JFM) for sustainable NTFP
  collection and agroforestry
• Vanbandhu Kalyan Yojana: Special scheme for tribal welfare and livelihood
  improvement

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Adopt contour farming, bunding, and terracing on hill slopes to reduce soil
  erosion and conserve water
• Grow maize and tur as intercropping to improve land use efficiency and
  reduce risk from mono-cropping
• Plant agroforestry trees (Neem, Mahua, Amla, Bamboo) on field boundaries to
  provide shade, fodder, and additional income
• Use improved seed varieties of maize and paddy for higher yields; treat seeds
  with thiram + carbendazim before sowing
• In irrigated valleys, practice crop rotation of cotton-wheat or paddy-wheat
  to maintain soil health
• Collect and compost crop residues to improve soil organic matter rather than
  burning
• Harvest rainwater through farm ponds, check dams, and percolation tanks;
  the government provides subsidies for water harvesting structures
• Use FYM (5-10 tonnes/ha) and vermicompost to improve soil fertility in
  low-fertility hill soils
• Practice mixed farming (crops + livestock + forest produce) for better
  risk management and household income stability
• Tribal farmers should form Self-Help Groups (SHGs) and Farmer Producer
  Organizations (FPOs) for collective marketing of lac, mahua, and turmeric
