FARMER SAHAYAK - BANASKANTHA DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Banaskantha is the largest district in North Gujarat, covering approximately
10,700 sq km with a population of over 3 million. It is bordered by Rajasthan
to the north and northeast. The district has 14 talukas: Palanpur, Deesa,
Dhanera, Tharad, Vav, Bhabhar, Vadgam, Kankrej, Lakhani, Deodar, Amirgadh,
Danta, Suigam, and Sami. The district is a major agricultural powerhouse and
is known as the "Potato Bowl of Gujarat" and the "Spice Capital of Gujarat"
due to its massive cumin and fennel production. The terrain is mostly flat to
undulating, with some hilly areas in the eastern Danta taluka bordering the
Aravalli hills.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Banaskantha falls in the North Gujarat Agro-Climatic Zone. The climate is
arid to semi-arid tropical. The district receives low to moderate annual rainfall
of 500-600 mm, with the monsoon highly erratic and often insufficient.
The average maximum temperature ranges from 28°C in January to 45°C in May,
while minimum temperatures range from 6°C in winter to 28°C in summer.
Relative humidity is very low in summer (15-25%) and moderate during the
monsoon (60-70%). The district is highly drought-prone and experiences
frequent heatwaves in May-June.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The dominant soil types are:
• Sandy loam to light sandy soils covering 70% of the district
• Medium black soil in some western and southern talukas
• Alluvial soils along the Banas river and its tributaries
• Saline-alkali soils in low-lying areas and near the Rann of Kutch border

Soil texture is predominantly light and sandy, with poor water-holding capacity.
Soil pH ranges from 7.5 to 9.0, often alkaline. Fertility status is low to
moderate; the sandy soils are deficient in organic matter, nitrogen, and zinc.
Deep black soils in some talukas have better fertility but are limited in extent.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 500-600 mm (low, erratic, drought-prone)
Irrigation sources:
• Borewells and tubewells: Primary source; depths now 150-350 metres in many
  areas due to severe groundwater depletion
• Dantiwada dam: Serves limited command areas in Dantiwada and Deesa talukas
• Narmada canal (Banaskantha-Mehsana branch): Reaches parts of Deesa, Palanpur,
  Dhanera, Tharad; transforming agriculture in command areas
• Check dams and farm ponds: Government has constructed thousands of check dams
  under Sujalam Sufalam and Jaldhara schemes
• Rainwater harvesting: Farm ponds, rooftop harvesting, and percolation tanks

Water issues: Banaskantha faces the most serious water crisis in Gujarat.
The water table has fallen 30-60 metres in the last 25 years. Many deep
borewells are dry. The Narmada canal has been a game-changer for command areas.
Saline groundwater is found in shallow aquifers near the Rann border.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Bajra (Pearl millet) - 1,50,000 ha, varieties: GHB-558, GHB-732, RCB-2
• Castor - 60,000 ha, varieties: GCH-7, GCH-9, GCH-11
• Cotton - 50,000 ha, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21, Bt hybrids
• Groundnut - 40,000 ha, varieties: GG-20, GG-22
• Guar (Cluster bean) - 25,000 ha, varieties: Guar-1, Guar-2

RABI (November-March):
• Potato - 1,20,000 ha (largest in Gujarat), varieties: Kufri Jyoti,
  Kufri Chandramukhi, Kufri Badshah
• Cumin - 70,000 ha, varieties: GC-1, GC-4, Gujarat Cumin-1
• Fennel - 35,000 ha, varieties: GF-1, GF-2, Gujarat Fennel-1
• Wheat - 60,000 ha, varieties: GW-366, GW-496, Lok-1
• Mustard - 20,000 ha, varieties: GJM-2, GJM-3
• Gram (Chickpea) - 25,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7

SUMMER (March-May):
• Isabgol (Psyllium) - 15,000 ha, varieties: Gujarat Isabgol-1, GI-2
• Watermelon, Muskmelon - in irrigated pockets
• Vegetables: Chilli, Tomato, Okra

Productivity: Potato 250-350 quintals/ha; Cumin 4-6 quintals/ha;
Fennel 8-12 quintals/ha; Wheat 4.5-5.5 tonnes/ha (irrigated); Castor
10-15 quintals/ha.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Potato: The dominant horticultural crop; Deesa, Palanpur, Dhanera are the
  main potato belts supplying markets across India
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli grown in irrigated areas for
  local markets
• Fruits: Ber (Ziziphus), Pomegranate, Guava in some talukas
• Spices: Cumin and fennel are the economic backbone of the district
• Medicinal: Isabgol is a major export-oriented crop

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds and crossbred; dairy is important
• Buffalo: Mehsani breed is native to the region; highly valued for milk
• Sheep and Goats: Significant population; rearing is common in drought-prone
  areas as an alternative livelihood
• Camel: Traditional draught animal in some areas near the Rann
• Poultry: Growing commercial poultry farms
• Fodder availability: Very limited; farmers depend on crop residues and
  purchased fodder. Fodder shortage is critical in summer and drought years.

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Potato: Late blight (Phytophthora infestans), Aphids (PVY/PLRV vectors),
  Cutworm, Jassids, Tuber moth
• Cumin: Cumin blight (Alternaria burnsii), Aphids, Powdery mildew, Wilt
• Fennel: Alternaria blight, Aphids, Powdery mildew
• Bajra: Downy mildew (Green ear), Ergot, Smut, Shoot fly
• Castor: Semi-looper (Achaea janata), Capsule borer, Leaf miner
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids
• Isabgol: Root rot, Powdery mildew, Aphids

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Severe groundwater depletion; water table declining 1-2 metres per year
in some talukas
• Drought and erratic rainfall affecting Kharif crop establishment
• Soil salinity and alkalinity in low-lying areas and near the Rann
• High input costs for deep borewell irrigation (electricity, pump maintenance)
• Labour shortage during potato harvesting and sowing season
• Declining soil organic matter in intensively cultivated areas
• Market price volatility for spices (cumin, fennel)
• Climate change increasing temperature extremes and monsoon variability

----------------------
• KVK: KVK Dantiwada (affiliated with SDAU, Sardarkrushinagar) - premier
  KVK for potato and spice research; phone: 02748-278219
• ATMA: Active in all talukas with farmer field schools
• Soil Testing Labs: District soil testing lab at Palanpur; mobile vans
• APMCs: Palanpur (main), Deesa, Dhanera, Tharad, Vav, Bhabhar, Kankrej
• Cold Storages: Extensive cold storage network for potato (Deesa, Palanpur,

• Narmada Canal: SSNNL branch canals expanding coverage annually
• Micro-irrigation: GGRC (Gujarat Green Revolution Company) provides 50-70%
  subsidy on drip and sprinkler systems; Banaskantha has the highest adoption
  rate in Gujarat
• Warehouses: NABARD-supported rural godowns for spice storage

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Potato farmers must practice strict crop rotation (potato should not be
  grown on the same field for more than 1 year in 3); rotate with cumin,
  fennel, or wheat to reduce disease and nematode buildup
• Adopt drip irrigation for potato, cumin, and fennel to save 40-50% water
  and increase yields by 20-30%
• In saline-alkali patches, apply gypsum @ 2-3 tonnes/ha and grow salt-tolerant
  crops like barley, mustard, or ber initially
• Use certified potato seed from KVK Dantiwada or government seed farms to
  reduce viral disease transmission
• Cumin and fennel farmers should treat seeds with thiram + carbendazim
  before sowing and spray preventive fungicides at flowering stage
• Maintain soil health through regular application of FYM, compost, and
  biofertilizers (Azotobacter, PSB)
• Install pheromone traps for potato tuber moth and pink bollworm monitoring
• Practice deep summer ploughing to expose soil pests and pathogens to solar
  heat (May-June)
• Use mulch (paddy straw, wheat straw) to conserve soil moisture and reduce
  weed growth in spice crops
• Join farmer producer organizations (FPOs) for collective marketing of spices
  to get better prices and access to export markets
