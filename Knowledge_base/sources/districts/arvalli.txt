================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - ARVALLI DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Arvalli is a relatively new district in North Gujarat, carved out of
Sabarkantha district in 2013. It is located in the eastern part of North Gujarat
bordering Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh. The district covers approximately
3,200 sq km with a population of about 1.0 million. It comprises 6 talukas:
Modasa, Bhiloda, Bayad, Malpur, Meghraj, and Dhansura. The district is
predominantly tribal, with a significant Bhil and Rathwa tribal population
in the eastern hilly talukas. Agriculture is mostly rainfed and subsistence-oriented
in the hilly areas, while the western plains have some irrigation.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Arvalli falls in the North Gujarat Agro-Climatic Zone. The climate ranges from
semi-arid in the western plains to sub-humid in the eastern hills. The district
receives moderate annual rainfall of 750-850 mm, but distribution is highly
uneven - the eastern hilly talukas (Bhiloda, Meghraj) receive more rain than
the western plains (Bayad, Modasa). The average maximum temperature ranges from
28°C in January to 43°C in May, while minimum temperatures range from 8°C in
winter to 26°C in summer. Relative humidity varies from 25% in summer to 75%
during the monsoon. The hills create local microclimates with higher rainfall
and cooler temperatures.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The district has diverse soil types:
• Sandy loam to loamy sand in western plains (Modasa, Bayad)
• Lateritic and red soils in hilly eastern talukas (Bhiloda, Meghraj, Dhansura)
• Medium black soil in river valleys and lowlands
• Shallow rocky soils on hill slopes and undulating terrain

Soil texture varies from sandy in the west to clay-loam in valleys. Soil pH
ranges from 6.5 to 8.0, with lateritic soils being slightly acidic and plain
soils mildly alkaline. Fertility status is low to moderate in hilly areas due
to soil erosion and leaching, while plains have moderate fertility. Organic
carbon is low (0.2-0.4%) in hilly soils. Zinc and boron deficiency is common.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 750-850 mm (uneven distribution; hilly areas get more)
Irrigation sources:
• Hathmati and Meshwa rivers and their tributaries
• Medium and minor irrigation projects (check dams, percolation tanks)
• Tubewells in western plains (Modasa, Bayad); depths 50-120 metres
• Farm ponds and traditional water harvesting structures (katas, bori bands)
• Narmada canal has limited reach into the district

Water issues: The eastern hilly talukas are largely rainfed with limited
irrigation. Water scarcity is acute in summer. Soil erosion in hills reduces
water retention. The western plains have better groundwater but face moderate
depletion. Watershed development is a critical need.

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October):
• Maize - 40,000 ha, varieties: Ganga-5, Ganga-11, GM-6, African Tall
• Bajra (Pearl millet) - 35,000 ha, varieties: GHB-558, GHB-732
• Paddy (Rice) - 15,000 ha in valleys and lowlands, varieties: GR-11, GR-12
• Tur (Pigeon pea) - 12,000 ha, varieties: GTP-82, GT-101
• Cotton - 20,000 ha in plains, varieties: G Cot-13, G Cot-21

RABI (November-March):
• Wheat - 25,000 ha (irrigated plains), varieties: GW-366, GW-496, Lok-1
• Gram (Chickpea) - 18,000 ha, varieties: GG-5, GG-7
• Mustard - 8,000 ha
• Fodder crops - Jowar, Lucerne for dairy

SUMMER (March-May):
• Vegetables - limited irrigated pockets
• Fodder - Jowar, Maize for green fodder

Productivity: Maize 15-20 quintals/ha (rainfed); Wheat 4.0-5.5 tonnes/ha
(irrigated); Bajra 12-15 quintals/ha; Cotton 2.0-3.0 quintals/ha lint.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Mango: Local and Kesar varieties in home gardens and orchards
• Vegetables: Tomato, brinjal, okra, chilli in small plots near villages
• Fruits: Guava, papaya, custard apple (seetaphal) in tribal areas
• Spices: Turmeric and ginger in limited hilly areas with good rainfall
• Forest produce: Mahua flowers, tendu leaves, medicinal plants from forests

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous breeds (Gir, local) and some crossbred cattle
• Buffalo: Local buffalo breeds for milk and draught
• Goats and Sheep: Large population in tribal hilly areas; important for
  livelihoods of Bhil and Rathwa communities
• Poultry: Backyard poultry is common in tribal villages
• Fodder availability: Limited in rainfed areas; farmers depend on forest
  grazing and crop residues. Fodder shortage is severe in summer months.

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Maize: Stem borer, Fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda), Leaf blight
• Bajra: Downy mildew (Green ear), Ergot, Smut, Shoot fly
• Paddy: Stem borer, Brown plant hopper, Leaf folder, Blast
• Tur: Pod borer (Helicoverpa armigera), Wilt (Fusarium), Phytophthora blight
• Cotton: Pink bollworm, Aphids, Whitefly
• Wheat: Rust, Karnal bunt, Aphids

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Predominantly rainfed agriculture in hilly talukas (60-70% area)
• Soil erosion on hill slopes and deforestation
• Low productivity due to traditional farming practices in tribal areas
• Limited market access and infrastructure in remote villages
• Water scarcity in summer and drought-prone years
• Shallow and rocky soils limiting crop choices in hilly areas
• Poor road connectivity affecting agricultural marketing

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Arvalli (or Sabarkantha KVK serving the area)
• ATMA: Tribal area programmes with special focus on watershed and
  sustainable agriculture
• Watershed Development: IWMP (Integrated Watershed Management Programme)
  active in hilly talukas
• Soil Testing: Mobile soil testing vans visit periodically
• APMCs: Modasa, Bhiloda, Bayad, Malpur
• Tribal Development: ITDP (Integrated Tribal Development Programme)
  schemes for input support and market linkage
• Forest Department: Joint Forest Management (JFM) for sustainable NTFP
  collection and agroforestry

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Hilly area farmers should adopt contour farming, bunding, and terracing to
  reduce soil erosion and conserve water
• Grow maize and tur as intercropping to improve land use efficiency and
  reduce risk from mono-cropping
• Plant agroforestry trees (Neem, Mahua, Amla) on field boundaries to provide
  shade, fodder, and additional income
• Use improved seed varieties of maize and bajra for higher yields; treat
  seeds with thiram + carbendazim before sowing
• In irrigated plains, practice crop rotation of cotton-wheat or maize-wheat
  to maintain soil health
• Collect and compost crop residues to improve soil organic matter rather
  than burning
• Harvest rainwater through farm ponds and check dams; the government provides
  subsidies for water harvesting structures under Sujalam Sufalam
• Use FYM (5-10 tonnes/ha) and vermicompost to improve soil fertility in
  low-fertility hill soils
• Practice mixed farming (crops + livestock) for better risk management and
  household income stability
