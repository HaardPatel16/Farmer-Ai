================================================================================
FARMER SAHAYAK - DANG DISTRICT AGRO-CLIMATIC PROFILE
================================================================================

1. DISTRICT OVERVIEW
--------------------
Dang is the smallest and most forested district in Gujarat, located in the
extreme southeastern part of the state on the Maharashtra border. It is the
only district in Gujarat that lies entirely in the Western Ghats. The district
covers approximately 1,750 sq km with a population of about 230,000, of which
over 90% belongs to tribal communities (mainly Bhil, Kokna, Varli). It comprises
3 talukas: Ahwa, Waghai, and Subir. The district is entirely hilly with dense
forests, waterfalls, and rich biodiversity. Agriculture is predominantly
subsistence-based and traditional.

2. AGRO-CLIMATIC ZONE
---------------------
Dang falls in the South Gujarat Agro-Climatic Zone (hilly/forest belt). The
climate is tropical humid with heavy monsoon rainfall. The district receives very
high annual rainfall of 2200-2500 mm, the highest in Gujarat. The monsoon is
prolonged from June to October. The average maximum temperature ranges from 26°C
in January to 34°C in May, while minimum temperatures range from 12°C in winter
to 22°C in summer. Relative humidity is very high (85-95%) throughout the year,
with the lowest in March-April (60-70%). The hills create a cool, moist climate
unique in Gujarat.

3. SOIL TYPES
-------------
The district has limited soil development due to steep slopes and dense forest cover:
• Lateritic soils in hill areas (50% of district)
• Red and yellow soils in mid-slopes and foothills
• Alluvial soils in narrow river valleys (limited area)
• Forest litter and organic soils under dense canopy

Soil texture is predominantly sandy loam to loam. Soil pH ranges from 5.5 to 6.5,
slightly acidic due to high rainfall and leaching. Fertility status is low due
to heavy leaching of nutrients, but organic matter content is high (2-4%) under
forest cover. Nitrogen and phosphorus are deficient; potassium is moderate.

4. WATER RESOURCES
------------------
Annual rainfall: 2200-2500 mm (very high, well-distributed, June-October)
Irrigation sources:
• Rivers and streams: The Dang has numerous perennial streams and rivers
  (Purna, Ambika, Khapri) fed by heavy rainfall
• Traditional water harvesting: Small farm ponds and weirs on hill streams
• Springs and natural water sources: Common in the hills
• Lift irrigation: Limited schemes on some streams

Water issues: Despite very high rainfall, irrigation is limited because:
• Steep terrain makes canal irrigation impossible
• Rivers are fast-flowing and seasonal for irrigation
• Most agriculture is rainfed with a single cropping season
• Water scarcity is NOT an issue, but water management for irrigation is difficult

5. MAJOR CROPS
--------------
KHARIF (June-October/November):
• Paddy (Rice) - 15,000 ha, traditional varieties and some improved varieties
• Nagli (Finger millet/Ragi) - 10,000 ha, traditional tribal staple crop
• Pigeon pea (Tur) - 5,000 ha, traditional varieties
• Black gram (Urad) - 3,000 ha, traditional varieties
• Vegetables: Colocasia, Yam, Cucurbits, Beans, Chilli

RABI (November-March):
• Limited Rabi cultivation due to lack of irrigation and cool temperatures
• Some finger millet and vegetables in low-lying areas

SUMMER (March-May):
• Very limited cultivation; some vegetables near water sources

Productivity: Paddy 10-15 quintals/ha (traditional, low-input); Nagli 8-12 quintals/ha;
The district has very low productivity by modern standards due to traditional
farming practices, but organic and traditional farming is valued.

6. HORTICULTURE
---------------
• Mango: Local varieties and some Kesar in lower areas; mango is an important
  forest and garden tree
• Chikoo (Sapota): Grown in lower foothills and home gardens
• Banana: Limited cultivation in valleys
• Vegetables: Colocasia, Yam, Pumpkin, Cucumber, Beans, Chilli, Ginger, Turmeric
• Forest produce: Bamboo, Mahua flowers, Tendu leaves, Kokum, Cashew, Amla,
  Medicinal plants (Guduchi, Ashwagandha, Rauwolfia)
• The district is famous for its organic and wild produce

7. LIVESTOCK
------------
• Cattle: Indigenous hill breeds, small and hardy; used for draught and milk
• Buffalo: Limited number; local breeds
• Goats and Sheep: Important for tribal livelihoods; reared in forest areas
• Pigs: Reared by some tribal communities
• Poultry: Backyard poultry is common
• Fodder availability: Good during monsoon; forest grazing is common. Fodder
  shortage in summer is managed by stall-feeding with tree leaves and crop residues.

8. CROP CALENDAR
----------------
Month          | Major Activities
---------------|-----------------------------------------------
June           | Paddy sowing (direct seeding and transplanting), Nagli sowing
July           | Paddy/Nagli sowing continues, Tur sowing
August         | Vegetables sowing, Weeding in paddy
September      | Monsoon crop maintenance, Weeding
October        | Early paddy harvest, Nagli harvest
November       | Main paddy harvest, Nagli harvest
December       | Limited Rabi vegetables in valleys
January        | Vegetable maintenance
February       | Vegetable harvest
March          | Land preparation, Some summer vegetables
April-May      | Forest produce collection, Land preparation for monsoon

9. COMMON PESTS & DISEASES
--------------------------
• Paddy: Stem borer, Gall midge, Brown plant hopper (BPH), Leaf folder, Blast,
  Sheath blight
• Nagli: Blast, Neck blast, Stem borer, Aphids
• Tur: Pod borer (Helicoverpa), Wilt (Fusarium)
• Vegetables: Fruit borer, Aphids, Leaf-eating caterpillars

Note: Pest pressure is generally lower in Dang due to heavy rainfall and rich
biodiversity (natural enemies), but fungal diseases are common due to high humidity.

10. KEY CHALLENGES
------------------
• Entirely rainfed agriculture with no major irrigation
• Steep terrain limiting mechanization and modern farming
• Heavy soil erosion during monsoon on hill slopes
• Leaching of nutrients due to very high rainfall
• Limited road connectivity and market access
• Low productivity due to traditional farming practices
• Limited access to improved seeds and inputs in remote villages
• Wildlife damage (monkeys, wild boar, elephants) to crops
• Conservation of forest while meeting livelihood needs
• Limited value addition and processing facilities for forest produce

11. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT
----------------------
• KVK: Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Ahwa (affiliated with Navsari Agricultural University)
  - provides tribal-specific training on sustainable hill agriculture
• ATMA: Tribal area programmes focusing on organic and sustainable farming
• Forest Department: Joint Forest Management (JFM) for sustainable NTFP
  collection and agroforestry
• Tribal Development: ITDP schemes for input support, market linkage, and
  infrastructure development
• Vanbandhu Kalyan Yojana: Special scheme for tribal welfare and livelihood
  improvement
• Watershed Development: IWMP for soil and water conservation in watersheds
• Organic Farming: Promotional schemes for organic certification and market
  linkage for Dang's organic produce

12. FARMER ADVISORIES
---------------------
• Adopt traditional terraced farming on hill slopes to reduce soil erosion and
  conserve water
• Maintain forest cover and agroforestry systems (areca nut, bamboo, fruit trees)
  for soil conservation and additional income
• Use traditional organic manures (compost, leaf litter, cow dung) to maintain
  soil fertility in leached soils
• Grow paddy and nagli as intercrops or in rotation to reduce risk
• Collect and compost all crop residues and forest litter for soil improvement
• Practice contour bunding and drainage channels to manage excess monsoon water
  and prevent soil erosion
• Harvest rainwater in small farm ponds and weirs for limited supplementary
  irrigation of vegetables
• Use local and traditional seed varieties adapted to local conditions; preserve
  seed diversity through community seed banks
• Form tribal cooperatives and FPOs for collective marketing of organic produce,
  bamboo, and forest products
• Avoid slash-and-burn (jhum) cultivation; adopt settled agriculture with
  agroforestry
• Apply rock phosphate and lime to address phosphorus deficiency and acidity in
  lateritic soils
• Practice mixed farming (crops + livestock + forest produce) for household
  food security and income stability
