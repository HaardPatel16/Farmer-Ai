MICRO-IRRIGATION BEST PRACTICES FOR GUJARAT FARMERS

1. INTRODUCTION
Micro-irrigation (drip and sprinkler) has transformed Gujarat agriculture. The state leads India in micro-irrigation adoption, with over 15 lakh hectares covered. Under the "Per Drop More Crop" component of PMKSY, Gujarat farmers receive subsidies of 50-70% for drip and sprinkler systems. However, many systems fail prematurely due to poor design, clogging, or maintenance neglect. This guide covers best practices for successful micro-irrigation in Gujarat's conditions.

2. DRIP IRRIGATION SYSTEMS

2.1 Components of a Drip System
- Head assembly: Control valve, filter (screen or disc type), fertilizer injector (venturi), pressure gauge, and air release valve
- Main line: PVC or HDPE pipe (75-110mm diameter) carrying water from the source to the field
- Sub-main: Smaller pipe (50-63mm) distributing water to lateral lines
- Laterals: Drip tubes (12-20mm diameter) laid along crop rows; emitters are spaced at plant-to-plant distance
- Emitters: Online (point source) or inline (labyrinth path) emitters with discharge rates of 2-8 liters per hour

2.2 Design Considerations for Gujarat
- Water source: Borewell water often has high TDS and iron; use disc filters (120-130 mesh) rather than screen filters, and consider sand media filters for very poor water
- Pressure: Drip systems operate at 1.0-2.5 kg/cm2. If the borewell pump delivers higher pressure, install a pressure regulator at the head.
- Emitter spacing: For cotton and groundnut (common in Gujarat), use 60cm x 60cm or 90cm x 60cm spacing with inline laterals at 60-90cm row spacing
- For orchards (mango, guava, citrus): Use one or two online emitters per tree, 4-8 liters/hour each, placed at the canopy drip line
- For vegetables (tomato, brinjal, chilli): Use closely spaced inline drip (20-30cm emitter spacing) or thin-walled drip tape

2.3 Drip Installation Best Practices
- Lay laterals along the crop row, not across slopes, to maintain uniform pressure
- In undulating fields, install pressure-compensating emitters to ensure equal discharge at all elevations
- Flush laterals before connecting emitters to remove manufacturing debris
- Install air release valves at high points to prevent vacuum damage and airlocks
- Use a bypass assembly for the fertilizer injector so the main line can operate without fertigation when needed
- Cover emitters with soil or mulch to prevent UV damage and algae growth
- For Gujarat's high temperatures, use thicker-walled laterals (0.4-0.6mm) rather than thin-walled tape, which degrades faster in heat

3. SPRINKLER IRRIGATION SYSTEMS

3.1 Types of Sprinklers for Gujarat
- Overhead impact sprinklers: Traditional brass or plastic impact sprinklers for field crops (cotton, groundnut, wheat)
- Mini-sprinklers: For orchards, vineyards, and vegetables; 40-100 liters per hour; wetting diameter 3-6 meters
- Rain guns (high-volume sprinklers): For large fields and commercial farms; 200-500 liters per hour; require higher pressure (3-5 kg/cm2)
- Micro-sprinklers: For orchards and plantations; apply water directly to the root zone with less evaporation loss

3.2 Design and Installation
- Spacing: Overhead sprinklers at 12m x 12m or 12m x 18m for medium-pressure systems; overlap should be 65% for uniform coverage
- Operating pressure: 2.0-3.5 kg/cm2 for most sprinklers; rain guns need 4.0-5.5 kg/cm2
- Sprinkler height: 0.5-1.0m above the crop canopy to minimize evaporation and wind drift
- In windy areas (coastal Gujarat and Kutch), use low-angle nozzles and closer spacing to reduce wind drift
- For row crops, install sprinklers in a triangular pattern rather than square for better uniformity

4. CLOGGING PREVENTION AND MAINTENANCE

4.1 Types of Clogging
- Physical clogging: Sand, silt, and debris from borewells or open water sources
- Chemical clogging: Calcium carbonate precipitation (common in Gujarat's hard water), iron oxide, and manganese deposits
- Biological clogging: Bacterial slime, algae growth in open systems, and root intrusion into emitters

4.2 Prevention Strategies
- Filtration: Always install the correct filter. For borewell water with sand, use sand media filters followed by screen/disc filters. For canal water, use sand media + disc filters. Clean filters daily or as indicated by pressure differential.
- Acid treatment: If water has high calcium hardness (common in Saurashtra and North Gujarat), inject dilute hydrochloric acid or N-pHuric acid through the venturi during flushing to dissolve carbonate deposits. Test water pH after injection to ensure it stays above 4.0.
- Chlorination: For biological clogging, inject chlorine (sodium hypochlorite or calcium hypochlorite) at 10-20 ppm for 30 minutes every 2-4 weeks during the irrigation season. This kills algae and bacterial slime.
- Flushing: Flush the entire system (open all lateral ends) at least once every 2 weeks during the irrigation season. Flush for 5-10 minutes until water runs clear. In Gujarat's dusty conditions, flush weekly during peak summer.
- Root barriers: For subsurface drip systems, apply a root-inhibiting herbicide (trifluralin) or use copper-impregnated emitters to prevent root intrusion

4.3 Maintenance Schedule
- Daily: Check filter pressure differential; clean if it exceeds 0.5 kg/cm2. Inspect the head assembly for leaks.
- Weekly: Flush all laterals, check for broken or clogged emitters, inspect main and sub-main lines for leaks
- Monthly: Check system pressure at multiple points, test water quality (EC and pH), clean fertilizer injector, check air release valves
- End of season: Drain all lines, flush thoroughly, remove and store plastic laterals if possible, inspect and service all valves and filters
- Before next season: Pressure-test the system, replace damaged emitters, recalibrate fertilizer injector, check pump performance

5. FERTIGATION (FERTILIZER APPLICATION THROUGH IRRIGATION)

5.1 Advantages of Fertigation
- Fertilizer use efficiency increases from 30-40% (soil application) to 80-90% (fertigation)
- Uniform application across the field; no plant-to-plant variation
- Can apply small, frequent doses matching crop demand precisely
- Reduces labor costs and fertilizer wastage
- Common in Gujarat for cotton, banana, sugarcane, and horticultural crops

5.2 Fertilizer Selection for Fertigation
- Urea: Highly soluble; suitable for all drip systems; apply at 1-2% solution concentration
- DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate): Good source of N and P; dissolve in warm water before injection
- MOP (Muriate of Potash): Soluble; suitable for fertigation; apply at 1-2% solution
- Calcium nitrate, potassium nitrate: Excellent for fertigation but more expensive
- Water-soluble NPK fertilizers (19-19-19, 20-20-20): Pre-formulated blends for fertigation
- Micronutrient chelates (EDTA forms): Soluble and stable in irrigation water; apply as per soil test recommendations
- Avoid: Ordinary single super phosphate (not water-soluble), calcium ammonium nitrate (can precipitate in hard water), and any fertilizer containing insoluble fillers

5.3 Fertigation Schedule Examples
- Cotton (Gujarat): 30% of N and K through fertigation at weekly intervals from flowering to boll development; 20% of P at planting through fertigation
- Banana: Daily fertigation with 1-2 g N, 0.5-1 g P, and 2-3 g K per plant per day after 4 months of planting
- Tomato: Weekly fertigation with 2-3 kg urea, 1-2 kg MOP, and 1 kg 19-19-19 per 1000 sqm during fruiting stage

5.4 Fertigation Equipment
- Venturi injector: Simple, no moving parts, operates on pressure differential; suitable for small farms; cost Rs. 2,000-5,000
- Fertilizer tank (bypass type): Tank connected to the main line via a bypass; water flows through the tank dissolving fertilizer; simple but less precise; cost Rs. 3,000-8,000
- Dosing pump: Electric or hydraulic pump that injects precise amounts; suitable for large commercial farms; cost Rs. 15,000-50,000

6. WATER USE EFFICIENCY AND CROP PERFORMANCE

6.1 Water Savings Compared to Flood Irrigation
- Drip irrigation: Saves 40-60% water compared to flood irrigation
- Sprinkler irrigation: Saves 30-50% water compared to flood irrigation
- Drip + mulching: Saves 60-70% water; the combination is highly recommended for Gujarat's water-scarce regions

6.2 Yield Improvements in Gujarat
- Cotton: 15-25% yield increase with drip irrigation due to better water and nutrient management
- Groundnut: 20-30% yield increase; especially effective in Saurashtra where water is limiting
- Banana: 30-50% yield increase with drip fertigation; almost universal in Gujarat's banana belt
- Vegetables: 25-40% yield increase with drip + fertigation
- Sugarcane: 20-30% yield increase with drip; also improves sugar content

7. SUBSIDIES AND FINANCIAL SUPPORT IN GUJARAT

7.1 PMKSY (Per Drop More Crop) Subsidy
- Small and marginal farmers: 70% subsidy (55% central + 15% state)
- Other farmers: 50% subsidy (35% central + 15% state)
- SC/ST farmers: Additional 10% state subsidy in some districts
- Maximum area: 5 hectares per farmer; can be extended for FPOs and cooperatives
- Application: Through the Gujarat Green Revolution Company (GGRC) or district agriculture office
- Required documents: 7/12 extract, Aadhaar, bank passbook, caste certificate if applicable, and water source proof

7.2 GGRC (Gujarat Green Revolution Company)
GGRC is the nodal agency for micro-irrigation in Gujarat. They empanel system manufacturers, process applications, and conduct field inspections. Contact GGRC at their district offices or through their website for application forms and empanelled vendor lists.

7.3 Cost Estimates (After Subsidy, 2024 rates)
- Drip for row crops (cotton/groundnut): Rs. 25,000-35,000 per hectare (after 50% subsidy)
- Drip for orchard crops: Rs. 40,000-60,000 per hectare (after 50% subsidy)
- Sprinkler for field crops: Rs. 15,000-25,000 per hectare (after 50% subsidy)
- Mini-sprinkler for vegetables: Rs. 30,000-45,000 per hectare (after 50% subsidy)

8. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
- Oversizing the pump: A high-discharge pump with a micro-irrigation system causes excessive pressure, pipe bursts, and emitter damage. Match the pump to the system design.
- Neglecting filtration: The biggest cause of system failure in Gujarat is inadequate filtration. Invest in good filters and maintain them.
- Poor system layout: Laying laterals across slopes causes uneven water distribution; always follow contour lines.
- Using the wrong fertilizers: Insoluble fertilizers clog emitters permanently. Only use water-soluble or specially formulated fertigation-grade fertilizers.
- Ignoring maintenance: A micro-irrigation system requires regular maintenance. The farmer who saves 50% on water but spends 20% more on maintenance still comes out far ahead.
- Not training laborers: Farm workers should know how to clean filters, flush lines, and recognize clogging symptoms.
