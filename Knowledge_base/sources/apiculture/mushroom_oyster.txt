OYSTER MUSHROOM CULTIVATION IN GUJARAT
========================================

OVERVIEW
Oyster mushroom (Pleurotus ostreatus and P. florida) is the most viable mushroom for 
Gujarat farmers. It grows on agricultural waste (paddy straw, sugarcane bagasse, 
cotton stalks), requires minimal space, and produces income within 3-4 weeks of 
inoculation. It is ideal as a year-round off-farm income activity, especially for 
women farmers and tribal communities.

WHY OYSTER MUSHROOM FOR GUJARAT
- Grows on locally abundant substrates: rice straw, wheat straw, sugarcane bagasse
- No need for soil; can be cultivated in a room, shed, or low-cost poly-house
- First harvest in 20-25 days after inoculation
- Biological efficiency: 80-120% (80-120 kg mushroom per 100 kg dry substrate)
- Vegetarian protein source (25-30% protein on dry weight basis)
- Growing urban demand in Ahmedabad, Surat, Vadodara, Rajkot

SUITABLE GROWING ENVIRONMENT
- Temperature: 20-28°C (ideal for Pleurotus florida and P. ostreatus)
- Humidity: 80-90% (critical for fruiting)
- Light: indirect diffuse light (no direct sunlight needed)
- Ventilation: good airflow to remove CO₂
- Gujarat climate: October to March ideal; summer needs cooling (evaporative cooler)

SUBSTRATE PREPARATION — PADDY STRAW METHOD

Materials Needed
- Dry paddy straw (or wheat straw): 100 kg per batch
- Oyster mushroom spawn (P. florida or P. ostreatus): 5 kg
- Polythene bags (50 × 75 cm, 100 micron thickness)
- Water
- Drum or tank for pasteurisation

Step 1: Substrate Preparation
- Cut straw into 3-5 cm pieces
- Soak in clean water for 12-16 hours
- Drain excess water; straw should have 60-65% moisture
  (Squeeze test: a few drops come out but straw does not drip freely)

Step 2: Pasteurisation (to kill competing moulds)
Method A — Hot Water Treatment:
  Heat water to 80°C; submerge straw for 1 hour; drain and cool to room temperature
Method B — Chemical Treatment:
  Soak in 2% lime (calcium hydroxide) solution for 12 hours; drain and wash

Step 3: Bag Filling and Spawning
- Spread a 5 cm layer of straw in polybag
- Add spawn (10% by weight of dry straw) as a layer
- Repeat: straw layer → spawn layer (3-4 layers)
- Seal bag with cotton plug (allows gas exchange)
- Make 6-8 holes (1 cm diameter) around the bag for primordia formation

Step 4: Incubation (Spawn Run)
- Place bags in dark room at 25-28°C
- Mycelium colonises substrate in 15-20 days (bag turns white/grey)
- Maintain humidity by spraying floor with water

Step 5: Fruiting
- Remove bags to fruiting room when fully colonised
- Increase humidity to 85-90% by spraying bags twice daily
- Maintain temperature at 22-26°C
- Provide indirect light for 8-10 hours
- Primordia (pin heads) appear in 3-5 days; mushrooms mature in 3-5 more days

HARVEST AND POST-HARVEST
- Harvest when cap edges curl under (before spores drop)
- Twist and pull gently; do not use knife (causes contamination)
- Each bag gives 3-4 flushes (crops) over 45-60 days total
- Clean bag surface between flushes; remove dead mycelium
- Fresh mushroom shelf life: 2-3 days at room temperature; 7-10 days refrigerated
- Dry in shade or dehydrator at 40°C to 8-10% moisture for longer shelf life
- Dried mushroom commands ₹600-1,200/kg vs ₹80-150/kg fresh

PRODUCTION CYCLE ECONOMICS
Per 100 kg dry straw batch:
- Substrate + spawn cost: ₹800-1,200
- Expected yield: 80-100 kg fresh mushroom over 3 flushes
- Sale at ₹100-150/kg fresh: ₹8,000-15,000
- Net profit per batch: ₹6,000-13,000 (15-20 days to first harvest)

Monthly Scale (100 bags):
- Investment: ₹5,000-8,000
- Income: ₹25,000-50,000/month
- Good side income for women SHGs; multiple units run simultaneously

MARKETING IN GUJARAT
- Sell fresh in local vegetable market; high demand in urban areas
- Supply to restaurants and hotels (consistent quality premium)
- Dried mushroom: health stores, export buyers in Surat and Ahmedabad
- Powder/value addition: mushroom powder for soups and nutraceuticals
- Ahmedabad wholesale vegetable market: daily demand for fresh oyster mushroom

COMMON PROBLEMS AND SOLUTIONS
- Green mould (Trichoderma): improper pasteurisation; reduce substrate moisture
- Dry stroma (no pinning): increase humidity; remove CO₂ buildup
- Abnormal mushrooms: poor ventilation; increase fresh air exchange
- Slow colonisation: low temperature or poor spawn quality; check spawn freshness
